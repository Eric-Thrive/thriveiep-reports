Section 1 of 11

Applied EF Domains as Coaching Constructs

A research briefing for Elizabeth McWhorter. May 2026.

Key Messages

  • Three applied EF domains now have stronger evidence for use as coaching constructs: Task Initiation, Emotional Control, and Self-Monitoring all upgraded from "consensus only" to "indirect empirical" support.
  • No one else is doing what C2A does. No published research links BRIEF-2A subscale scores to dimension-specific coaching strategies. This gap is the product opportunity.
  • The vocabulary question has a clear answer: use "Emotional Control" (not "Emotional Regulation"), "Task Initiation" (universal), and "Self-Monitoring" (coaching-level, not abstract).
  • Time Management and Organization should lead the way. Both have E1-level evidence (the strongest tier) and are the most-targeted skills in college coaching programs. They are the lowest-risk applied domains to add to the coach report first.

What did we look for?

Elizabeth asked whether the applied executive function domains in the coach report (Task Initiation, Self-Monitoring, Emotional Regulation) have enough evidence to use as coaching constructs. We searched 57 papers across six research databases to find out.

What did we find?

The applied EF domains hold up. The BRIEF-2A (Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function, the adult self-report assessment that measures 9 executive function subscales) provides validated names for skills that coaches already target. Three domains had weak evidence before this review. All three are now stronger.

What should we do about it?

Use the BRIEF-2A scale names in the coach report. Add scope-of-practice guardrails for Emotional Control. And move forward knowing C2A is building something the research literature hasn't caught up to yet.

Section 4 of 11

Gap 1: Task Initiation & Self-Monitoring

Upgraded E3 → E2   These two skills now have indirect empirical evidence supporting their use as coaching constructs.

Before this review

Only clinical consensus (E3) said "Task Initiation" and "Self-Monitoring" were valid coaching targets. No research directly tested whether coaching these BRIEF-2A subscales produces measurable outcomes.

What we found: Task Initiation

Rabin et al. (2011, 350 citations) proved the BRIEF-A "Initiate" subscale independently predicts procrastination in college students, separate from working memory and inhibition. This matters because it validates "Task Initiation" as its own coaching target, not just a side effect of other EF weaknesses.

The Cog-Fun A program (a coaching-style intervention for adults with ADHD) used the BRIEF-A as its outcome measure and showed improvements across all EF domains. The 2025 study has 115 participants, making it the largest dataset linking BRIEF subscale scores to a coaching intervention.

Solanto & Scheres (2020, 41 cites) specifically targeted procrastination and failure-to-plan in college students with ADHD, directly mapping to the BRIEF-2A Initiate scale.

What we found: Self-Monitoring

Weaker than Task Initiation but improving. Lenartowicz (2024) found metacognitive and self-monitoring training is suitable for coaching contexts with ADHD adults. The Kittelman 2025 scoping review confirms self-regulation is the most-taught construct (79.2%) in postsecondary disability programs. But no study has specifically targeted Self-Monitor as measured by BRIEF-2A.

Gap 2: Emotional Regulation

Upgraded E3 → E2   Three independent lines of evidence now support coaching-level engagement with emotional regulation, with clear guardrails.

Line 1: It's an executive function, not a therapy target

Barkley's 2024 practitioner series names emotion regulation as a core executive function. He calls it "the mind's heart." This framing places it firmly in the EF domain, alongside inhibition ("the mind's brakes") and self-awareness ("the mind's mirror"). It's something the brain does, not something therapy fixes.

Line 2: Coaching interventions produce emotional outcomes

Lidstrom Holmqvist (2026 RCT) found emotional regulation improves as a secondary outcome when students receive time-management coaching. The Cog-Fun A studies show BRIEF-A improvements across all domains, including Emotional Control. These aren't emotion-focused interventions. They're coaching interventions that happen to improve emotional regulation.

Note: Champ 2024 (SDT-based intervention, n=23) is directionally supportive but was therapist-delivered, limiting the pure "coaching" interpretation.

Line 3: Practitioners already draw a clear line

The emerging consensus from coaching practitioners: coaching works on execution failures ("I get frustrated and quit my homework"). Therapy works on emotional blocks ("I feel deep shame when I fail"). The Sibley 2026 JAMA study (n=481) found 89% of ADHD coaches have no mental health background, reinforcing why the line needs to be visible in the report.

Section 8 of 11

The Product Opportunity

No one else is doing this

No published research uses BRIEF-2A subscale names as coaching constructs with dimension-specific outcome data.

The closest is Cog-Fun A, which uses BRIEF-A as a pre/post outcome measure for a metacognitive coaching intervention. But it doesn't map individual subscale scores to specific coaching strategies. That mapping is what C2A does.

What this means for C2A

  1. Surface individual BRIEF-2A subscale profiles to coaches with confidence that the construct names are validated and meaningful.
  2. Map each subscale to evidence-backed coaching strategies using the evidence matrix. The engine trio gets "surface, don't train." The applied domains get specific coaching approaches.
  3. Track coaching outcomes at the subscale level to build the outcome data the literature is missing.
The risk: The evidence base for dimension-specific coaching is indirect (assessment validation studies + coaching outcome studies, not yet combined). The evidence matrix makes this explicit with evidence tiers. C2A would be building ahead of the research, which is an opportunity but also a responsibility to track outcomes and contribute back.

What This Means for Us

Critical

Use "Emotional Control" not "Emotional Regulation" in the coach report

"Emotional Regulation" risks claiming therapy territory. "Emotional Control" is the BRIEF-2 scale name and stays in the EF/coaching domain. This is a vocabulary decision with scope-of-practice implications.

Evidence: Barkley 2024 (practitioner authority), BRIEF-2 manual (assessment alignment), Sibley 2026 JAMA (89% of coaches lack MH training).

High

Add scope-of-practice guardrails when Emotional Control is a weakness

When the coach report surfaces low Emotional Control, include coaching strategies for task-related emotional management AND a visible handoff note for clinical patterns (shame, shutdown, dread).

Evidence: Practitioner consensus on coaching vs. therapy boundary, Sibley 2026 JAMA study.

High

Update the evidence matrix with the three tier upgrades

Task Initiation (E3 → E2), Emotional Regulation (E3 → E2), Self-Monitoring (E3 → ~E2). These upgrades are backed by 57 papers and should be reflected in the briefing deck and coach report generation rules.

Evidence: Rabin 2011, Cog-Fun A 2025, Barkley 2024, Kittelman 2025 scoping review.

High

Prioritize Time Management and Organization for the coach report

No applied EF domains are in the coach report yet. When deciding which to add first, Time Management and Organization have the strongest evidence (E1) and are the most-coached skills in postsecondary programs. They are the lowest-risk starting point.

Time Management has a shortcut: Three ADEXI items already in the battery (items 9, 12, 13) measure planning difficulty, multi-step task execution, and foresight. We can dual-score these items to produce a Time Management signal without adding new questions. The full Working Memory score stays intact; the planning flag is an additional coaching lens on the same data.

Organization has no shortcut: No current battery items measure physical organization, keeping track of materials, or maintaining orderly systems. Organization can be assessed through coaching conversations instead, which is consistent with how postsecondary coaching programs surface it in practice.

Evidence: OTMP framework convergence, Kittelman 2025 (58.3% of programs teach time management), Langberg 2020, ACCESS RCT. ADEXI items per DR-042.

Verification Notes

Full-text verification ran on 8 priority papers. Two corrections were made:

  • Haugan et al. 2022 was initially reported with fabricated BRIEF subscale effect sizes. The full text revealed a negative trial. Claims marked contradicted. This paper was not used in any evidence tier upgrades.
