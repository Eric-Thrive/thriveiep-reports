Section 1 of 13

How Bloom Reports Get Built: An Architecture Overview

Key Messages

  • Bloom Reports are placement decision tools, not clinical summaries. A stack of clinical documents goes in; a concise, 1,200-word report comes out that answers one question: what does this student's next placement need to provide?
  • The pipeline never guesses. When documents contradict each other, a source hierarchy resolves what it can automatically. Anything it cannot resolve stops the pipeline and waits for a human clinician.
  • Three different AI models share the work, each picked for what it does best: fast extraction, deep synthesis, and cheap quality checks. Two stages use no AI at all, running pure rules instead.

What does the pipeline do?

It takes a stack of clinical documents (psychological evaluations, discharge summaries, IEPs, call transcripts) and runs them through 6 processing stages separated by 4 quality gates. The output is a structured Bloom Report that synthesizes everything a placement team needs to know.

Why not just hand everything to one big AI call?

Clinical data is high-stakes. A single monolithic call could mix up dates from different documents, silently pick one provider's opinion over another, or hallucinate details. The pipeline isolates each document during extraction, reconciles conflicts with explicit rules, and gives humans veto power at critical junctures.

How reliable is it?

41 automated checks across 3 layers validate every report before it ships. The system catches everything from wrong age math to missing citation evidence to sections that describe the student's history without actually informing placement decisions.

Section 2 of 13

What Is a Bloom Report?

A Bloom Report is a therapeutic placement profile. Families, educational advocates, treatment teams, and admissions directors use it to determine the Least Restrictive Environment that can safely meet a student's needs.

Think of it as a translation layer. On one side: a pile of clinical documents written by different providers at different times with different terminology. On the other side: a clear, 2-page report that any stakeholder can read and act on.

What makes it different from a clinical summary?

Every section answers a specific placement question. "Needs & Challenges" does not just list diagnoses; it answers "What must the milieu be staffed and structured to handle daily?" "Treatment History" does not just list placements; it answers "What has been tried, what happened, and why is a higher level of care now indicated?"

The Pipeline at a Glance

Documents flow through 6 stages and 4 gates. Stages do work (parse, extract, reconcile, synthesize, review). Gates check that the work meets quality thresholds before the next stage begins. If a gate fails, the pipeline halts.

S1 Parse → S2 Extract → G1 → S3a Reconcile → S3b Classify → G1b → S4 Synthesis → G2 → S6 Review → HRG → Report

Notice the numbering gap: there is no Stage 5 today. That slot is reserved for Z3 formal verification in Phase 2, which will mathematically validate reasoning traces before the quality review runs. The gap is intentional forward planning, not a bug.

Two Types of Stage

Deterministic Zero AI tokens

Stages 1 and 3a run pure rules: regex pattern matching, date arithmetic, source hierarchy lookups. They are fast, cheap, and perfectly reproducible. No AI means no hallucination risk.

Agentic LLM-powered

Stages 2, 3b, 4, and 6 call Claude models. Each stage uses a different model chosen for its strengths. Extraction needs throughput (Sonnet). Synthesis needs depth (Opus). Review needs speed (Haiku).

Section 4 of 13

Stages 1 & 2: Ingestion

These two stages correspond to Agent 1 (Document Analyst) in the Bloom Protocol. They turn raw documents into structured, machine-readable data.

S1

Stage 1: Parse & Classify Deterministic

Each uploaded document gets classified by type using regex pattern matching: psychological evaluation, IEP, discharge summary, treatment note, or call transcript. Basic metadata (dates, source names) is extracted. No AI is involved; this is pure text parsing.

S2

Stage 2: Claim Extraction Sonnet 4.6 · 16K tokens

Each document is processed independently by Claude Sonnet. The model extracts structured JSON claims: diagnoses, medications, risk indicators, strengths, family observations, timeline events. Each claim gets a confidence score (0.0 to 1.0) and source grounding (page, paragraph).

Why process each document in isolation? This prevents cross-document contamination. If a discharge summary mentions an event from a prior placement, Stage 2 extracts only what is explicitly stated in that document. It does not infer dates or details from other files. This isolation is what makes accurate reconciliation possible in Stage 3.

Stage 2 is also parallelizable: all documents can be extracted simultaneously since they do not depend on each other. For a case with 6 documents, that means 6 parallel Sonnet calls instead of 6 sequential ones.

Section 5 of 13

Gate 1: Schema Validation

Before the pipeline invests any more compute, Gate 1 runs a quick sanity check on what Stage 2 produced.

What does it check?

  • Demographics populated: At minimum, the student's name must be present.
  • Claims exist: At least one document must have yielded extracted claims. If nothing was extracted, something went wrong upstream.
  • Date/age consistency: Any computed ages must be within a ±2 year tolerance of dates in the documents. This catches obvious date parsing errors.
  • Claim ID uniqueness: Every extracted claim must have a unique identifier. Duplicates would corrupt downstream reconciliation.

Gate 1 uses Zod schema validation (a TypeScript-native runtime type checker). If any critical check fails, the pipeline halts immediately. There is no point running conflict reconciliation on malformed extraction data.

Design principle: Fail fast, fail cheap. Gate 1 burns zero AI tokens. It runs in milliseconds. Catching a bad extraction here saves the cost of an Opus synthesis call that would produce garbage output.
Section 6 of 13

Stage 3: Reconciliation & Conflict Classification

This is where the pipeline takes isolated document extractions and weaves them into a single coherent timeline. It corresponds to Agent 2 (Timeline Reconciler) in the Bloom Protocol, but splits the work into two sub-stages.

3a

Stage 3a: Reconciliation Deterministic

Fully rule-based. Selects the anchor document (the most authoritative source for placement sequence, usually a call transcript or parent interview). Builds a master chronology. Computes the student's age at each event from their date of birth. Detects placement overlaps. Flags data gaps (for example, if no psychological evaluation is present).

The Source Hierarchy

When two documents disagree, Stage 3a applies these deterministic rules:

Conflict TypeWho WinsWhy
Placement names & sequenceParent/family reportThe parent lived it; they know the order
Admission/discharge datesThat facility's own recordsA discharge summary knows its own dates
Student agesComputed from DOBNever trust a stated age; always do the math
Diagnostic conclusionsEach evaluator's own reportNote convergence or divergence; do not override
3b

Stage 3b: Conflict Classification Sonnet 4.6 · 8K tokens

Any conflicts the source hierarchy could not resolve get classified by Claude Sonnet into two categories:

Category 1: Factual Discrepancy

Contradictory dates with no clear winner. Placement sequences that do not align even after checking the parent interview. These halt the pipeline for human clinician input because getting this wrong would change the placement recommendation.

Category 2: Clinical Divergence

Two providers genuinely disagree on a clinical matter (one diagnoses ASD, another rules it out). These are not errors to resolve. They are part of the clinical picture. The pipeline passes them to the synthesis stage with instructions to present both perspectives transparently.

Clever optimization: Stage 3b short-circuits with zero tokens if Stage 3a resolved all conflicts deterministically. In clean cases (no contradictions across documents), this agentic stage costs nothing.
Section 7 of 13

Gate 1b: The Human Halt

This is the pipeline's most important safety mechanism. Gate 1b checks three things:

  • Anchor document identified: The reconciliation must have selected an authoritative source.
  • Master chronology non-empty: There must be at least one timeline event.
  • No Category 1 conflicts remaining: If any factual discrepancies could not be resolved by the source hierarchy, the pipeline stops.

What happens when it halts?

The pipeline sets a terminal status of "human_review" and surfaces concise questions to the clinician. The format is deliberately terse: what is unclear, plus one sentence on why it matters for the report. No multi-paragraph analysis. The clinician provides the answer, and the pipeline resumes at Stage 4.

The Threshold Test

Before flagging a Category 1 item, Stage 3b applies a threshold test: would resolving this discrepancy change the placement recommendation or clinical interpretation?

  • If no: note it briefly in Data Gaps and proceed. Not every date conflict matters for placement.
  • If yes (or genuinely uncertain): flag it and halt. Better to pause than to synthesize a report on a shaky foundation.
This is calibrated interruption. The system does not stop for every minor inconsistency. It stops only when getting the answer wrong could send a student to the wrong placement. Every halt costs clinician time, so the threshold is set to minimize unnecessary interruptions while never letting a consequential error through.

Stage 4: Placement Synthesis

This is the heart of the pipeline. It corresponds to Agent 3 (Clinical Synthesizer) and is the only stage that runs on Claude Opus 4.6, the most capable model in the family. This is where extracted claims become a coherent narrative.

What it produces

  • 9 report sections, each answering its guiding question
  • Reasoning traces (conditional, causal, temporal) with evidence pointers back to source claims
  • Level of Care recommendation grounded in failed interventions and safety data
  • BBI alignment rationale screening recommendations against Building Bridges Initiative pillars
  • Conflict resolutions for any remaining ambiguities, each with a resolution strategy (pick doc A, pick doc B, flag as ambiguous, or synthesize)

The Placement Relevance Filter

Before drafting, Stage 4 reviews compiled data for each section and identifies the 2-3 findings that most directly answer that section's guiding question. Details that do not inform what the placement must provide, what staff need to manage, what has already failed, or what strengths a program can leverage are omitted.

A finding can be true and well-sourced and still not belong in a 1,200-word placement tool. This filter is what keeps reports concise and actionable instead of exhaustive.

Why Opus?

Synthesis is the highest-stakes stage. It requires simultaneously: tracking a master chronology, applying clinical frameworks (F.I.D., BBI, medical necessity), preserving stakeholder voices with accurate attribution, and producing prose at an 8th-10th grade reading level. This is the task that benefits most from the deepest reasoning capability.

Gate 2: Constraint Validation

After synthesis, Gate 2 runs a battery of deterministic checks on the output before it proceeds to quality review.

CheckWhy It Matters
All 9 QC sections present with non-empty narrativesMissing sections mean incomplete placement picture
No em dashes anywhere in outputBloom writing standard: em dashes are banned (use commas, semicolons, parentheses)
All conflicts have a resolution with stage attributionNo unresolved ambiguity should reach the final report
Level of Care recommendation presentThe core deliverable; without it the report cannot inform placement
All referenced claims exist in extractionPrevents hallucinated citations to claims that were never extracted
The em dash check sounds trivial, but it is not. LLMs love em dashes. Claude will insert them naturally unless explicitly told not to. The Bloom Protocol bans them because reports are read by families, not literary critics. Catching them here is cheaper than having a human find them in review.

If any critical check fails, the pipeline halts. This is the third and final halt point before quality review.

Stage 6: Quality Review

The final processing stage corresponds to Agent 4 (Quality Reviewer). It runs on Claude Haiku 4.5, the fastest and cheapest model. It does two things:

6a: Deterministic Checks (no AI)

  • Narrative presence per section
  • Em dash detection (a second pass, catching any that slipped through)
  • Claim cross-reference validation (every cited claim exists in the extraction)
  • Demographic parity check: flags demographic descriptors in placement-relevant sections (a bias detection mechanism)

6b: LLM Coherence Check (Haiku)

Evaluates whether the synthesis output reads coherently: logical flow, transitions between sections, consistent tone. Importantly, it does not re-evaluate clinical content. Haiku is not qualified to second-guess Opus on clinical reasoning; it only checks that the narrative hangs together as readable prose.

Stage 6 computes a per-section confidence score as the fraction of checks passed. If any section scores below 0.6, or if the demographic parity check triggers, or if the coherence parse fails, the report is flagged for human review.

Why Haiku for review? Coherence checking is a lightweight task. You do not need Opus-level reasoning to detect whether paragraphs flow logically. Haiku runs in milliseconds and costs a fraction of a cent. Using Opus here would be like hiring a surgeon to take your blood pressure.

Model Tiering: The Right Tool for Each Job

The pipeline uses three different Claude models, each chosen for what it does best. This is a deliberate cost-performance tradeoff, not just an optimization.

StageModelMax TokensWhy This Model
1 ParseNone (deterministic)0Regex classification; AI adds no value here
2 ExtractSonnet 4.616KHigh throughput, parallelizable per-doc; structured extraction is Sonnet's sweet spot
3a ReconcileNone (deterministic)0Rule-based source hierarchy; date math does not need reasoning
3b ClassifySonnet 4.68KShort-circuits if clean; classification is a fast task
4 SynthesisOpus 4.616KHighest-stakes stage; needs deep clinical reasoning + narrative quality
6 ReviewHaiku 4.52KFast coherence check; does not re-evaluate clinical content

The Economics

In a typical clean case (no Category 1 conflicts, 5-6 source documents):

  • Stage 3b short-circuits: 0 tokens (all conflicts resolved deterministically)
  • Stage 2 runs in parallel: wall-clock time is one Sonnet call, not N sequential calls
  • Stage 4 is the only Opus call: the most expensive model runs exactly once
  • Stage 6 uses Haiku: the cheapest possible model for a task that does not need depth
The pipeline is designed so that the expensive model (Opus) only sees pre-validated, pre-reconciled data. Every deterministic stage and gate upstream exists to ensure Stage 4 gets clean input, reducing the chance it produces something that fails Gate 2 and wastes an Opus call.

The Evaluation Framework: 41 Checks

Beyond the inline gates, the pipeline has a separate end-to-end evaluation framework that runs 41 checks across 3 layers. This is used for regression testing, prompt tuning, and quality benchmarking.

Layer 1: Deterministic (15 checks) Automated

Binary pass/fail checks that need no AI. Does the report have all required sections (S1-S5)? Are em dashes absent and abbreviations correct (F1, F2, F9, F10)? Is a Level of Care recommendation present? Are data gaps documented?

Layer 2: Traceability (15 checks) Automated

Validates evidence chains. Can every clinical claim be traced back to a source document (C1, C4, C7)? Are inline citations present for key claims (F3)? Do concrete examples accompany behavioral descriptions (EX1-3)? Were reconciliation decisions applied correctly (RC1-2)?

Layer 3: LLM Judge (11 checks) Haiku 4.5

These checks require reading comprehension. Does each section answer its placement guiding question (P1-P8)? Is the clinical narrative accurate and not contradicted by the extractions (C5-C6)? Does the F.I.D. (Frequency, Intensity, Duration) data use concrete numbers, not vague qualifiers (F4)?

Reference Case: Emily F

The evaluation framework is baselined against a complex validated case: Emily F (6 source documents, 568 extracted claims, 5th-iteration gold-standard report).

LayerCurrent Baseline
Layer 1: DeterministicS2, F1, DG fail (3 of 15)
Layer 2: TraceabilityAll pass (15 of 15)
Layer 3: LLM JudgeNot yet baselined

When checks fail, the evaluation framework recommends interventions across 5 tiers: ground-truth audit, config tuning, prompt tuning, stage modification, or architecture change. This creates a structured improvement loop.

Section 13 of 13

What This Means for Us

Architecture Strengths

Key Insight Deterministic-first design minimizes AI risk

By handling document classification, timeline reconciliation, and source hierarchy with pure rules, the pipeline avoids AI hallucination in the areas most prone to factual error (dates, ages, placement sequences). AI is only used where human-level judgment is genuinely needed: extracting meaning from unstructured text, classifying ambiguous conflicts, synthesizing narrative, and checking coherence.

Key Insight Three halt points create a graduated safety net

Gate 1 catches data quality issues before any expensive processing. Gate 1b catches factual conflicts before synthesis. The Human Review Gate catches quality issues after synthesis. Each halt point addresses a different failure mode, and each one is positioned to minimize wasted compute.

What Comes Next

Phase 2 Stage 5: Z3 Formal Verification

Will use the Z3 theorem prover to mathematically validate reasoning traces from Stage 4. For example: if the synthesis says "interventions escalated from outpatient to residential," Z3 can verify that the timeline events actually support that claim. This adds a layer of logical proof between synthesis and review.

Phase 3 Gate 3: Conformal Prediction

Will add statistical confidence bounds to section quality scores. Instead of a binary pass/fail, the evaluation will produce calibrated probability intervals (for example, "85% confidence this section is clinically accurate with a ±5% margin"). This enables more nuanced quality routing.

Open Questions

  • How should the pipeline handle cases where Stage 2 extraction confidence is consistently low across all documents? Currently there is no aggregate confidence gate.
  • Should the demographic parity check in Stage 6 trigger a hard halt or a soft warning? The current implementation flags for human review, but the threshold is not yet calibrated.
  • What is the right baseline target for Layer 3 (LLM Judge) checks? The Emily F case has not yet been scored on placement utility (P1-P8).
