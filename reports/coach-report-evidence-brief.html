Orientation

What the coach report is for, and the two-layer model beneath it

TL;DRTwo reports, not one. The student report (already live) shows a student's level on 12 durable skills; this brief is about the coach view that explains why a skill is constrained. The whole design rests on a two-layer model: the durable skills on top, and a diagnostic "why" layer (EF, needs, motivation) beneath them, not beside them.

This brief is about the coach-facing view that complements it, giving a coach the diagnostic depth and "why" framing the student version does not surface. The three research questions

This is a research brief. Each section below is a literature synthesis first, with a short "what this could mean for the coach report" coda at the end. The three questions map the correlational web among the three node-types (EF, motivation, durable skills) and then make the one applied decision the web informs:

  • RQ1 (EF x motivation): how do all EF dimensions relate to the self-determination and motivation measures (autonomy, relatedness, competence, intrinsic motivation, amotivation)? This owns the EF-to-motivation edge only.
  • RQ3 (predictors x durable skills): how do EF and the SDT/motivation measures relate to the 12 durable skills, the reported outcome? This owns the predictor-to-outcome edge only.
  • RQ2 (the applied-EF decision): the engine already scores three of the seven applied EF and shows them to coaches; are they earning their place given they re-read the same items as durable skills already reported, and should the other four be built? Does the evidence say any of this adds real, non-redundant value? This synthesizes RQ1 and RQ3 into a recommendation.

Two ground rules that govern every number in this brief.

Published literature only. No internal pilot R-squared, delta-R-squared, or sample-size figure is cited as evidence anywhere. Internal signal is framed only as "consistent with" the published literature or "a hypothesis to test."

The part-whole caveat. The engine reuses each measured Layer 1 signal as an ingredient that rolls up into as many as five durable skills. So any internal finding that a Layer 1 signal "predicts" a durable-skill score is partly predicting a thing from its own parts. That is an upper bound, not clean evidence.

Background

Background literature review: the science under the coach report

TL;DRThe coach report sits on three sciences: self-determination theory and executive function (both mature and well-evidenced), and measurement science (the load-bearing one). The headline from measurement: self-report ratings and lab tasks are not the same thing, and any two self-reports from one person at one sitting share a "rater fingerprint." That is why Layer 1 signals belong as explanatory hypotheses, not as independent measurements stacked next to the outcome.

The short version. The coach report sits on three bodies of published science: self-determination theory (why autonomy, relatedness, and competence drive motivation), executive-function theory (the brain's air-traffic control), and the measurement science that tells us how much to trust a self-report rating. The first two are mature and well-evidenced. The third is the load-bearing one for us, because C2A measures everything by rating self-report, never by lab task. The honest headline from the measurement literature is that ratings and lab tasks are not the same thing (they correlate weakly), and that any two self-report scales filled out by the same person at the same sitting will share some overlap that is the rater's fingerprint, not a real connection. That single fact is why the coach report should treat its Layer 1 signals (EF, needs, motivation) as explanatory hypotheses about why a skill is constrained, not as independent measurements stacked next to the outcome.

This section sets up the three research questions that follow. It does not answer them. It establishes the theory, the published effect sizes, the measurement traps, and the gaps that every later section has to respect.

1. Theory: why these constructs should (or should not) relate

Self-determination theory and the three psychological "vitamins"

The metaphor for leadership: self-determination theory (SDT) says people run on three psychological vitamins, and you need all three. Autonomy (feeling the choice is yours), competence (feeling effective), and relatedness (feeling connected). Starve any one and motivation curdles. This is the foundational claim from Ryan and Deci's 2000 statement of the theory, where the three needs are framed as essential nutrients for intrinsic motivation and well-being. High

A second theoretical move matters for how C2A is built: need satisfaction and need frustration are two separate processes, not two ends of one dial. Satisfaction energizes growth; frustration awakens passivity, self-centeredness, and defensiveness. They can co-occur. This satisfaction-versus-frustration distinction (Vansteenkiste and Ryan 2013; Vansteenkiste, Ryan, and Soenens 2020) is exactly the structure the C2A six-facet needs measure rests on, where each of autonomy, relatedness, and competence is scored for both satisfaction and frustration. High as theory backed by a large review base.

The companion idea is the "bright path / dark path" dual-pathway model: autonomy-supportive environments feed need satisfaction and self-driven motivation, while controlling environments feed frustration and pressured motivation, and these two paths run in parallel rather than as one continuum (Haerens et al. 2015). Moderate, a single large cross-sectional study, well-cited.

Executive function: the brain's air-traffic control

Executive function (EF) is the system that holds a goal in mind, blocks distractions, and switches between tasks. The canonical "unity and diversity" account names three core components: working memory (holding and updating), inhibition (blocking), and cognitive flexibility / shifting (switching). These three are exactly what C2A measures with ADEXI (working memory, inhibition) and a short in-house cognitive-flexibility rating scale (the published Cognitive Flexibility Scale in the design docs is deprecated). High, broad consensus.

EF and motivation are theorized to interact, not to live in separate rooms. In a small lab study, monetary incentives improved inhibition, updating, and shifting, but emotional distractors wiped out the incentive boost on inhibition and shifting (Yeung et al. 2024, N=45). Read this only as a theory illustration that the "hot" emotional boundary and the "cool" cognitive boundary are real and porous, not as an effect size you can carry forward. Low, small, lab, young-adult sample.

Statistical detail: the Howard self-report inflation gap

The Howard et al. 2021 split is the cleanest published demonstration of self-report inflation in this exact literature. The same antecedent (intrinsic motivation) predicts objective academic performance at ρ≈.13 but self-reported performance at ρ≈.32. The outcome being self-reported roughly doubles the apparent relationship. The practical reading: when both sides of a correlation are self-reported (as they almost always are in C2A), treat the larger end of that range as the realistic ceiling, not the expected truth. Caution for citation: this is the Howard, Bureau, Guay, Chong, Ryan student-motivation meta-analysis, not the separate Howard, Slemp, and Wang need-support / need-thwarting paper.

3. Method and measurement caveats: the part that matters most for C2A

This is the load-bearing subsection, because it governs how much weight every later correlation can carry. C2A rates everything. No lab tasks. So we have to be precise about what a rating is and is not.

Rating-based vs. performance-based EF: a method gap, not a "ratings are wrong" finding

The single most important background fact: performance-based EF (lab tasks) and rating-based EF (questionnaires) correlate at only a median r ≈ .19, and just 24% of those correlations even reach significance (Toplak, West, and Stanovich 2013). High. The authors' own interpretation is that the two methods "assess different underlying mental constructs": lab tasks capture the efficiency of cognitive abilities, while ratings capture success at real-world goal pursuit.

Why this is good news, not a weakness, for a coach report. A coach does not care how many milliseconds a student needs to switch between trials on a screen. A coach cares whether the student actually manages their time, initiates tasks, and recovers from setbacks in daily life, which is precisely the side ratings capture. So C2A's all-rating design does not need an apology. It does mean one hard rule for this brief: the lab-EF literature (for example the Trail-Making-Test effect sizes seen in some coaching trials) stays strictly in background and is never used as evidence about what C2A measures, because C2A never measures EF that way.

Common-method variance: the rater's fingerprint

The metaphor: when one person answers every question on one survey at one sitting, some of the apparent "correlation" between two scales is just the shared fingerprint of that rater and that survey format, not a real connection between the two things being measured. A long-standing methodological estimate puts roughly 26% of the variance in a typical self-report measure down to method rather than the trait itself (Cote and Buckley 1987, as synthesized in Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Lee, and Podsakoff 2003). High as a well-established baseline. The practical move: treat that share as a discount factor on any C2A correlation where two self-report scales are compared, for example a self-rated EF scale against a self-rated needs scale. The Podsakoff et al. 2003 DOI (10.1037/0021-9010.88.5.879) and the Cote & Buckley 1987 source (10.2307/3151642) are now Crossref-verified; the 26% method-variance estimate originates in Cote & Buckley and is synthesized in Podsakoff et al.

Jangle and part-whole: when two names hide one thing

The "jangle fallacy": giving two things different names does not make them different things. A self-report EF scale and a conscientiousness scale can be largely the same questionnaire wearing two name tags. Published estimates put the overlap between self-report EF and the conscientiousness trait at roughly ρ ≈ .20 (Wilmot and Ones 2019; Buchanan 2016). Moderate. Both citations are now Crossref-verified (Wilmot & Ones 2019, PNAS, 10.1073/pnas.1908430116; Buchanan 2016, Psychological Assessment, 10.1037/pas0000192, whose title directly states self-report EF correlates with personality rather than performance-based EF). The exact ρ ≈ .20 should still be confirmed against the source tables before load-bearing use.

There is a second, sharper version of this problem that is specific to C2A's architecture, and the brief must keep stating it: the part-whole caveat. The scoring engine reuses each measured Layer 1 signal as an ingredient that rolls up into as many as five of the twelve durable skills. So if an internal analysis ever shows that a Layer 1 signal "predicts" a durable-skill score, part of that prediction is the signal predicting a thing built partly from itself. That is an upper bound, not clean evidence. Any internal pilot number must be framed only as "consistent with" the published literature or "a hypothesis to test," never as proof.

A possible bridge between rated EF and motivation

Because there is no published direct correlation between a standard self-report EF battery and a validated SDT autonomous-motivation scale (see Gaps below), the field leans on bridges. One candidate is the Barkley Deficits in Executive Functioning Scale (BDEFS), which contains a Self-Motivation subscale, studied by Knouse, Feldman, and Blevins 2014 across two samples (N≈250 and N≈229). Moderate. Important framing flag: the paper's registered title and headline focus are "Executive functioning difficulties as predictors of academic performance: examining the role of grade goals," not a Self-Motivation-subscale study per se. The DOI (10.1016/j.lindif.2014.07.001) resolves cleanly and the BDEFS does carry a Self-Motivation subscale, so the bridge is plausible, but verify the subscale-level results in the paper before citing this as the load-bearing EF-to-motivation bridge in RQ1.

Population limits

Much of the applied-EF and EF-coaching evidence rides on ADHD or disability samples rather than the general population. Findings from clinical samples may not transfer cleanly to a general college-and-career-transition cohort, and later sections should flag where an effect size comes from a clinical sample. This is a recurring limit, not a one-off.

4. Gaps: what is NOT published, stated plainly

  • No direct EF-to-autonomous-motivation correlation exists. There is no published study correlating a standard self-report EF battery with a validated SDT autonomous-motivation scale. The EF-by-motivation overlap is entirely triangulated: shared conscientiousness and neuroticism variance, the BDEFS Self-Motivation bridge, the procrastination bridge, and the affect bridge. RQ1 must say this plainly rather than imply a direct link exists.
  • No clean adult EF-to-achievement meta-analysis. The strongest EF-to-achievement meta-analysis (r≈.365) is children ages 6 to 12. Adult work-and-career outcomes have no equivalent; labeled adult neighbors (general mental ability, effort regulation) have to stand in, and that substitution must be named.
  • No full-chain study. Nothing in the published literature runs the whole proposed path (needs → motivation → EF use → durable-skill outcomes) in one model. Each edge is studied separately. The coach report's two-layer story is an assembly of separate evidence, not a single validated chain.
  • Applied-EF coaching value is thin outside clinical samples. The case for applied EF (RQ2), both the three Deployed and the four Candidate, leans heavily on ADHD and disability research, so general-population value is largely unproven and must be tiered Low where that is the only evidence. For the Deployed trio the sharper problem is different: item-level redundancy with durable skills the student report already shows.

What this could mean for the coach report

The method, jangle, and common-method trio is the honest backbone of the whole brief. Any EF-to-motivation or EF-to-durable-skill overlap that C2A observes is partly shared-method and partly shared-conscientiousness, so the coach report should frame Layer 1 signals as explanatory hypotheses about why a skill is constrained, not as independent measurements sitting next to the outcome. That is exactly the two-layer architecture. And the rating-versus-performance distinction (Toplak) is the reason the coach report can treat self-reported EF as legitimate "real-world goal pursuit" signal rather than apologizing for the absence of lab tasks.

RQ1

RQ1: How EF dimensions relate to self-determination and motivation measures

TL;DRThe EF-to-motivation link is real in theory but has never been measured directly: no published study puts a self-report EF battery next to a validated motivation scale in the same sample. We reach it through four indirect bridges (affect, shared personality, a built-in self-motivation subscale, and procrastination) and mark the missing direct number as a genuine gap rather than inventing one.

The short answer: the link between executive function and motivation is real in theory and shows up in intervention studies, but in the published correlational literature it is triangulated, not direct. No study has ever put a standard self-report EF battery and a validated self-determination (SDT) motivation scale side by side and reported the correlation between them. So we reach the EF-to-motivation connection through four indirect bridges (shared personality variance, a procrastination bridge, an affect bridge, and the intervention evidence that motivation decides whether EF effort sticks), and we mark the direct coefficient as a genuine gap rather than inventing a number to fill it.

One framing note before the evidence. This section is strictly about the wiring between EF and motivation. It does NOT cover how either one predicts the 12 durable skills (that is RQ3). If a study correlates an EF measure with, say, communication or resilience, it lives in RQ3, not here.

1. Theory: why these constructs should relate (and why the relationship is messy)

Think of executive function as the engine and motivation as the fuel. EF is the set of mental control processes (holding things in mind, resisting distraction, switching gears, and, in the broader applied list, managing time, planning, getting started, and regulating emotion) that let a person steer behavior toward a goal. Motivation, in SDT terms, is about why the person is steering at all and how self-endorsed that reason feels. An engine with no fuel does not move, and fuel with no engine does not steer. So on its face EF and motivation should be related: you need both to actually pursue a goal.

SDT sharpens this into a specific mechanism. Ryan and Deci's account (Moderate as a measured claim, high-authority as a review) holds that controlled motivation (doing something because you feel you have to) is vitality-draining because it sets up internal conflict, while autonomous motivation (doing something you genuinely endorse) protects against self-regulation fatigue. In plain terms: the same act of executive effort is expensive when you are forcing yourself and cheaper when you actually want to. That converges with the older "ego-depletion" idea that self-control is a limited resource: motivation quality is predicted to change how fast the EF tank empties.

The companion theoretical pillar is the satisfaction-versus-frustration distinction. Vansteenkiste and Ryan (High for the SDT distinction itself) argue that need satisfaction energizes growth-oriented, effortful, self-regulated functioning, while need frustration awakens passivity and defensiveness. EF is exactly the effortful, self-regulated functioning that satisfaction is supposed to energize, so the theory predicts that frustrated needs (especially competence and autonomy frustration) would degrade EF deployment. That is the conceptual reason to expect an EF-to-motivation relationship at all.

But theory also predicts the relationship will be hard to measure cleanly, for two reasons. First, both constructs, when measured by rating self-report (which is how C2A measures everything), share variance with the same affective and dispositional space, so part of any correlation is the same trait measured twice. Second, the SDT prediction is dynamic (motivation quality changes the cost of EF effort over time), which a single cross-sectional survey cannot capture. So the honest theoretical expectation is: a real but modest link, easily confounded, and best seen in within-person or intervention designs rather than one-shot correlations.

Heavy stats and the four-bridge logic in full

Why "triangulated" and not "direct." A direct test would administer a rating EF battery (BRIEF-A, BDEFS, or ADEXI) and a validated SDT scale (the BPNSFS for autonomy, relatedness, and competence satisfaction and frustration, or an autonomous-versus-controlled motivation scale) to the same sample and report the EF-by-SDT correlation matrix. No such study appears in the evidence set. The single closest co-occurrence is Alesi et al. 2024 (row 2): cross-sectional, disability-only, no effect size, no DOI. So the strongest honest statement is that the direct rating-EF-by-validated-SDT-scale correlation is a published gap.

Bridge 1: affect / emotional regulation (rows 1, 4). Emotional state moderates EF (Yeung 2024: emotional distractors wiped out the incentive boost to inhibition and shifting), and need frustration versus satisfaction is the SDT engine of affect (Vansteenkiste & Ryan 2013). Emotional regulation, one of the three Deployed Applied EF, is therefore where EF and motivation physically meet. Tier: Low for the lab link (N=45, performance-based, not C2A's rating method), High for the SDT distinction, but the join between them is inferred, not measured.

Bridge 2: personality / shared variance (rows 5, 9). Rating EF correlates with mood, conscientiousness (ρ ≈ .20, Wilmot & Ones 2019), and neuroticism; SDT autonomous motivation lives in that same dispositional space. So part of any EF-to-motivation overlap is the same Big-Five variance measured twice, a "jangle" risk (two different names for one underlying thing). Even if a direct correlation were published, it would be partly common-trait and common-method variance rather than a pure functional link.

Bridge 3: BDEFS Self-Motivation (row 8). A rating EF instrument literally contains a self-motivation subscale (Knouse, Feldman & Blevins 2014, samples of 250 and 229). This is the most direct rating-EF-to-motivation tie that exists, but it is overlap by construction: the instrument defines self-motivation as part of EF, so it cannot serve as independent evidence that EF and SDT-motivation correlate. It is good for face validity, not for a clean coefficient. (the Self-Motivation-subscale framing of this paper is unverified at the subscale level; see Background. Treat it as a face-validity bridge, not a confirmed coefficient.)

Bridge 4: procrastination (row 7). Procrastination is simultaneously an applied-EF failure (task initiation, self-monitoring) and a motivational/avoidance phenomenon, and self-report EF predicts it (Rabin et al. 2011). This is the cleanest behavioral overlap, Moderate tier, but it is a shared symptom, not a measured EF-by-SDT-scale correlation.

The theoretical spine (rows 3, 4). SDT predicts that EF effort under controlled motivation depletes while autonomous motivation makes it sustainable (Ryan & Deci 2020), and that need frustration degrades the growth-oriented functioning EF supports (Vansteenkiste & Ryan 2013). This is why we expect an EF-to-motivation relationship. But theory plus four indirect bridges is not the same as a direct coefficient.

A note on what was deliberately excluded. Several high-strength findings in the source files are NOT in this table because they are EF-to-outcome or SDT-to-outcome relationships (RQ3) or coaching-intervention efficacy rather than an EF-to-motivation correlation. Excluded: all EF-to-academic-achievement and EF-to-quality-of-life claims; all SDT-to-achievement, persistence, and dropout claims; and all coaching-intervention trials (which show coaching changes EF and motivation, not the cross-sectional link RQ1 asks for). Those belong to RQ3 and RQ2.

3. Method and measurement caveats: what bounds every number above

  • Rating, not performance. C2A measures all EF by rating self-report (a person scoring their own working memory or planning), with no lab tasks. The large performance-based EF literature does not transfer, because performance and rating EF correlate only r ≈ .1 to .3 (median r approx .19; range roughly .1 to .3) (row 6, Toplak 2013). They are effectively different measurement worlds. RQ1 evidence must come from rating-EF studies, which are scarcer, so the table is thin partly because we are honestly excluding the bigger lab literature.
  • Common-method variance inflates everything. Where a rating-EF-to-motivation correlation does eventually get measured on a single survey, both sides are self-report at the same timepoint, so shared-method variance (the standard estimate is around 26%) inflates the coefficient. Treat any future single-survey number as an upper bound, not a clean estimate.
  • Jangle / part-whole risk. Rating EF and SDT motivation share personality variance (bridge 2) and, in the BDEFS case, share an actual subscale (bridge 3). So a measured EF-to-motivation correlation can be partly two labels for one thing rather than two things relating. This matters doubly for the internal pilot, where the engine reuses each measured signal as an ingredient that rolls up into as many as five durable skills, so any internal predictive advantage is partly predicting a thing from its own parts: an upper bound, not clean evidence.
  • Self-report inflation. People who rate their own EF poorly also tend to rate their mood and dispositions negatively (row 5, Meltzer 2017), so the correlation can ride on a general negative-self-view halo rather than a true functional tie.
  • Population limits. The one cross-sectional EF-and-motivation co-occurrence (row 2) is in a disability sample (specific learning disabilities), and the procrastination bridge is in college students. There is no clean general-adult rating-EF-by-validated-SDT-scale correlation in this evidence set. So even the triangulated edges generalize cautiously.

4. Gaps: stated plainly

  • No direct coefficient exists. No published study reports a rating-EF battery against a validated SDT autonomous-motivation scale in the same sample. This is the central gap, and it is genuine, not a search artifact.
  • No need-by-need EF table exists. The literature does not resolve any SDT need (autonomy, relatedness, competence, satisfaction or frustration) against any EF dimension with a coefficient. The per-need table above is mostly empty by necessity.
  • No general-adult, rating-based, non-clinical study. What little direct co-occurrence exists is in disability or student samples.
  • No within-person test of the depletion prediction. The strongest theoretical claim (controlled motivation depletes EF, autonomous motivation protects it) is dynamic, and the evidence set contains no longitudinal or diary study testing it on rating-based EF.

What this could mean for the coach report

Because the EF-to-motivation link is triangulated rather than directly measured, the coach report should present EF and motivation as two related lenses on the same student, not as one causing the other. A coach can be told "this student rates low on task initiation and also shows signs of amotivation, and these often travel together," which the procrastination and affect bridges support, but the report should not assert a precise EF-causes-motivation (or vice versa) magnitude, because no such number is published. The honest framing (related, co-occurring, theoretically linked, but not directly quantified) is itself the responsible thing to surface to coaches.

RQ3: How EF and SDT/motivation measures relate to the 12 durable skills (the reported outcome)

TL;DRStrong evidence linking a predictor to a durable-skill outcome lives in two places: proxy outcomes (grades, GPA, job performance) and a few named-skill literatures (critical thinking, creative problem solving, empathy, adaptability, resilience). The short answer: the strong, trustworthy published evidence linking a predictor to a durable-skill outcome lives almost entirely in two places. First, in proxy outcomes (grades, GPA, job performance, persistence) where a real-world result stands in for the unmeasured skill. Second, in a handful of named-skill literatures where a single durable skill (critical thinking, creative problem solving, empathy, adaptability, resilience) has been studied on its own. Nobody has ever published a study that uses "the 12 LE3 durable skills" as a labeled outcome bundle and related EF or motivation to it. So the honest picture is: robust at the proxy level, thinner and more scattered at the named-skill level, and empty at the "durable skills as a set" level.

Think of it like trying to predict whether someone is a good cook. We have solid data on whether they finish culinary school (the proxy), patchy data on individual dishes they can make (the named skills), and no study at all that scores "the 12 signature dishes this specific restaurant calls durable." That is exactly the shape of the evidence here.

1. Theory: why these constructs should relate

The two-layer logic of the C2A design predicts these links, and the theory behind each is sound. Executive function is the brain's air-traffic-control system: holding goals in mind (working memory), filtering out distractions (inhibition), and switching strategies when the situation changes (cognitive flexibility). Every one of the 12 durable skills is a complex, goal-directed behavior, and complex goal-directed behavior is precisely what EF is theorized to enable. Critical thinking needs you to hold a claim in mind while you check it against counter-evidence (working memory plus inhibition). Adaptability is, almost by definition, cognitive flexibility expressed in the wild. Empathy and social awareness require holding another person's mental state in mind while suppressing your own default reading (working memory plus inhibition). So the theory says EF should sit beneath the durable skills as an enabling engine, which is exactly where the construct map places it.

On the motivation side, Self-Determination Theory (SDT) predicts that when the three basic needs are met (autonomy = "I choose this," relatedness = "I belong here," competence = "I can do this"), people engage more deeply and persist longer, which over time builds skill. Met needs feed autonomous motivation, autonomous motivation drives sustained engagement, and sustained engagement is how a durable skill gets durable. The theory is directional and well-specified. The catch, which we return to in the gaps section, is that "engagement builds skill over time" is a longitudinal claim, and most of the evidence we have is a single snapshot in time.

3. Method and measurement caveats

Three problems sit underneath every number above. A coach report that quotes these correlations without carrying these caveats would be overselling.

The adult EF-to-work gap. The strongest, highest-tier EF-to-outcome figure (r ≈ .365) is from children. There is no general-adult meta-analysis to match it. The adult evidence that does exist comes mostly from ADHD, autism, and TBI samples, where EF problems are larger and easier to detect. So when anyone says "EF predicts durable-skill outcomes in adults," they are stitching together a children's meta-analysis and a set of clinical-adult studies. That stitch is reasonable, but it is an inference, not a measured general-adult fact, and the coach report should say so.

Self-report inflation and shared-method variance. Howard et al. 2021 is the cleanest demonstration: the same predictor correlates about .13 with objective achievement but about .32 with self-reported achievement, more than double, just because the outcome was self-rated. C2A measures everything by rating self-report, with no lab tasks anywhere, and the durable-skill scores are themselves built from self-report instruments. When the predictor and the outcome are both rated by the same person at the same sitting, a chunk of the correlation is just the person's general self-view leaking into both answers (the "mono-method bias" the source files flag). The honest implication: any EF-to-durable-skill or motivation-to-durable-skill association observed inside C2A should be read toward the objective end of Howard's range, not the self-reported end.

The part-whole caveat, and why the internal pilot numbers cannot be cited as evidence here. The C2A engine reuses each measured signal as an ingredient that rolls up into as many as five durable skills. The deployed construct map is explicit: each of the three Deployed Applied EF reads the same IPIP subscale that feeds a durable-skill outcome (task initiation and the Initiative durable skill share ipip_cluster_1; self-monitoring and Critical Thinking share ipip_cluster_2; emotional control and Adaptability share ipip_cluster_4, at the item level; kinship with Collaboration and Resilience is conceptual, not item-level). So if an internal signal looks like it "predicts" a durable-skill score, part of that is predicting a thing from its own parts. That is an upper bound, not clean evidence. This is exactly why RQ3 stands only on the external published studies in Tables A and B, and why any internal pilot result may be framed only as "consistent with" or "a hypothesis to test."

One more measurement note: jangle and "looks like real life" is not "predicts real life"

Several of the named-skill measures are self-perception scales (for example, self-perceived communication competence). A self-perception of a skill and the skill itself are not the same thing even when they share a name (the "jangle" problem: two differently-named things that are actually the same, or one name covering two different things). Relatedly, the veridicality-versus-verisimilitude guardrail says a measure that looks like real life is not automatically a measure that predicts real life. Both point the same direction: discount, do not inflate, when predictor and outcome are both self-reports of similar-sounding constructs.

4. Gaps: what is not published, stated plainly

  • No "12 durable skills" outcome bundle exists in the literature. The 12-skill rubric is a ThriveIEP/NLU construct. The published literature speaks to the named skills one at a time, or to proxies, never to the bundle. There is no number to put in this cell, and none should be invented.
  • No general-adult EF-to-real-world-outcome meta-analysis. The clean meta-analytic anchor (Cortes-Pascual) is children. The adult anchors are clinical (ADHD/autism/TBI). The typical college-age adult is the population we most want and have the least clean evidence for.
  • Several interpersonal skills rest on non-typical samples. Collaboration, Curiosity, Networking, and Relationship Building draw their EF/SDT evidence from neurodivergent, older-adult, or child samples, or from broad SDT reviews, rather than labeled-outcome studies in typical adults. Tier these Low to Moderate with a population-limit flag.
  • Most named-skill links are single, cross-sectional studies. A single snapshot cannot tell us whether the predictor builds the skill over time (the SDT theory's actual claim) or just co-occurs with it. The longitudinal exceptions (Garn 2019, Austin 2014, the transfer studies) are the trustworthy minority.

What this could mean for the coach report

The high-tier, population-general evidence clusters on two edges: the motivation-to-proxy edge (Cerasoli, Van den Broeck, the longitudinal ARC-mediation chain) and the EF-to-cognitive-skill edge (working memory and cognitive flexibility into creative problem solving, critical thinking, social awareness, and adaptability). A coach report could present those links with confidence. The interpersonal skills (Networking, Relationship Building, Collaboration) and Curiosity ride on neurodivergent, older-adult, or review-level evidence, so they should carry lower-confidence flags. And because every C2A measure is a same-sitting self-report, the report should frame any EF-to-skill or motivation-to-skill story as an explanatory hypothesis a coach can probe with the student, not as a measured predictive fact, with the self-report-inflation and part-whole caveats stated rather than buried.

A quick orientation on what this question even is. The student report already runs on the three core EF, which this brief calls the EF triad: working memory (holding goals in mind), inhibition (filtering out distractions), and cognitive flexibility (switching strategies when the situation changes). The applied EF name more specific, everyday capacities such as task initiation, planning, and time management.   • Deployed Applied EF (the BRIEF-2A trio, also called the self-regulation domains): task initiation, self-monitoring, emotional regulation. Already scored, already shown to coaches.
  • Candidate Applied EF (not built): planning/prioritization, organization, time management, sustained attention. Not measured today.

We are asking whether applied EF helps a coach explain why a durable skill is constrained, not whether it is a new outcome to report. Second, C2A measures every EF by self-report rating. 1. Theory: why applied EF should, or should not, add value over core EF

The theoretical case for the expansion rests on one robust, well-cited finding: rating-based "everyday" EF and performance-based "lab" EF measure genuinely different things and correlate only weakly, somewhere around r = .1 to .3 (median r approx .19; range roughly .1 to .3).

Lab tasks measure the efficiency of a cognitive ability under tidy, structured conditions (can you hold seven digits, can you stop a button-press on cue). Ratings measure how well goal pursuit actually goes in the messy, open-ended contexts of real life (do you start your assignments, do you lose your keys, do you blow deadlines). The classic statement of this divergence is Toplak, West and Stanovich 2013 High, and several studies show that the rating side out-predicts the lab side for real-world academic and occupational outcomes (Barkley and Murphy 2010 High; Dijkhuis et al. 2020 Moderate). On its face, this says: the contextualized, applied facets carry the real-world signal, so give coaches the applied facets.

But there is a sharp catch, and it is the single most important theoretical point in this section. That literature compares ratings to lab tasks. C2A has no lab tasks. It measures its three core EF by rating (ADEXI, a cognitive-flexibility rating scale), and the three applied EF it already deploys are IPIP rating items too (any added ones would be as well). So the "applied beats core" headline cannot be the reason C2A applied EF would beat C2A core EF, because both C2A layers are ratings of the same person at the same sitting. The lab-versus-rating gap simply does not transfer to a rating-versus-rating comparison.

What survives is a narrower, weaker, but still defensible claim: within rating scales, the more contextualized and applied facets (planning, organization, metacognition) tend to carry more unique predictive weight for everyday outcomes than the broad EF-triad facets (working memory, inhibition, flexibility) do. That narrower claim is what the per-dimension evidence below actually supports. It is worth keeping the distinction crisp, because an unwary brief would borrow the strength of the famous lab-versus-rating result for a C2A claim it cannot actually back.

The theoretical case against a blanket expansion is the non-redundancy problem. If an applied EF is just core EF wearing everyday clothes, or worse, if it re-measures a durable skill the student report already shows, then adding it inflates the dashboard without adding information. Theory predicts exactly this for the three Deployed dimensions: task initiation shares items with the Initiative durable skill, self-monitoring shares items with Critical Thinking (and is conceptually upstream of Collaboration), and emotional regulation shares items with Adaptability (and is conceptually bound up with Resilience). The theory, in other words, predicts a mixed result, and that is what the evidence delivers.

2. Evidence per applied EF: validity, incremental value, and overlap

For each of the seven, three questions matter. Validity: is the construct real and separately predictive? Incremental value: does it add signal beyond core EF (and is it a movable, coachable target)? Overlap risk: which core EF and, critically, which already-reported durable skill it may re-measure. Effect sizes and study details live in the collapsible blocks; the prose carries the verdict.

2a. Planning / Prioritization (not deployed): the strongest, cleanest add on the evidence

Validity is High and incremental value is Moderate. The planning and organization facets, more than the broad inhibition facet, are the EF components most tied to academic outcomes, and planning is separately teachable, which is the clearest evidence that it is its own lever rather than a relabeling of the EF triad.

Planning/prioritization evidence detail
  • The BRIEF Plan/Organize subscale (rated) predicted school grades above and beyond ADHD symptoms, IQ, and prior achievement. Source: Langberg, Dvorsky and Evans 2013, DOI 10.1007/s10802-013-9750-z. Moderate (the finding is high-confidence, but the sample is youth ADHD).
  • Meta-analysis (21 samples, n = 7,947): metacognition/behavioral-regulation subscales and cognitive flexibility were the EF components most associated with academic performance (overall EF r = .365). Source: Cortes-Pascual et al. 2019, DOI 10.3389/fpsyg.2019.01582. High, but children aged 6 to 12, not adults.
  • Direct instruction of metacognition (plan, monitor, evaluate) produced large learning and transfer gains versus extended practice (d = 0.64 to 0.87). Source: Zepeda et al. 2015, ERIC EJ1082661. High design quality, but adolescent science learning, not coaching.
  • Brief if-then planning (Mental Contrasting with Implementation Intentions) raised goal-directed practice by more than 60%. Sources: Duckworth et al. 2011, ERIC EJ911106 High; Clark et al. 2021, ERIC EJ1304548 Moderate.

Overlap risk: lowest of the seven. Planning/prioritization does not map cleanly onto any single one of the 12 durable skills, which is precisely what makes it a good expansion candidate: it is plausibly genuinely new diagnostic signal. It has mild conceptual overlap with Critical Thinking and Creative Problem Solving (planning is an input to both) and a built-in dependence on core working memory (you cannot hold a plan without it), but it does not re-measure a reported outcome. This is the dimension to add first.

2b. Organization and 2c. Time Management (not deployed): a cluster, not two dials

Both have Moderate validity and Moderate incremental value, and both are demonstrably movable by coaching. The important nuance is that organization, time management, and planning travel together in nearly every intervention study, which is a double-edged finding: it shows they respond to coaching, but it also suggests they may be hard to separate statistically. They may be one factor wearing three names.

Organization and time-management evidence detail
  • Organizational-skills interventions (OTMP: organization, time management, planning) improved planning, time management, and study behaviors in college students with ADHD. Source: LaCount et al. 2018, DOI 10.1177/1087054715594423. Moderate (RCT).
  • A 17-study systematic review of EF coaching for college students with disabilities reported improved GPA, time management, organization, study skills, behavioral regulation, and metacognition. Source: Anderson and Marino 2024, ERIC EJ1445898. Moderate.
  • "Organization-in-time" added 10.8% unique variance to quality of life beyond diagnosis in adult ADHD (alongside the BRIEF-A Metacognition Index at 15.1%). Source: Grinblat and Rosenblum 2025, DOI 10.3390/brainsci15121262. Moderate.
  • Time management is repeatedly the most-cited hurdle to college completion for neurodivergent students and among the most frequently taught study-skills constructs (58.3% of programs). Sources: Earnest and Crowley 2025, DOI 10.1080/07370008.2025.2463652 Moderate; Kittelman et al. 2025, DOI 10.1177/21651434251364748 Low.
  • The cleanest "time management is a movable target" evidence: a pragmatic RCT (N = 75) of a group time-management intervention for adults with ADHD/autism/mental disorders produced significant, 3-month-durable gains in time management, organization, planning, and (as a side effect) emotional regulation. Source: Lidstrom Holmqvist et al. 2026, DOI 10.1080/09638288.2026.2614218. Moderate.

Overlap risk: low against durable skills, moderate within the applied-EF set. Neither organization nor time management re-measures any of the 12 durable skills. Their redundancy risk is internal: heavy bundling with each other and with planning. The recommendation that falls out of this is to treat planning, organization, and time management as a single candidate cluster, not three independent dials, until evidence for their separability exists. Showing a coach three near-identical bars would manufacture false precision.

2d. Task Initiation (deployed today): valid, but a high overlap trap with the "Initiative" durable skill

Validity is Moderate and incremental value is Moderate (recently upgraded from clinical consensus to controlled evidence). The single best published hint that task initiation is not already captured by the three core EF is that the BRIEF-A Initiation scale predicted academic procrastination over and above working memory and inhibition.

Task-initiation evidence detail
  • BRIEF-A Initiation was a significant independent predictor of academic procrastination, above and beyond working memory and inhibition. Source: Rabin, Fogel and Nutter-Upham 2011, DOI 10.1080/13803395.2010.518597. Moderate. This "over and above core EF" result is the key non-redundancy evidence against core EF.
  • A multicenter RCT (N = 108) showed CBT targeting behavioral activation (CADDI) outperformed standard CBT on the activation outcome (d = 0.49), upgrading task initiation from consensus to controlled evidence. Source: Stralin et al. 2025, DOI 10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1564506. Moderate.
  • A movement-and-mentorship EF coaching program (no therapy frame) showed significant pre-post gains in task initiation, planning/organization, and working memory. Source: Wilcox et al. 2026, DOI 10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1761762. Low (quality-improvement study, no control group).

Overlap risk: high, and it is the marquee example of the non-redundancy trap. "Initiative" is one of the 12 reported durable skills (Pillar 2, Leaders with Purpose and Agency). "Task initiation" is the candidate applied EF. In theory these are not the same: the durable-skill Initiative is self-starting, proactivity, and agency in pursuit of goals, while EF task initiation is the narrower neurocognitive capacity to begin a task without undue procrastination. But to a coach skimming a report, "low on Initiative" and "low on Task Initiation" will read as the same sentence. The deeper problem is internal and now verified in the deployed construct map: the live initiate dimension reads ipip_cluster_1 (subscale initiative_initiate), the very same subscale that feeds the Initiative durable-skill score. The applied-EF dimension uses that subscale raw, while the durable skill blends it with other sources (Initiative weights the cluster at roughly 0.46 alongside a CAAS-SF subscale), so they are not identical numbers, but they ride on the identical items. Any apparent "task initiation explains Initiative" relationship is therefore partly an artifact of shared items, predicting a thing from its own parts. Decision flag: because this dimension is already live and shown to coaches, the coach report must explicitly separate "the capacity to start (EF, the why)" from "the disposition to take initiative (durable skill, the what)," or it risks showing the coach the same number twice with two labels.

2e. Self-Monitoring (deployed today): strong "why" candidate, high overlap with Critical Thinking and Collaboration

Validity is Moderate and incremental value is Moderate (also recently upgraded by controlled trials). Like task initiation, self-monitoring predicted procrastination over and above working memory and inhibition, and the broader metacognition index uniquely predicted quality of life. As an explanatory layer it is one of the most appealing candidates: watching your own output is conceptually upstream of evaluating your reasoning and reading your effect on a group.

Self-monitoring evidence detail
  • BRIEF-A Self-Monitor predicted academic procrastination over and above working memory and inhibition. Source: Rabin et al. 2011, DOI 10.1080/13803395.2010.518597. Moderate.
  • Metacognitive (self-monitoring) deficits are a distinct contributor to functional impairment in adult ADHD, separable from core attention/hyperactivity symptoms. Source: "Training of awareness in ADHD: Leveraging metacognition," Journal of Psychiatry and Brain Science 2024, DOI 10.20900/jpbs.20240006. Low (minireview; Crossref lists no author for this record, so the earlier "Lenartowicz et al." attribution is unverified).
  • The BRIEF-A Metacognition Index uniquely predicted quality of life (+15.1% variance beyond diagnosis). Source: Grinblat and Rosenblum 2025, DOI 10.3390/brainsci15121262. Moderate.
  • CO-OP metacognitive strategy training (Goal-Plan-Do-Check) beat usual occupational therapy on both trained and untrained everyday goals (generalization), though gains faded by 3 months without boosters. Source: Dawson et al. 2025, DOI 10.3390/brainsci15111195. Moderate (RCT, N = 87).
  • A "strategic mindset" field experiment (N = 1,070) raised strategy use and exam scores via metacognitive prompting. Source: Chen et al. 2025, DOI 10.1038/s41539-025-00367-6. Moderate.

Overlap risk: high. Per the deployed construct map, the live self_monitor dimension reads ipip_cluster_2 (subscale critical_thinking_sm), the very same subscale that feeds the Critical Thinking durable skill, so at the item level it re-measures Critical Thinking outright. Its kinship with Collaboration is conceptual (self-monitoring as the upstream "why" of reading your effect on a group), not item-level. (Worth noting for anyone cross-checking the decision records: DR-042's design-intent table mapped self-monitoring to Collaboration and Social Awareness, but the deployed construct map wires it to the Critical Thinking subscale; this brief follows the deployed source, which is the authority.) Because the Critical Thinking items are literally shared, any "self-monitoring predicts Critical Thinking" pattern observed internally is partly a shared-item artifact, not independent evidence. Decision flag: self-monitoring is a strong diagnostic-why candidate worth surfacing to coaches, but it must not be presented as independent corroboration of the Critical Thinking score it helped produce.

2f. Emotional Regulation (deployed today): high overlap plus a scope-of-practice flag

Validity is Moderate and incremental value is Moderate but largely indirect: in the strongest evidence, emotional regulation improves as a secondary cascade of time-management and SDT-based coaching, not as a directly targeted dimension. It can be moved directly in non-clinical samples by structured non-therapy programs, but the cleanest signal here is a side-effect signal.

Emotional-regulation evidence detail
  • Emotional dysregulation is named a core feature of adult ADHD in the field's major consensus paper. Source: Cortese et al. 2025, World Psychiatry, DOI 10.1002/wps.21374. Moderate.
  • Adults with ADHD consistently self-identify emotional dysregulation among their most impairing challenges. Sources: Rosenthal et al. 2025, DOI 10.1177/10870547251352589 Moderate; Grinblat and Rosenblum 2025, DOI 10.1192/j.eurpsy.2025.2169 Low (qualitative).
  • Strongest signal is indirect: emotional regulation improved as a secondary outcome of time-management and SDT-based coaching. Source: Lidstrom Holmqvist et al. 2026, DOI 10.1080/09638288.2026.2614218. Moderate.
  • It can be moved directly: a mindfulness RCT (N = 114) improved emotion regulation versus active control (p = 0.03, d = 0.41). Source: Mendola et al. 2026, DOI 10.1177/13591045251377898. Moderate.
  • Negative-trial caution: the Haugan 2022 RCT (DOI 10.1186/s12888-022-04019-6) is a verified negative result; group CBT added no incremental effect on BRIEF EF (effect sizes near zero). An earlier version of this finding carried fabricated effect sizes (d = 0.58, d = 0.44) that do not appear in the paper. Do not cite Haugan as evidence that BRIEF subscales are intervention-sensitive.

Overlap risk: high, plus a product-risk flag. Per the deployed construct map, the live emotional_control dimension reads ipip_cluster_4 (subscale adaptability_ec), the very same subscale that feeds the Adaptability durable skill, so at the item level it re-measures Adaptability outright. Its kinship with Resilience is conceptual rather than item-level. Either way it risks re-measuring a reported outcome the student report already shows. On top of redundancy there is a scope-of-practice boundary: the coaching field is split on whether emotional regulation is even coaching territory, with several 2026 practitioner sources framing it as the boundary domain that may trigger a therapy referral (shame spirals, shutdown) and a minority treating it as integral to coaching (practitioner blogs, Low). Surfacing emotional regulation to coaches therefore carries a product-risk dimension beyond pure statistical redundancy.

2g. Sustained Attention (not deployed): the weakest case for a standalone dial

Validity as a construct is Moderate, but incremental value as a separate, trainable target is Low. Sustained attention is reliably named as a core EF challenge, and CBT reliably improves inattention symptoms, but "sustained attention as a trainable capacity" is the dimension with the least clean support: the best vigilance-training RCT was null, and there is no adult dimension-specific RCT showing it is a separable, coachable lever.

Sustained-attention evidence detail
  • Listed consistently as a core EF challenge by adults with ADHD. Sources: Grinblat and Rosenblum 2025 (qualitative) Low; Dawson and Guare practitioner framework Low.
  • CBT for adult ADHD reliably improves inattention symptoms (Cochrane meta-analysis, Lopez et al. 2018, DOI 10.1002/14651858.cd010840.pub2 High), but the cleanest sustained-attention lab-vigilance-training RCT was null. So inattention symptoms respond while sustained attention as a capacity remains weakly supported. Source: claim GC1, nlu-coach-view-construct-compendium. High confidence on the null/mixed picture.
  • The compendium's own gap-search summary states sustained attention "remains weakest."

Overlap risk: low against durable skills, but the binding problem here is not overlap, it is weak standalone evidence. There is no adult RCT showing sustained attention is a separable, coachable dimension. It is the applied EF with the least support for inclusion as a distinct dial.

3. Non-redundancy, assessed three ways

The reference frame demands that "non-redundant" be tested against three things, not one. Here is the verdict on each axis.

Against the 3 core EF. This is the axis the expansion clears most cleanly for the right dimensions. The two cleanest results, that task initiation and self-monitoring each predicted procrastination over and above working memory and inhibition (Rabin et al. 2011), are the published evidence that at least some applied EF carry signal the core three do not. Planning/organization being the EF components most tied to academic outcomes (rather than the inhibition facet) points the same way. So against core EF, the applied facets are not redundant, with the caveat that this evidence is rating-based and mostly clinical.

Against the ARC / motivation layer. This is the axis where the evidence is thinnest, and it is handled in detail in RQ1, so we cross-reference rather than reproduce. RQ1's finding is that the EF-to-motivation link is real in theory but triangulated, not directly measured, reached through the affect, shared-personality-variance, BDEFS Self-Motivation, and procrastination bridges. The practical implication for RQ2 is that applied EF and the motivation layer are not clean substitutes for each other: they share variance (especially through procrastination and self-motivation), but no published study puts a rating EF battery beside a validated SDT scale, so we cannot say applied EF is redundant with ARC. The honest stance is that the overlap is partial and unmeasured, which argues for keeping them as distinct lenses rather than collapsing them.

Against the 12 durable skills already reported. This is the axis with the sharpest, most actionable findings, and it is where three of the seven fail the clean-add test. Those three are exactly the Deployed trio. Task initiation overlaps Initiative, self-monitoring overlaps Critical Thinking, and emotional regulation overlaps Adaptability, each at the item level (their kinship with Collaboration and Resilience is conceptual). The mechanism is not merely conceptual: per the deployed construct map, each Deployed Applied EF reads the very same IPIP subscale that feeds its durable-skill score (clusters 1, 2, and 4 respectively). RQ3 documents the EF-to-durable-skill relationships in full; we cross-reference it here rather than reproduce its tables. The RQ2-specific point is that for these three dimensions, an internal "applied EF predicts durable skill" relationship is partly predicting a thing from its own parts, which is an upper bound, not clean evidence.

4. Method and measurement caveats

Four caveats sit underneath every claim above and must travel with any coach-report decision.

Rating-versus-lab does not save the C2A case. Repeating the load-bearing point from the theory section: the famous "applied EF beats core EF" literature compares ratings to lab tasks, and C2A rates everything, so that result does not directly justify C2A applied EF over C2A core EF. The defensible narrower claim is that within ratings, contextualized facets tend to carry more unique everyday-outcome variance than abstract facets.

Common-method variance and self-report inflation. Because predictor and outcome are both self-reports from the same person at the same sitting, a chunk of any observed correlation is the person's general self-view leaking into both answers rather than a true construct relationship. The published twin of this concern is the verisimilitude-versus-veridicality guardrail: a measure that looks like real life (Suchy et al. 2024, DOI 10.1037/pas0001297 High) is not automatically a measure that predicts real life.

The part-whole / dual-scoring artifact (the published twin of Hard Rule 2). The engine reuses each measured signal as an ingredient that rolls up into as many as five durable skills. So any internal "applied EF predicts durable skill" result is partly mechanical. This is exactly why the internal pilot's predictive numbers cannot stand as evidence here, and why internal signal may be framed only as "consistent with" or "a hypothesis to test against an item-independent outcome" such as a coach rating or a later timepoint.

Population limits. Nearly all applied-EF coaching evidence rides on ADHD, learning-disability, autism, or other neurodivergent/disability samples (Langberg, Solanto, LaCount, Lidstrom Holmqvist, Stralin, and others). EF deficits are larger and easier to detect in these groups, so the correlations may not transfer cleanly to the general college population C2A serves. The scarcity is not a search artifact: one scoping review found only 28 usable EF-in-higher-education studies out of 8,237 screened (Pretorius and Heyns 2026, DOI 10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1636067 Moderate). Controlled EF intervention research in general college populations is genuinely sparse.

5. Gaps: what is not published, stated plainly

  • No head-to-head rating-versus-rating test. No published study compares a rating core-EF battery against a rating applied-EF battery for predicting the same real-world outcome, which is the exact comparison C2A's design would need to justify the expansion on its own terms. The lab-versus-rating literature is a neighbor, not a substitute.
  • No general-population applied-EF coaching evidence. The coaching evidence is overwhelmingly clinical/neurodivergent. Whether applied EF is a movable, coachable lever in typical college students is untested.
  • No separability evidence for the planning/organization/time-management cluster. They co-move in every intervention; no study cleanly factors them apart, so we cannot say they are three independent dials.
  • Sustained attention has no adult, dimension-specific, separable-lever RCT. The capacity (as opposed to the symptom) remains the weakest-supported of the seven.
  • The internal advantage, if any, is unmeasurable from current data without an item-independent outcome. Until an applied-EF signal is tested against something it did not help build (a coach rating, a later timepoint), every internal "it predicts the durable skill" number is an upper bound, not evidence.

What this could mean for the coach report

Reconciling the evidence with what shipped. On the published evidence alone, the cleanest, lowest-redundancy adds would be Planning/Prioritization and the Organization/Time-Management cluster (presented as one lens, not three dials). But those are exactly the four not built, and DR-042 had a defensible reason: it judged the planning and organization family already covered by the core-EF readouts. Meanwhile the three already shipped, Task Initiation, Self-Monitoring, and Emotional Regulation, are the high-overlap ones, each reading the same IPIP subscale that feeds a reported durable skill (Initiative; Critical Thinking; Adaptability). So the live question is not "what to add" but "how to frame what is already in front of coaches."

For the three already deployed and on the coach dashboard: they are valid and genuinely useful as explanatory signal, but the coach report must carry explicit anti-double-counting framing, separating "the capacity to start, monitor, or regulate (EF, the why)" from "the durable skill it shadows (the what)," with emotional regulation also carrying a coaching-versus-therapy boundary note. They must not be shown as independent corroboration of the durable-skill scores they helped produce. For the four not built: if the team revisits the expansion, planning/prioritization is the best-supported next candidate, followed by the organization/time-management cluster, with sustained attention the weakest case and reasonable to defer. Either way, because every C2A signal is a same-sitting self-report and the engine reuses each signal across up to five durable skills, the applied EF belong in the coach report as explanatory hypotheses a coach can probe with the student, not as measured predictive facts.

Honest limits

Honest limits and evidence-quality summary

None are fatal; stating them is what makes the coach report trustworthy.
