Designing Coach View: What the Evidence Says Belongs In It

A leadership-facing decision brief on what should go into Coach View, a proposed coach-facing profile. It reviews the science behind sixteen candidate building blocks, then scores each on evidence, feasibility, and non-redundancy to produce a ranked inclusion recommendation. The decision sits in Part 6; Parts 1 to 5 are the evidence behind it.

Prepared for ThriveIEP leadership · Drawn from the ThriveIEP research archive and published literature · Instrument facts verified against C2A data files

Executive Summary

This report exists to answer one question: of sixteen candidate building blocks, what should go into Coach View, the proposed coach-facing profile? It scores each candidate on evidence, feasibility, and non-redundancy, and ends in Part 6 with a ranked inclusion scorecard and a cutline. Everything between here and there is the evidence feeding that call.

Key message 1. The skills that stick are the ones a person owns. When someone works on a skill because it matters to them, it lasts. When they do it because someone is making them, it fades the moment the pressure lifts. Motivation type, not motivation amount, is the durability switch.
Key message 2. The relationships among our dimensions are real but modest, and they chain together. Psychological needs feed motivation strongly. Motivation feeds real-world performance modestly. Each link is published and measurable. No single link is large enough to carry the product alone, which is exactly why the layered model matters.
Key message 3. Coach View does not exist yet. It is a proposed coach-facing profile, and the job of this report is to decide what should go in it, based on the evidence. The idea is to show a coach a picture of a student across sixteen candidate building blocks drawn from the research: ten executive-function skills (the brain's self-management tools, like working memory and time management), two about motivation (what a student needs and why they act), and four about their situation (things like financial stress and culture that color everything else). To ground that decision, we read our current C2A assessment files to see how many of the sixteen we could already measure today. Five have their own questionnaire: working memory, inhibition, cognitive flexibility, psychological needs, and motivation type. The other eleven do not. Nine have no questions behind them anywhere in the assessment, including the seven everyday-skill ideas (like time management and task initiation) that coaches lean on most. One, strengths-first, is a way of framing results, not something to score. One, culture, we can only touch sideways: we can measure a person's cross-cultural skill, but not their cultural background. Our current assessment is not thin; it asks plenty that rolls up into our twelve durable-skill scores. The takeaway is that most of these sixteen candidates are well-supported in the literature but not yet measured by us, so deciding which ones are worth building is the central choice this report exists to inform.

What does the outside research say predicts durable skills?

Two findings are solid. First, the kind of everyday self-report measure we use captures how a person actually functions day to day, which is what coaching cares about and what predicts real-life outcomes. The trade-off, covered in Part 1, is that a self-report score reflects personality and mood as much as cognitive capacity. Second, motivation that comes from inside a person predicts whether a coached skill is still there months later, while pressure-driven motivation predicts short-term compliance that decays.

What do the numbers actually look like?

Psychological needs predict intrinsic motivation strongly (correlations near 0.55). Intrinsic motivation predicts performance modestly (correlation about 0.26). At work, feeling capable predicts task performance hardest (about 0.40). These are honest, medium-sized links from large meta-analyses, not blockbuster effects. Part 4 lays them out link by link.

Where should we be cautious?

The "real-life ratings beat lab tests" story is partly a measurement artifact. When you measure the skill and the outcome the same way, at the same time, from the same person, they look more linked than they are. The cleanest proof: intrinsic motivation correlates with self-reported grades at 0.32 but with actual grades at only 0.13. We must say "our signals add value," not "our signals predict outcomes," and we must never cite our own pilot's numbers as proof.

So what does the report recommend?

Build the first version of Coach View around what is both well-supported and measurable today: motivation type as the anchor, the three psychological needs as the engine behind it, and the three core-EF dimensions we already score. Add the four situational factors in their proper roles, as context flags and framing, not as student scores. Fund one or two applied-EF instruments (time management first, then planning), since those are the skills coaches most want but we cannot yet measure. Defer the rest. The full ranked scorecard, with the cutline and the close calls, is in Part 6.

How to Read This Briefing

Two ground rules shaped every claim here, so you can trust what is asserted and see clearly where we flag uncertainty.

Rule 1: We verified our own instruments against the files

Every statement about what C2A measures was checked against the actual data files in our assessment engine, not from memory. Where a construct has no instrument, we say so plainly with a not scored flag rather than implying we measure it.

Rule 2: The report stands on published research, not our pilot

The evidence here comes from the outside scientific literature. Every number in the math section is from a published meta-analysis or large study, summarized faithfully, with no speculation. We do not cite our own pilot's prediction numbers as proof. There is a specific reason for that caution, in the box below.

The part-whole caveat. Our engine deliberately re-uses each measured signal as an ingredient that rolls up into as many as six of our twelve "durable skills." So if our internal model looks like it predicts durable-skill scores from these signals, it is partly predicting a thing from its own parts. Any internal "predictive advantage" is therefore an upper bound, not clean evidence. Treat internal results only as a hypothesis to test, never as proof.

Rule 3: The final decision is structured judgment, not a formula

Part 6 scores each candidate 1 to 5 on evidence, feasibility, and non-redundancy, and draws an inclusion cutline. Those scores are our reasoning made explicit and comparable, anchored to the evidence tiers below, not a metric computed from data. The point is to make the call visible and debatable, not to imply false precision. Where you would weight the three criteria differently, the verdict is yours to move; Part 6 ends with the open calls.

Evidence tiers used throughout

High Randomized trials or meta-analyses. Moderate Correlational or quasi-experimental studies. Low / consensus Expert and clinical practice agreement, not yet tested in trials.

A note on correlations for the non-statistician. A correlation (r) runs from 0 (no relationship) to 1 (perfect). In social science, 0.1 is small, 0.3 is medium, and 0.5 is large. Squaring it gives the shared variance: a correlation of 0.3 means the two things share about 9 percent of what makes them vary. Most real human relationships live in the 0.2 to 0.4 range, so "modest" is normal, not disappointing.

Part 1 · Background

An Academic Review of the Foundations

This part is the scholarly backdrop for everything that follows. It is more technical and more thorough than the rest of the briefing on purpose: leadership should be able to see that the proposed Coach View model would rest on two mature research traditions, and should also see, in full daylight, where those two traditions blur into each other. The plain-language version of each construct comes in Part 2; this part is the literature.

Two bodies of theory underpin the proposed model. The first is Self-Determination Theory (SDT), the dominant modern account of human motivation. The second is the executive function (EF) tradition from cognitive psychology and neuropsychology. The motivation building blocks (ARC: autonomy, relatedness, competence; and motivation type) come from the first, and the ten EF dimensions from the second. The central analytic theme of this review, and the one most consequential for how a coach would read such a profile, is that when EF is measured by self-report questionnaire (as our current C2A assessment measures all of it), the EF and motivation traditions stop being cleanly separate. They share measurement, they share variance, and in at least one widely used instrument they literally share a subscale. We lay out the supportive evidence and we are explicit about where the overlap is directly measured versus only inferred.

Self-Determination Theory: Needs, Motivation Quality, and the Internalization Continuum

SDT (Ryan and Deci, 2000, 2020) holds that humans have three basic psychological needs: autonomy (acting from one's own volition), competence (feeling effective), and relatedness (feeling connected). When the social environment satisfies these needs, motivation becomes more self-determined and behavior more durable. When the environment thwarts them, motivation degrades and well-being suffers.

SDT's most important claim for our purposes is about the quality, not the quantity, of motivation. Motivation falls on an internalization continuum: external regulation (acting under reward or punishment), introjected regulation (acting from internal pressure, guilt, ego), identified regulation (acting because the goal is personally valued), integrated regulation (the goal fits one's identity), and intrinsic motivation (acting for inherent interest). The further toward the intrinsic end, the more "autonomous" the motivation, and autonomous motivation predicts persistence, deep learning, and well-being more reliably than controlled motivation does.

A refinement matters here: need satisfaction and need frustration are not simply opposite ends of one scale. Vansteenkiste and Ryan (2013) describe a dual pathway: satisfaction drives a "bright path" toward growth, while active frustration drives a separate "dark path" toward distress and defensiveness. This distinction becomes important later, because need frustration behaves like a stressor, and stress measurably degrades executive function.

Executive Function: Unity, Diversity, and a Replication Problem

The EF tradition describes the brain's self-management system. The canonical model (Miyake et al., 2000) proposes three separable but correlated core functions: inhibition, working memory (updating), and cognitive flexibility (shifting), a structure summarized as "unity and diversity." Diamond (2012) treats these three as the core from which higher-order functions like planning and reasoning are built. This three-part model is the most cited framework in the field.

It is also less settled than its popularity suggests. Karr et al. (2018) re-analyzed 46 samples and found that no single factor model converged and fit across all of them; the low rate of clean replication suggests a literature that has favored well-fitting but underpowered models. In plain terms: the idea that EF cleanly splits into three factors does not reliably reproduce. This is relevant context for any product that reports EF as a set of distinct sub-scores, including ours, and it is why the latent inter-correlations among working memory, inhibition, and flexibility appear as a "no clean published value" cell in Part 4.

A second tradition, associated with Barkley, reframes EF not as a set of cognitive abilities but as self-regulation across time: self-directed actions a person takes to manage their own future behavior. This framing deliberately folds emotion regulation and self-motivation into EF itself. That theoretical choice is the conceptual root of the overlap this review examines, because an instrument built in the Barkley tradition will, by design, contain motivation-flavored content.

The Rise of Rating-Based EF, and What It Actually Measures

Lab EF tasks (the Stroop test, card sorting, digit span) are precise but artificial, and clinicians noticed they often failed to predict how a person actually functioned in daily life. That gap drove the creation of rating-based EF measures: self-report or informant questionnaires such as the BRIEF-A, the BDEFS (Barkley's scale), and the ADEXI, which ask people to describe their everyday memory, focus, organization, and follow-through. C2A measures all of its EF this way. It runs no lab tasks.

Here is the pivotal empirical fact. Performance-based EF tasks and rating-based EF questionnaires barely agree: across 20 studies the median correlation is only about r = .19 (Toplak, West and Stanovich, 2013), meaning they share roughly three to four percent of their variance. Toplak and colleagues argue they measure genuinely different things: tasks measure the efficiency of a cognitive process under optimal conditions, while ratings measure success at goal pursuit in typical daily life. So a rating-based EF score is not a weak proxy for cognitive capacity. It is a measure of something else.

What is that something else? The evidence points squarely at personality and affect. This is the hinge of the whole review, so it is worth stating carefully.

The central finding: self-report EF scores track personality, not measured cognition. Buchanan (2016) ran three well-powered studies and found that self-reported EF problems correlated with neuroticism and (low) conscientiousness at medium-to-large size, but did not correlate with performance on standard EF tasks. Meltzer et al. (2017) found that worry and anxiety were tied to all nine BRIEF-A scales, and concluded the BRIEF-A "only minimally taps objective EF." Soto and Kofler et al. (2020) found EF ratings tracked the underlying construct far worse (about r = .30) than academic ratings tracked academic tests (about r = .63). The convergent conclusion across these studies: a self-report EF questionnaire behaves, statistically, more like a personality and mood inventory than like a cognitive test.

Motivation (ARC) and Rating-Survey EF: How Deep Is the Overlap?

We now reach the question this section exists to answer in depth. If self-report EF is substantially personality and affect, and if SDT motivation is also assessed by self-report and also correlates with personality and affect, then how much do our EF dimensions and our motivation dimensions actually overlap? The honest answer has three parts: the overlap is real and converges from several directions; one widely used EF instrument literally embeds a motivation subscale; and yet the single most direct test has never been published.

First, the load-bearing honesty point. There is no published study directly correlating a standard self-report EF battery (BRIEF-A, BDEFS, ADEXI) with a validated SDT autonomous-motivation scale. The cleanest possible number for "how much do rating-EF and ARC-motivation overlap?" does not exist in the literature. Anyone who claims a precise figure is guessing. We therefore build the case indirectly, from convergent evidence, and we label it as such.

Second, the overlap is established by triangulation. Four independent bridges connect the two construct families:

  • Shared personality variance. Self-report EF loads on conscientiousness and neuroticism (Buchanan 2016; Meltzer 2017). Autonomous motivation and need satisfaction also correlate with those same traits. Two measures that both reduce partly to conscientiousness will correlate with each other through that shared core, even if neither "causes" the other.
  • An embedded motivation subscale. Barkley's BDEFS, built on the EF-as-self-regulation theory, contains a Self-Motivation subscale. In a two-sample study (Knouse, Feldman and Blevins 2014; N = 250 and N = 229), that Self-Motivation dimension was the EF dimension most consistently and uniquely related to college grades after controlling for prior GPA, with part of that link running through students' grade goals (though grade goals did not fully account for it). This is not overlap by inference; it is a motivation construct sitting inside an EF instrument and doing the predictive work.
  • The procrastination bridge. Procrastination is theorized as a self-regulatory and motivational failure, not a cognitive-capacity deficit (Steel 2007, meta-analysis of 691 correlations: the strongest predictors are task aversiveness, low self-efficacy, impulsiveness, and low conscientiousness). Self-report EF predicts procrastination too (Rabin 2011: the BRIEF-A Initiate scale among others). So a behavior the motivation literature claims and a behavior the EF-rating literature claims are the same behavior.
  • The affect bridge. The BRIEF emotional-regulation index measures perceived control over distress, which is close to the definition of (low) neuroticism, the negative-affect trait. Need frustration in SDT also produces negative affect. Both families touch the same affective core.

Third, mechanism. When a single person answers an EF questionnaire and a motivation questionnaire in the same sitting, the two scores share common-method variance: a portion of their correlation comes from how they were measured, not what was measured. Across 70 multitrait-multimethod studies, method variance averaged about 26 percent of the variance in a typical self-report measure (Cote and Buckley 1987, as reported in Podsakoff et al. 2003). That is the size of artifact you can get for free, before any real relationship. For an all-self-report instrument like C2A, this is not a footnote; it is the reason internal correlations among our own dimensions cannot be taken at face value.

What this would mean for reading such a profile. If a profile showed a student low on applied-EF signals and also low on autonomous motivation, that pattern would be partly real and partly an artifact of measuring both by the same self-report method, filtered through the same underlying conscientiousness and mood. The two scores would not be independent confirmations of each other. They should be read as overlapping views of one self-perception, not as two separate instruments agreeing. This is a key design constraint for whatever goes into Coach View.

The Overlap, Dimension by Dimension

Because Part 5 profiles each EF dimension individually, it is worth specifying, for each one, exactly where the motivation overlap sits and whether it is directly measured or inferred. The honest pattern: the overlap is strongest and best-evidenced for the applied "self-regulation" dimensions (task initiation, emotional regulation, sustained attention) and thinnest for the core cognitive ones, though even those are touched through stress.

Method Variance, the Jangle Fallacy, and Why Naming Matters

Two final concepts frame how we should talk about all of this. The jangle fallacy is the error of assuming two differently named measures assess two different things when they may assess one. "Self-report executive function," "conscientiousness," and "self-discipline" may be, to a meaningful degree, three names for one underlying self-perception. The grit literature is the cautionary tale: grit correlates with conscientiousness at about .84 and adds almost nothing beyond it (Crede et al. 2017), a renamed construct that promised more than it delivered.

The practical upshot for the Coach View design is not that the approach is wrong. SDT and EF are both robust, well-validated traditions, and a rating-based instrument that captures self-perceived daily functioning genuinely predicts real-life outcomes better than a lab test does. The upshot is about interpretation and honesty: the EF and motivation dimensions we are considering are overlapping self-perceptions measured by a shared method, not independent instruments. That recognition shapes every recommendation in Part 6, and it is the reason we refuse to cite our own internal correlations as proof, since those correlations sit inside exactly the method-variance and part-whole overlap this section describes.

The Building Blocks: Sixteen Pieces in Four Layers

The proposed Coach View would be built from sixteen candidate constructs stacked in four layers. Think of it like a car: an engine, the daily driving habits, the reason you are driving, and the weather you are driving through.

Core EF (the engine)Working memory, inhibition, cognitive flexibilityThe raw mental machinery under the hood

Core EF: The Engine

Three raw cognitive abilities. These are the hardest to change and, on their own, the weakest predictors of real-life success. They matter mostly as the machinery the applied skills run on. We measure them by self-report questionnaire (how a person describes their own memory, focus, and flexibility), which is the right instinct for capturing daily functioning, with the caveats Part 1 lays out.

Working memory

Your mind's whiteboard. How much you can hold and juggle in your head while working on something. Erase it too fast and instructions and half-finished steps slip away.

Inhibition

Your mind's brakes. The ability to pause before acting, resist a distraction, and not blurt the first thing that comes up.

Cognitive flexibility

Your mental gear-shift. Switching strategy, seeing a problem a new way, adapting when the plan stops working. This is the engine part most damaged by stress and trauma.

Applied EF: Daily Driving

Seven skills that describe how the engine shows up in an actual week. This is the layer coaches care about most, because it is where real change happens. It is also the layer our system does not currently measure with any questionnaire.

Time management

Reading the clock the way it actually behaves for you. Estimating how long things take and getting the right thing done at the right time.

Organization

Keeping your stuff and information where you can find them, so the system holds even when your memory does not.

Planning / prioritization

Mapping the route before you drive. Breaking a big goal into ordered steps and deciding what matters most right now.

Task initiation

Getting off the starting line. Beginning without waiting for last-minute panic, even when you know what to do.

Self-monitoring

Watching yourself work as you go. Noticing whether your approach is working and catching your own mistakes.

Sustained attention

Keeping your beam on one thing over time without your focus drifting off.

Emotional regulation

Handling the heat of a hard task. Staying steady when something is frustrating instead of boiling over or shutting down.

Motivation: The Why

Two layers answer a different question than EF. Not "can the person do it?" but "do they want to, and why?" This turns out to be the layer that decides whether anything sticks.

ARC psychological needs (autonomy, relatedness, competence)

Three needs every person has. Autonomy is feeling like the driver, not the passenger. Relatedness is feeling seen and connected, that someone is in your corner. Competence is feeling capable and improving. When these are fed, motivation grows on its own. When they are starved, it collapses.

GMS motivation type (the quality of the why)

Not how much motivation, but what kind. Intrinsic: "I find this genuinely worthwhile, or it connects to what I want for my family." External regulation: "I'm only doing this because someone makes me." Amotivation: "I don't see the point and I've checked out." The type predicts durability; the amount does not.

Coaching Context: The Weather

Four conditions that are not skills, but change how every skill above behaves. A low score can be the weather, not the person. Reading these wrong is how a coach mistakes a hard week for a fixed limit.

Strengths-first framing

Which lens the coach holds up before saying a word. Lead with what works, not what is broken. The lens quietly shapes what the coach expects, and expectations have a way of coming true.

Financial precarity

Money stress runs a background program that never closes, taxing the exact mental functions we measure. A low score may be a temporary tax, not a ceiling.

Trauma history

Trauma retunes the brain toward "react first," shrinking the planning part and amplifying the alarm. The job is to sequence coaching, not to diagnose or treat.

Culture

The same three needs are universal but show up differently across backgrounds. What makes a student feel understood matters more than the coach's credentials or self-rating.

Part 3 · What Actually Predicts

Feeds the decision: this part sets each candidate's Evidence score in Part 6. How strongly the literature ties a construct to real outcomes is what moves that column.

What Our Everyday Measure Captures

So what? C2A measures executive function as everyday self-reported behavior, how a person describes running their actual week. Part 1 covered why that method exists and how it differs from lab testing; here the point is simpler. This is the layer tied to real-life outcomes, which makes it a defensible foundation for coaching.

A self-report rating captures success at getting things done in a messy real life full of time pressure, stakes, and competing goals. That is precisely what coaching tries to move, and it predicts grades, work, and daily functioning, with one important catch covered two sections down.

The evidence that everyday ratings track real-life impairment
  • Self-rated EF predicted impairment on all 11 occupational measures and carried the predictive weight when entered alongside other measures (Barkley and Murphy, 2010; n = 352, adults with ADHD). High
  • Performance and rating measures assess "different underlying mental constructs, the efficiency of cognitive abilities and success in goal pursuit," correlating only weakly (Toplak, West and Stanovich, 2013; the detail is in Part 1). High

Why it matters to us. A coaching profile built on how someone actually runs their week is measuring the layer tied to life outcomes. That is a defensible foundation.

Ecological Validity: Why Everyday Ratings Predict Real Outcomes

So what? "Ecological validity" just means a measure looks and behaves like real life, so it tracks real outcomes. The everyday self-report measure we use carries genuine predictive weight for grades, work, and quality of life, which is the core reason the design is sound.

The evidence is strongest for the metacognition and organization facets: how a person reports managing and monitoring their own work uniquely forecasts academic and quality-of-life outcomes.

The incremental-validity evidence
  • The everyday metacognition rating added 15.1 percent unique quality-of-life variance beyond diagnosis; organization-in-time added another 10.8 percent (Grinblat and Rosenblum, 2025). Moderate
  • Self-reported daily EF explained 36 percent of variance in academic progress (Dijkhuis et al., 2020). Moderate
  • An everyday EF composite plus a naturalistic task explained 36 percent of variance in daily functioning (Bayard et al., 2026). Moderate

Why it matters to us. Measuring EF as everyday behavior is the right design instinct, and the workforce field reached the same conclusion independently by defining durable skills as observable behaviors.

An Honest Caveat: Looks Like Real Life Is Not the Same as Predicts Real Life

So what? Before we lean too hard on how well our ratings predict outcomes, there is a catch that protects our credibility if we name it first.

A lot of the evidence that ratings win is confounded. If you measure the skill with a questionnaire and the outcome with a questionnaire, at the same time, from the same person, they will look strongly linked partly because of how you measured, not what you measured. Resembling real life is not the same as forecasting it. This caveat applies squarely to us: because every C2A score is a self-report rating, our core EF, applied signals, needs, and motivation all share the same method, so correlations among them are inflated by that shared method before any true signal is counted.

Verisimilitude (a test that looks like real life) is not veridicality (a test that predicts real life). When outcomes are measured a different way, lab tests can show higher cross-method predictive validity. (Suchy et al., 2024) High

The cleanest proof of this artifact. In one large meta-analysis, intrinsic motivation correlated with self-reported achievement at 0.32 but with objective achievement at only 0.13 (Howard et al., 2021). Same construct, same studies; the only difference is how the outcome was measured. The self-report version looks more than twice as strong. This is exactly why we do not cite our own pilot numbers: our signals and several outcomes are scored from overlapping items, so same-method inflation plus the part-whole overlap would make any internal advantage look bigger than it is.

Why Motivation Decides Durability

So what? You can teach a skill perfectly and watch it vanish in a month. Whether it lasts depends less on the teaching and more on why the person is doing it.

When motivation comes from inside (the person finds the work worthwhile or tied to their own values), the skill persists. When it comes from outside pressure, you get short-term compliance that decays the moment the pressure lifts. The reason is mechanical: acting under pressure means one part of you constantly overriding another, which is draining and unsustainable.

Skills adopted under controlled motivation decay; controlled regulation is "vitality-draining" because one part of the self suppresses another, making the effort itself unsustainable. (Ryan and Deci, 2020) High
The durability and sequencing evidence
  • Autonomous motivation to transfer predicted skill retention weeks after training; controlled motivation did not (Gegenfurtner, 2013). Moderate
  • Needs satisfaction drives autonomous motivation over time in a longitudinal mediation model (Garn, Morin and Lonsdale, 2019; n = 1,789). High
  • Amotivation collapses engagement first: it is the strongest negative correlate of objective achievement at r = -.21 (Howard et al., 2021). High
  • Sequencing is a genuine gap. No trial compares motivation-first versus EF-first. Evidence leans toward weaving them together: the largest coaching effect (d = 1.10 for self-regulation) came from a protocol that integrated motivation support into structured EF coaching from the start (Field et al., 2013). Moderate

Why it matters to us. Motivation type is the moderator that decides whether any coached EF skill survives. It is the durability engine of the whole product.

Three honest limits on this map, stated up front:

1. No single published study links the whole chain. There is no model that runs needs to motivation to EF-in-use to durable skills with one set of path numbers. So we present the chain link by link, each link from its own best study, and we mark the links nobody has quantified.

2. The adult EF-to-work number is a genuine gap. The cleanest EF-to-achievement meta-analysis is on children aged 6 to 12. For adults we report the honest neighbors (general mental ability, effort regulation) and label them as such.

3. "Durable skills" as a named construct is barely studied directly. The literature uses proxies: grades, job performance, quality of work, persistence. We use those proxies and say so.

Shared vs Unique Variance: Who Adds What

So what? The real question for a layered product is not "does each piece correlate with outcomes?" but "does each piece add something the others miss?" That is unique variance, and it is the whole case for measuring more than one layer.

For a layered product like ours, the question is whether EF, needs, and motivation each add something the others miss. The published evidence on unique contribution is clearest for the everyday self-report measures we use.

  • Everyday EF ratings carry real predictive weight. Self-rated daily EF explained 36 percent of variance in academic progress (Dijkhuis 2020), and an everyday EF composite explained 36 percent of daily-functioning variance (Bayard 2026).
  • Within EF, the metacognition and time-organization facets carry distinct weight. Metacognition added 15.1 percent unique quality-of-life variance, and time-organization added a further 10.8 percent, beyond diagnosis (Grinblat 2025).
  • Motivation and EF are distinct but overlapping layers. They are not redundant, but because we measure both by self-report they share method variance, so their unique contributions cannot be cleanly separated from our own data (see the warning below).

The shared-variance warning, with the cleanest number. A large share of any "ratings predict outcomes" result is shared method variance, not true signal. The Howard 2021 contrast is the proof: intrinsic motivation to self-reported achievement is 0.32, but to objective achievement only 0.13. The 0.19 gap is roughly the size of the method artifact. When our own engine scores a signal and an outcome from overlapping items, we are sitting inside that same gap, which is why internal predictive numbers are an upper bound only.

What we cannot yet partition. No published study cleanly decomposes how much unique variance ARC needs, motivation type, and applied EF each contribute to a single durable-skill outcome, controlling for the others. That joint variance decomposition is a real open question, and a fair thing for our own validation work to target, on a clean non-overlapping outcome.

Profile Cards

Each candidate construct as a coach-facing card: what it is, whether and how our current assessment measures it today (verified against our files), what a coach can do, the durable-skill link, and the evidence tier. A "scored" badge means we can already measure it; "not scored" means it would have to be built if Coach View is to include it.

Core EF

A reminder that carries through every card below: C2A measures all of these by self-report questionnaire, not by lab task. A "scored" badge means we have a validated rating scale for it, not that we run a cognitive test. As Part 1 and Part 3 explain, that puts every C2A score, core and applied alike, on the rating side of the measurement divide, where it shares variance with personality and mood and behaves statistically more like conscientiousness than like a lab ability test.

Working memory scored High

How we measure it: ADEXI inventory, 9 items, 5-point scale (verified). Coach move: do not try to train it, offload it. Checklists, written plans, reminders, templates. Durable-skill link: best engine proxy for Communication and Critical Thinking. Note: the "cannot be trained, compensate instead" finding rests on three meta-analyses (combined n > 3,000; Sala 2019), the best-supported single recommendation in the EF set.

Inhibition scored High / Moderate

How we measure it: ADEXI inventory, 5 items, 5-point scale (verified). Coach move: remove triggers plus pause-and-plan practice. Durable-skill link: primary EF for Empathy and relationship skills. Caveat: inhibition is not the same as emotional control; do not treat one as a proxy for the other.

Cognitive flexibility scored High (construct) / Moderate (training)

How we measure it: a separate in-house 4-item scale (C2A-CF), not ADEXI (verified). Coach move: practice in real scenarios, not brain-training games. Durable-skill link: primary EF for Creative Problem Solving, Adaptability, and Resilience. Most vulnerable to stress and trauma.

File flag: our data carries a provenance conflict on this scale. The definition says it is ThriveIEP-authored and unpublished; the norms registry attributes its reference to Holst and Thorell (2018). Reconcile before any external claim about the scale's origin.

Applied EF (none currently scored)

Read this first: none of the seven applied-EF skills are scored by any C2A questionnaire (verified). There is no instrument, item set, or scale for them in our files; they are coaching vocabulary we use, not signals we measure. The evidence below is for what coaches can do, not for what we currently assess. If we did build rating scales for them, those scores would sit on the same self-report, conscientiousness-flavored footing as our existing EF scores (see Part 1).

Time management not scored High

Full toolkit works: planners, timers, time-estimation practice. Top-priority dimension to surface. Group training held gains at 3 months (Lidstrom Holmqvist 2026).

Organization not scored High / Moderate

Compensate with systems and templates first, then strengthen. Bundled with time management in the strongest programs (Langberg 2020).

Planning / prioritization not scored Moderate

Goal decomposition and if-then plans; teach explicitly. Direct metacognition instruction produced large effects, d = 0.64 to 0.87 (Zepeda 2015).

Task initiation not scored Moderate

Treat procrastination as an EF failure, not a character flaw. Behavioral-activation RCT, d = 0.49 (Stralin 2025).

Self-monitoring not scored Moderate

Build "is my approach working?" check-ins into the cycle. Gains decayed at 3 months without boosters (Dawson 2025). Weakest of the recently upgraded three.

Sustained attention not scored Low / Moderate

Design the focus environment; use physical activity. Mostly accommodate-and-buffer. Symptoms respond to therapy but trainable capacity does not.

Emotional regulation not scored Moderate

Coach move: frame as task-related frustration tolerance, an EF skill, not therapy. Often improves on its own when time and organization stabilize. Guardrail: shame spirals, shutdown, or dread with a trauma origin are therapy territory; the report needs clear handoff triggers. 89 percent of ADHD coaches have no mental-health training (Sibley 2026).

Integrity flag from our archive

An earlier-cited trial (Haugan 2022) was found on full-text check to be a negative study with mis-stated effect sizes. Do not cite it as evidence that emotional regulation is trainable.

Motivation

ARC needs: autonomy, relatedness, competence scored High

How we measure it: BMPN-18 inventory, 18 items, six subscales of 3 items each, 5-point scale (verified). Coach moves: offer real choices and rationales (autonomy); build genuine connection before pushing on performance (relatedness); engineer early wins (competence). Links: competence predicts work task performance hardest (.40); relatedness drives the interpersonal cluster; autonomy drives Initiative and Curiosity. File note: BMPN norms are self-flagged as estimated, to be replaced by pilot norms.

GMS motivation type: intrinsic, external, amotivation scored High (mechanism)

How we measure it: GMS-Bookends, 10 items, three subscales, 5-point scale, combined into an autonomy index (verified; endpoints-only subset, so it does not measure the middle regulation types). Coach move: diagnose the type, not just the amount. For external regulation, connect the task to the person's own values. For amotivation, reduce frustration and rebuild competence before any skill push. Link: the moderator that decides whether every other coached skill is retained.

Coaching Context

Strengths-first framing not scored Moderate

A report banding and copy convention (Start Here / Growing / Strength), not an instrument (verified). Lead with strengths, then frame gaps as growing edges. The combined approach beats strengths-only (van Woerkom 2016).

Financial precarity not scored Moderate

Run a bandwidth check before reading EF scores; stabilize resources first. Stress impairs flexibility and working memory (Shields 2016), but the "13 IQ points" claim has not replicated reliably (de Bruijn 2021). Treat low scores as a possible state, not a ceiling.

Trauma history not scored Moderate / Low

Stabilize before optimizing; teach grounding; provide written summaries; expect non-linear progress. Bright-line referral: suicidality, acute crisis, or flashbacks go to clinical care. The coaching-vs-clinical boundary is practitioner consensus, not tested in trials.

Culture partial Moderate / High

We score cultural intelligence (SFCQ, 10 items), the respondent's cross-cultural skill, not the student's cultural background. Lead with relatedness; be group-specific (about 4x more effective). What the student perceives matters more than the coach's self-rating (Soto 2018).

Part 6 · The Decision

How We Scored Each Candidate for Inclusion

This is the part the whole report exists to serve: a construct-by-construct judgment on what belongs in Coach View. We rate each of the sixteen candidates on three things, then read the pattern. The decision is the point; everything before this is the evidence feeding it.

Each candidate gets a 1-to-5 rating on three questions, and the three average into a composite. We draw an inclusion cutline at 3.0: at or above, the construct earns a place in the first version of Coach View; below, it waits.

  • Evidence (1-5). How well does the published literature support this as a real predictor of durable-skill outcomes or a sound coaching target? Anchored to the tiers in Parts 3 to 5: High evidence maps to 4 to 5, Moderate to 3, Low or consensus to 1 to 2.
  • Feasibility (1-5). Can we implement its intended role well? A construct we already score is a 5; one with no instrument that must be built is a 2; one we can only measure sideways, or that carries clinical-scope risk, is lower.
  • Non-redundancy (1-5). Does it add unique signal, given the overlap problem from Part 1? A construct that mostly duplicates another (heavy shared method or motivation variance) scores low; a distinct layer scores high.

Read the numbers as structured judgment, not measurement. These are our ratings, anchored to the evidence tiers, not a computed metric from data. A 3.7 is not more precise than "strong on two of three counts." The score exists to make the reasoning visible and comparable across sixteen constructs, and to force an explicit cutline, not to manufacture false precision. The role column matters as much as the number: some constructs belong in Coach View as a measured score, others only as a context flag or a framing rule.

What the Scorecard Says

Four groups fall out of the ranking, and each carries a different action.

The clear core: build Coach View around motivation and the three scored core-EF dimensions

ARC needs and motivation type top the list (4.7 each) because they have the strongest evidence, are already measured, and add the most unique signal. Motivation type is the natural anchor: it is the durability switch that decides whether any other coached skill survives. Working memory, inhibition, and cognitive flexibility round out a measurable v1 we can ship today. Cognitive flexibility carries one homework item: reconcile the provenance conflict in our files before any external claim.

Non-score layers that earn their place: strengths-first framing, financial precarity, trauma, culture

These score well not as student scores but in their intended roles. Strengths-first (4.3) is a framing rule, cheap to implement and pure upside. Financial precarity (4.0) and trauma (3.3) belong as context flags that tell a coach a low score may be a temporary state, not a ceiling; trauma also needs referral bright-lines. Culture (3.3) belongs as practice guidance, with an honest note that we measure cross-cultural skill, not a student's background. Including these as anything other than their proper role would be a mistake the scores would otherwise hide.

The build-first applied skill: time management, then planning

Time management has the best evidence of any applied-EF skill (RCT support, most-taught in the field) but the lowest feasibility, because we have no instrument for it. It clears the cutline (3.3) on evidence strength alone, making it the top candidate to build a questionnaire for. Planning sits exactly on the line (3.0) as the second build priority.

The defers, and why the math is honest about them

Organization, task initiation, self-monitoring, emotional regulation, and sustained attention fall below the cutline, and the reasons are instructive. Task initiation has strong evidence but is dragged down by redundancy: it overlaps motivation and procrastination so heavily that the motivation layer may already capture it (Part 1). Emotional regulation is better handled as a guardrail flag than a score, given the clinical-scope risk. Sustained attention is last because the evidence that it is a trainable capacity is weak. None of these are "bad" constructs; they are simply lower-yield for a first version than the layers above them.

Design Recommendations

Given that scorecard, here is how Coach View should be built, ordered by how much each protects the product and our credibility.

Critical Do not show the applied-EF skills as if we measure them

The seven everyday-skill ideas are exactly what coaches most want to see, which is the trap. We score none of them today. In v1, either omit them or present them explicitly as coaching language, never as measured scores. Build time management and planning first if we want them as real signals. Evidence: verified directly from our files.

Critical Never cite our own pilot's prediction numbers as proof of a construct's value

The part-whole overlap plus same-method inflation makes any internal predictive edge an upper bound. The published proof of that inflation is the 0.32-vs-0.13 self-report gap (Howard 2021). When deciding what to include, lean on the external literature, not our internal correlations. Evidence: Suchy 2024; Howard 2021.

High Anchor the profile on motivation type, with ARC needs as the engine behind it

Motivation type gates durability and scored highest, so it should organize the whole view. ARC needs explain how to move it. Both are measurable today. Evidence: Ryan and Deci 2020; Bureau 2022.

High Treat context as flags, not scores, and frame everything strengths-first

Financial precarity and trauma should appear as state-vs-trait flags with referral bright-lines, not as numbers about the student. Strengths-first framing should wrap the entire profile. These are the cheapest, highest-integrity additions on the board. Evidence: Shields 2016; van Woerkom 2016.

Moderate Before claiming any construct predicts durable skills, validate on a clean outcome

Every "this belongs because it predicts outcomes" claim needs an outcome measured separately from the items that feed it. Until then, inclusion rests on external evidence, not internal prediction. Evidence: the variance-decomposition gap in Part 4.

How Coach View Should Behave in Practice

If the constructs above are included, these are the usage principles that follow from the evidence, the behavior the product should encode.

1. Read motivation type before anything else

It gates everything. A student running on external pressure or amotivation will not retain coached skills, so skill work is wasted until the why shifts. Evidence: Ryan and Deci 2020; Howard 2021.

2. Feed needs to build motivation, do not lecture about it

Offer choice (autonomy), build connection (relatedness), engineer early wins (competence). The needs-to-motivation link is the strongest in the model (.44 to .58). Evidence: Bureau 2022; Van den Broeck 2016.

3. For EF, compensate first, strengthen second

Working memory cannot be trained, so offload it. Reserve strengthening for the dimensions that respond, and only once motivation supports the effort. Evidence: Sala 2019; Field 2013.

4. Check the weather before reading the scores

Financial stress and trauma can masquerade as low EF. Run a bandwidth check and stabilize conditions before treating a low score as a ceiling. Evidence: Shields 2016; McEwen 2015.

5. Integrate, do not sequence rigidly

No trial proves motivation-first or EF-first is better. The biggest coaching effect wove the two together from the start (d = 1.10). Evidence: Field 2013.

Open Decisions for Leadership

The scorecard makes a recommendation; these are the calls only you can make.

  • Where to draw the cutline. We set it at 3.0. Moving it to 3.3 drops time management, culture, trauma, and planning out of v1; moving it to 2.5 pulls organization, task initiation, and self-monitoring back in. The four constructs clustered right at 3.0 to 3.3 are the real decision.
  • Whether to fund the applied-EF instruments. Time management and planning have the evidence but no questionnaire. Building them is the single biggest lever for making Coach View measure what coaches actually want.
  • How aggressively to weight feasibility. Our scoring treats "we can measure it today" as worth a lot. If the goal is the most evidence-complete profile rather than the fastest, evidence should outweigh feasibility and several deferred applied-EF skills move up.
  • Whether task initiation is redundant with motivation. The literature suggests the motivation layer may already capture it. Confirming this would settle whether it is worth a separate instrument.

Gaps and Limitations

Where this briefing is thin, and where we should resist over-claiming.

  • The measurement gap is the big one. Our current assessment can score only five of the sixteen candidate constructs. Coach View is still a proposal, so deciding which of the other eleven are worth building is the core open question, not a description of something we already ship.
  • No published study links the full chain. Needs to motivation to EF-use to durable skills has never been estimated as one model. We assembled it link by link; the joins are inference, not measurement.
  • The adult EF-to-work number does not exist. The clean EF-to-achievement meta-analysis is on children. For adults we used general mental ability and effort-regulation proxies and labeled them.
  • "Durable skills" as a named construct is barely studied. The literature predicts grades, job performance, and persistence; the durable-skills label itself is largely untested, which is both our opportunity and the reason our evidence is indirect.
  • Within-core-EF correlations are not cleanly published, and the three-factor EF model fails to replicate consistently (Karr 2018).
  • The applied-EF coaching evidence rides on ADHD and disability populations. Controlled research in general college and working-adult groups is sparse: one scoping review found only 28 usable studies from 8,237 screened.
  • Our EF and motivation scores are not independent measures. Because every C2A signal is self-report, EF and motivation share method, personality, and mood variance; a low-EF, low-motivation pattern is partly one self-perception seen twice, not two instruments agreeing. The direct EF-vs-SDT-motivation overlap has never been measured (see Part 1).
  • Our internal numbers are off-limits as proof. Part-whole overlap plus same-method inflation; use them only to generate hypotheses.
  • Two file issues to fix: the cognitive-flexibility provenance conflict, and the estimated (not yet validated) norms on BMPN and SFCQ.
  • A few exact decimals need a final source check before any are quoted as precise figures in external materials (some per-cell SDT values were read from preprint or image-PDF tables).
