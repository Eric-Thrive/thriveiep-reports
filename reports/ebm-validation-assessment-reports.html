Can We Build a Safety Net for AI-Generated Assessment Reports?

Research Brief / 51 sources across 7 databases / March 30, 2026

  • No one has done this before. Applying constraint-based validation to AI-generated psychometric assessment reports is genuinely novel. The grant landscape is empty.
  • The building blocks are ready. Energy-based models, probabilistic soft logic, and neuro-symbolic verification are mature research tools. Clinical teams are already validating AI reports with transferable methods.
  • Federal funding is wide open. SBIR programs are about to reopen after a 6-month pause. ED/IES explicitly funds AI assessment tools for students with disabilities. A proposal prepared now gets a 4-8 week head start.

What problem are we solving?

C2A's DDx engine (Differential Diagnosis engine, a pattern-matching system) generates coaching reports from 12 skill dimensions. Each skill gets its own interpretation. But nobody checks whether the combined narrative makes sense across all 12 skills. A student might get contradictory advice, or a critical discrepancy between skills might go unmentioned.

What did we find?

We reviewed 51 sources across AI/ML, education, clinical medicine, and federal funding. Three research threads converge: energy-based models can score whole documents for internal consistency (146-1,758 citations). Neuro-symbolic systems that can veto bad outputs show the strongest clinical results (2026 systematic review). And clinical teams have already built practical validation rubrics for AI-generated reports.

Is it fundable?

Highly. The ED/IES SBIR Phase IB program (fit score: 5 out of 5) is designed exactly for this: adding a new AI component to an existing evidence-based education technology product. Award size: $250K for 9 months, with $1M Phase II follow-on. The SBIR reauthorization bill passed both chambers of Congress in March 2026 and is awaiting presidential signature.

What should we do?

Draft the ED/IES SBIR Phase IB proposal now. Build a lightweight prototype (pairwise skill constraints using Probabilistic Soft Logic) in parallel to generate preliminary data. The practical framing is "neuro-symbolic constraint validation" (what we build) with "energy-based theoretical grounding" (what makes it novel to grant reviewers).

Section 2 of 11

Three Research Threads That Converge on Our Problem

Thread 1: Energy-Based Text Validation

MODERATE evidence

An energy-based model (EBM) assigns a single score to any input-output pair. Low score means "this output is compatible with this input." High score means "something is off." Yann LeCun defined this framework in 2006 (1,758 citations). In 2020, Deng et al. showed that scoring entire documents this way beats scoring one word at a time. In 2022, Kumar et al. (MuCoLa) proved you can compose multiple constraints into one energy function.

For C2A: each of 12 skill dimensions contributes one energy term. The combined energy checks whether the full narrative is internally consistent. If Working Memory scores high but Processing Speed scores low, the report must mention that discrepancy, or the energy goes up.

Thread 2: Neuro-Symbolic Verification

HIGH evidence (PRISMA systematic review)

"Neuro-symbolic" means combining a neural network (the LLM that writes the report) with a symbolic system (a logic engine that checks the rules). Gorenshtein et al. (2026) reviewed all published approaches in clinical medicine. Their key finding: "Gains increase with symbolic authority. The strongest results come from iterative validation where the symbolic layer can veto outputs."

Translation: Letting the constraint checker reject bad reports (not just flag them) produces the best outcomes. C2A's DDx engine already works this way. It is deterministic and rule-based. Adding a constraint validation layer extends this architecture naturally.

Thread 3: Clinical Report Validation Methods

LOW evidence (proof-of-concept studies, N=6-29)

All 7 clinical papers are from 2025-2026. This field is moving fast. Key methods: (1) Binary codebook decomposition, where you break a report into yes/no checkable items, achieves Cohen's kappa of 0.80 for AI psychiatric reports. (2) A 4-criterion rubric for LLM-generated clinical letters shows 3.5% hallucination rate and 86.2% coding accuracy. (3) "AI sparring," where a second AI model validates the first, is emerging as a governance pattern.

These methods transfer directly to DDx report validation. Break each report into verifiable binary items: "Did the report correctly reference the accommodation category? Y/N." "Does the psychometric score in the narrative match the source data? Y/N."
Section 3 of 11

Why "Just Ask the AI to Check Itself" Is Not Enough

Self-Consistency Has a Mathematical Ceiling

HIGH evidence (Nature, 493 citations)

The most common approach to checking AI outputs is "self-consistency": generate the same answer multiple times and see if they agree. Farquhar et al. (2024, Nature) introduced semantic entropy, which improves on this by measuring uncertainty at the level of meaning, not just word choice. But Xue et al. (2025) proved mathematically that self-consistency approaches hit a performance ceiling near supervised oracle levels.

This is a key grant argument: self-checking is not just imperfect, it has a provable upper bound. An external validation layer using structural constraints can exceed that bound because it checks things the LLM literally cannot see about its own output.

"Smooth Falsehoods" Are the Real Threat

VERY LOW evidence (preprint, 0 citations)

Miya (2025) introduced the concept of "smooth falsehoods" in the Eidoku paper: statements that are highly probable (they sound right) but are structurally disconnected from the ground truth. Probability-based verifiers cannot detect these because the statement is probable. Only structural constraint checking can.

In C2A terms: a report that sounds clinically plausible but recommends Band-5 accommodations for a student scoring in Band 1. The language is smooth and confident. But the structure is wrong. An energy function or constraint solver catches this; self-consistency does not.

What is PSL?

Probabilistic Soft Logic (PSL) uses weighted logic rules with continuous truth values between 0 and 1, instead of strict true/false. This matters for assessment reports because skill scores fall on a spectrum, not in binary categories. A rule like "IF Working Memory is high AND Processing Speed is low, THEN the report should mention this discrepancy" can have a weight of 0.9, meaning it is strongly preferred but not absolutely required.

Recommendation

START HERE PSL rules for the grant proposal and MVP. AUGMENT with Z3 solver for hard safety constraints (e.g., "never recommend clinical interventions beyond coaching scope"). COMPARE against current DDx template matching and LLM self-check as baselines.

Section 5 of 11

Is This Actually Novel?

  • Yes, with qualification. No published work applies energy-based models, PSL, or neuro-symbolic constraint satisfaction to multi-dimensional psychometric profile narrative validation.

What is the closest existing work?

  • Clinical ICD-10 coding (Yu, 2025): Neuro-symbolic verification for structured diagnostic codes. Close in method, but codes are discrete labels, not narratives.
  • Psychiatric documentation (Gulegen et al., 2026): Binary codebook validation for AI-generated reports. Close in domain, but no energy-based or constraint-satisfaction approach.
  • WISC-R automated analysis (Replogle & Eicke, 1985): The earliest precedent for automated multi-subtest battery interpretation. No validation layer. Forty years old.
  • MuCoLa constrained generation (Kumar et al., 2022): Constraint composition for text generation. General-purpose, not assessment-specific.

What kind of contribution is this?

This is an engineering application of known techniques (EBMs, PSL, constrained decoding) to a new domain (psychometric assessment report validation). It is publishable as a novel application paper at venues like AIED, LAK, EDM, or the AAAI Special Track on AI for Social Impact. It is defensible as an SBIR Phase I feasibility study. It is NOT a fundamental algorithmic contribution.

What would a research collaborator need to see?

  1. A formal specification of the constraint space (12 dimensions × 4-5 bands × cross-skill rules)
  2. A proof-of-concept PSL rule set running on real C2A student profiles
  3. Comparison against the current DDx template matching baseline
  4. Cohen's kappa or similar inter-rater reliability metric for validated vs. unvalidated reports

Section 7 of 11

Architecture: How Complex Is This Really?

The Math Is Manageable

12 skill dimensions × 5 interpretive bands = 60 possible single-dimension states. Pairwise constraints: C(12,2) = 66 pairs. With 4-5 rules per pair, that is roughly 300 pairwise rules. PSL (Bach et al., 2015) demonstrated inference on millions of ground atoms. 300 rules is trivial by comparison.

Full 12-dimensional joint constraints are tractable because most skill dimensions are approximately conditionally independent given the coaching lens (Executive Function, SDT, Motivation, Resilience). Each lens groups 3 skills that interact strongly; cross-lens interactions are weaker.

Minimum Viable Version

START with pairwise constraints only (66 pairs). This captures the most important discrepancies, like high Working Memory with low Processing Speed. THEN add coaching-lens-level constraints (4 groups of 3 skills) for cross-group coherence.

Minimum Viable Research Scope: A 9-Month Phase I

Aim 1: Constraint Space Formalization (Months 1-3)

  • Formalize 12 skill dimensions × 4-5 interpretive bands as a constraint graph
  • Enumerate cross-skill consistency rules via expert elicitation from the C2A clinical team
  • Encode rules in PSL with continuous truth values
  • Deliverable: PSL rule specification + validation against 50 real C2A profiles

Aim 2: Validation Layer Prototype (Months 3-6)

  • Implement PSL-based validation layer in the DDx report pipeline
  • Generate candidate report narratives via LLM
  • Score narratives against the constraint graph
  • Compare: PSL validation vs. current DDx template matching vs. LLM self-check
  • Metrics: constraint violation rate, inter-rater reliability (Cohen's kappa), generation latency

Aim 3: Feasibility Assessment (Months 6-9)

  • Pilot with 20 real student profiles from C2A
  • Expert panel review: 3 coaches rate validated vs. unvalidated reports
  • Analyze: false positive rate, constraint coverage, latency overhead
  • Cost-effectiveness: validation layer compute cost vs. coach review time saved
  • Deliverable: Phase I final report with go/no-go for Phase II
