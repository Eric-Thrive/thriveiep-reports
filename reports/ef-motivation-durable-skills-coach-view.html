Section 01 of 23

Executive Function, Motivation, and Durable Skills

The complete Coach View evidence, in one place. This master briefing answers three questions: what are the constructs we measure, which of them actually predict durable skills (and why), and what should a coach do about each one. It draws on roughly 285 studies and our own NLU cohort data.

Key Messages

  • How a student uses executive function predicts durable skills; raw cognitive capacity barely does. In our NLU cohort, the everyday/applied side of EF carried almost all the unique prediction of durable skills. The abstract "engine" side added almost nothing once applied EF was in the model.
  • The reason is content, with an honest asterisk. C2A measures every layer by self-report rating (the ADEXI, the flexibility questionnaire, and the applied-EF items are all "how often does this happen to you" scales), so the gap is not lab-test-vs-survey. Applied-EF items describe goal-directed everyday behavior that sits right next to the durable-skill outcomes; core-EF items describe basic cognitive mechanics that sit further away. Part of the gap is real signal; part is that our predictor and outcome share a method.
  • Motivation decides whether skills stick. Psychological needs (autonomy, relatedness, competence) and the type of motivation (wanting to vs. having to) determine whether a coached skill is durable or fades under stress.
  • We profile all 16 constructs. Each gets what it is, how C2A measures it (or doesn't), what a coach can do, and how solid the evidence is, including four EF skills newly upgraded from expert opinion to controlled-trial evidence.
  • We are honest about three holes. We measure our most important layer most thinly; we have not shown the 16 constructs are truly distinct; and we have not yet proven that coaching these skills actually moves durable skills. Naming these is a strength, not a weakness.

How this report is organized

Part 1 (Concepts) defines the building blocks. Part 2 (Unique vs. Shared Variance) shows which constructs actually add predictive value, with a close look at performance vs. rating-scale EF. Part 3 (The Applied Work) profiles all 16 constructs, maps them to durable skills, and says what to do. Evidence badges: High = strong/replicated, Moderate = real but limited, Low = consensus or emerging. Heavy statistics are tucked into expandable boxes.

Section 02 of 23

The Big Picture: A Student in Four Layers

Picture a student as a vehicle on a road. C2A measures the vehicle in layers, plus the conditions around it.

The Engine (core EF)

Raw thinking power: working memory, inhibition, cognitive flexibility. Necessary, but rarely what separates students on real-world skills.

Daily Operations (applied EF)

How the engine runs day to day: time management, organization, planning, task initiation, self-monitoring, sustained attention, emotional regulation. Where coaching lives.

The Fuel (motivation)

Psychological needs (autonomy, relatedness, competence) and the type of motivation. Decides whether skills get used and whether they stick.

The Road (context)

Strengths-first framing, financial stress, trauma history, culture. These shape how the whole profile should be read and sequenced.

The result that drives everything: in our NLU cohort, a model combining all three vehicle layers explained 62.5% of why students differ on durable skills, but the Daily-Operations layer carried almost all the unique weight, the Fuel added a small real amount, and the raw Engine added essentially none. Part 2 explains what "unique weight" means and why this happens.

A profile is a snapshot, not destiny. Much of what looks like low EF is state (stress, financial load, life chaos), not a fixed trait. Most profiles are a mix of the two. Read a first assessment as a starting point for conversation, not a verdict on the person.

Part 1 · Concepts  |  Section 03 of 23

Concept Review: Two Kinds of Executive Function

Executive function (EF) is the brain's self-management system. It comes in two flavors that matter enormously for prediction.

Core EF (the engine)

The three raw capacities: working memory (holding info in mind), inhibition (the brake pedal), and cognitive flexibility (switching gears), the classic "Miyake triad." In the research these are often measured with lab tasks, but C2A measures them by self-report rating (the ADEXI and a flexibility questionnaire), the same method as every other C2A layer.

Important nuance: even on its own terms, the triad does not replicate cleanly across populations (Karr et al., 2018), so a single "core EF" score is shakier than it sounds.

Applied EF (daily operations)

The same machinery as it shows up in real life: getting started, planning, organizing, managing time, monitoring yourself, regulating emotion. Built on top of core EF but not the same thing.

Adele Diamond's framework puts it well: complex EFs like planning and problem-solving are built on core EFs but carry their own predictive weight for real-world outcomes.

The everyday/applied layer is where durable skills (the C2A outcome of interest) actually get expressed, and as Part 2 shows, it is the layer that predicts them.

The technical version: the EF hierarchy

Diamond (2012) distinguishes core EFs (inhibition, working-memory updating, shifting) from higher-order EFs (reasoning, planning, problem-solving) that are built on the core but functionally distinct. Miyake & Friedman's unity/diversity model shows the core EFs are correlated yet separable, and the common-EF factor is related to but not identical with general intelligence, which is why EF carries variance beyond IQ. Karr et al. (2018), re-analyzing 46 samples, found the three-factor model frequently failed to converge, suggesting the clean triad is partly an artifact of underpowered, well-fitting published models.

Concept Review: Motivation Has a Type, Not Just an Amount

From Self-Determination Theory (SDT), the most-validated framework in motivation science, two ideas matter.

ARC: the three needs (the fuel tank)

Autonomy (this is my choice), Relatedness (I belong and am seen), Competence (I can do this). Feed them and people grow; starve them and people get defensive and withdraw.

Satisfaction and frustration of needs run on separate tracks, so a coach needs different moves for each. C2A measures ARC with the BMPN-18.

GMS: the type of motivation (the fuel quality)

A continuum from intrinsic ("I want to") through external regulation ("I have to / for a reward") down to amotivation ("why bother"). The dividing line that counts is autonomous vs. controlled.

Quality beats quantity: the same skill is durable under "I want to" and fragile under "I have to." C2A measures this with the GMS-Bookends.

The technical version: internalization and why quality matters

SDT's organismic integration theory places regulation on a continuum (external, introjected, identified, integrated, intrinsic). The more internalized the regulation, the more autonomous and durable the behavior. Ryan & Deci (2020) describe controlled motivation as "vitality-draining because it entails conflict, one part of personality overriding another." This is the mechanism behind skill decay: a skill maintained under controlled motivation burns regulatory fuel and collapses under stress, while an internalized one is self-sustaining.

Concept Review: Durable Skills and the Four Context Factors

Durable skills (the outcome)

The transferable human capabilities C2A reports on, things like communication, collaboration, resilience, initiative, adaptability, critical thinking. The workforce field validates these as observable behaviors in real contexts (NACE validated theirs against roughly 60,000 supervisor ratings), the same "measure it as it shows up in real life" logic that runs through this whole report.

The four context factors (the road conditions)

These are not scores; they are lenses that change how a profile should be read.

Strengths-first framing

How a profile is presented (strengths-first vs. deficit-first) measurably changes coach expectations and student performance, more so for marginalized students.

Financial precarity

Money stress imposes a temporary "bandwidth tax" on the exact EF skills C2A measures, and it is largely reversible.

Trauma history

Chronic adversity shifts the brain toward reactive over planful processing; safety must come before skill-building.

Culture

The needs are universal, but the entry point shifts. Relatedness leads, and group-specific adaptation beats generic.

Part 2 · Unique vs. Shared Variance  |  Section 06 of 23

The Real Question: Does a Construct Add Anything?

Here is the idea the whole evidence base turns on, in plain terms.

Shared variance

When two measures are correlated, they split the credit for predicting an outcome. That overlapping credit is shared variance. Most of our constructs overlap a lot.

Unique variance (the real test)

The slice a construct explains that nothing else already did. The honest question is never "does X correlate with the outcome," it is "does X add anything once everything else is in the model." That is called incremental validity.

Why this is sharpest for executive function

Because there are two ways to measure EF, and they barely agree:

  • Performance tasks (lab puzzles, card-sorting, memory span): measure the efficiency of cognitive machinery in a quiet room.
  • Rating scales (everyday self-report: "I lose track of steps," "I run late"): measure success at pursuing goals in noisy real life.

You would expect these to agree. They correlate only weakly (about 0.1 to 0.3). The landmark review concluded they assess different constructs, "the efficiency of cognitive abilities" versus "success in goal pursuit" (Toplak, West & Stanovich, 2013). This is the correspondence problem.

Crucial for reading C2A honestly: C2A uses none of these performance tasks. The ADEXI (core EF), the cognitive-flexibility questionnaire, and the applied-EF items are all self-report rating scales. So the correspondence problem tells us ratings are a sound choice over lab tests, but it does not explain our internal result that core EF added almost no unique variance, because both our layers are ratings. That gap needs a different explanation, which the next two slides give.

Performance vs. Rating Scale: When You Pit Them Head to Head

When researchers put both kinds of EF measure in the same model and ask which carries unique predictive weight for real-world outcomes, the everyday/rating measure wins, repeatedly.

StudyPopulationWhat happened with both in the model
Barkley & Murphy, 2010 High352 adultsEveryday EF ratings predicted all 11 job-functioning outcomes and dominated the lab tests when entered jointly.
Dijkhuis et al., 2020 ModCollege studentsSelf-reported daily EF lifted academic-progress prediction to 36%; performance-based EF reached only 25%.
Bayard et al., 2026 ModAdults, clinicalOnly the everyday rating and a real-world task predicted daily functioning. Lab tests added zero unique variance.
Chaytor et al., 2006 HighMixed adultsLab EF tests explained only 18 to 20% of everyday EF; real-world context variables added much more.
Dekker et al., 2017 ModChildrenRating EF and performance EF each added unique variance; neither alone captured the full picture.

The pattern holds across job performance, school grades, and daily living. The everyday measure is the one connected to the life outcome.

How this applies to C2A: every study above pits a performance arm against a rating arm. C2A has no performance arm, so this body of work justifies our choice to measure EF by rating. It does not, on its own, explain the core-vs-applied gap inside our all-rating battery, that is the next slide.
The mechanism: why the rooms differ

Lab tasks deliberately strip away context, motivation, stakes, multi-step goals, and emotion, exactly the conditions that make real-world self-management hard. Burgess et al. (2006) argue the classic tests (Stroop, Wisconsin Card Sort) were adapted from laboratory paradigms "far removed" from real concerns, and call for function-led design instead. The metacognition/behavioral-regulation layer (initiation, planning, organization, monitoring) is the EF cluster most tied to academic and occupational outcomes (Cortes Pascual et al., 2019, meta-analysis, r=.365).

Layer by Layer: What Actually Adds Unique Value

Putting the layers into one model (as our NLU analysis did) shows how the credit splits once overlap is removed.

17.4%

4.9%
ARC needs unique variance
~0%
Core EF unique variance (once applied EF is in)
  • Applied EF carries unique weight over core EF and over IQ. This matches the literature (Barkley & Murphy; Dekker; ADEXI incremental validity).
  • Core EF mostly shares variance with applied EF rather than adding its own. Because both are self-report ratings, this is not a lab-test-vs-survey effect. Two things are likely happening: applied-EF items describe goal-directed everyday behavior that sits close to the durable-skill outcomes (so they predict well), while core-EF items ("I have difficulty remembering lengthy instructions") describe basic cognitive mechanics further from the outcome; and even among rating scales, behavioral-regulation/metacognition content predicts real-world outcomes better than basic-cognitive content (Cortes Pascual 2019; Dekker 2017).
  • ARC needs add a modest unique slice. Consistent with an independent finding that an ARC-style measure explained 33% of first-year GPA, but overlaps with EF, so its unique contribution is smaller (Graham & Vaughan, 2022).
  • Motivation and EF share a lot. They are tangled, not independent, which is why integrated coaching beats fixing one then the other (Part 3).
Plain takeaway: of everything C2A measures, the everyday/applied EF layer is the one most associated with durable skills. Core EF looks redundant here, though partly because it shares variance with applied EF and sits further from the outcome in content. That is a real case for leading the Coach View with applied EF, but the next slide flags why we should hold the exact number loosely.

The Caveats We Say Out Loud First

The pattern is real and well-supported. But before we put a hard number like "17%" in front of a funder, there are three honest objections a measurement scientist will raise, so we raise them ourselves.

1. Shared method AND content inflate the headline

Applied EF and durable skills are both self-reported by the same student in the same sitting, and their items overlap in content (both describe goal-directed everyday behavior). So a meaningful part of the 17.4% is the same source describing similar things twice, not pure prediction (Suchy et al., 2024). Core EF, being more distal in content (basic cognitive mechanics), is partly penalized for the same reason. Because every C2A measure is a rating scale, none of this is a lab-test artifact; it is a method-and-content artifact. Until we test against a method-independent outcome, treat the size of the applied-EF advantage as an upper bound, not a fact.

2. We measure our most important layer most thinly

The layer doing all the predictive work is the one we capture least well. Working memory gets a 9-item validated scale, but task initiation, self-monitoring, and emotional regulation are derived from short, double-duty personality items, and organization and sustained attention are not measured at all. We are leaning hardest on our flimsiest measurement.

3. We have not shown the 16 constructs are truly distinct

These constructs overlap heavily (that is the shared-variance story), and in our NLU data the four skill "pillars" did not behave as distinct groups, skills inside a pillar were no more related to each other than to skills in other pillars. Fair question: are we reporting 16 labels for maybe 3 or 4 underlying factors? We should test the discriminant validity of our own measures, not just assume it.

What would make the case bulletproof

Add one outcome not from the same self-report sitting (a coach rating, behavioral log, or later-timepoint score); run a factor analysis to see how many distinct things we actually measure; and remember n=32 is small (our optimism-corrected estimate already fell from 62.5% to about 53%). Treat the figures as directional until the next cohort.

Part 3 · The Applied Work  |  Section 10 of 23

The Engine: Core Executive Function

The big practical lesson: you mostly work around these, you don't train them up.

Working Memory High

What it is: Holding information in mind long enough to use it.

C2A measure: ADEXI (9 items).

Coach move: Compensate only, external tools, simpler environment. Do not try to train it (three meta-analyses, N greater than 3,000, show no far-transfer).

Durable-skill link: Critical Thinking, Communication.

Inhibitory Control High

What it is: The brake pedal, stopping an automatic reaction.

C2A measure: ADEXI (5 items).

Coach move: Mostly accommodate/compensate; modest direct trainability (d=0.378) via behavioral practice, not brain games.

Durable-skill link: Empathy, Collaboration.

Cognitive Flexibility Moderate

What it is: Switching gears when the situation changes.

C2A measure: C2A-CF (Yoshida CFQ, 4 items).

Coach move: Trainable only in context (d=0.37); use real scenarios, not generic computer training.

Durable-skill link: Creative Problem Solving, Adaptability, Resilience (predicts it better than inhibition does).

Headline for coaches

Working memory is compensate-only, the single biggest correction to old coach guidance. Offload it to tools, do not try to build it.

Daily Operations: The Highest-Leverage Skills

Time Management High

What it is: Estimating how long things take and getting them done.

C2A measure: Can be dual-scored from 3 existing ADEXI items; no standalone scale yet.

Coach move: Full toolkit with RCT support (calendars, time-estimation practice, group coaching, gains held at 3 months). Fixing it cascades into better regulation and initiation.

Durable-skill link: Initiative.

Organization Moderate

What it is: Keeping things and information in order.

C2A measure: Not measured (no items). Coaching conversation.

Coach move: Systems, templates, tech; usually bundled with time management.

Durable-skill link: Daily-Operations cluster.

Planning / Prioritization Moderate

What it is: Mapping steps to a goal and deciding what comes first.

C2A measure: Partial via ADEXI; otherwise inferred.

Coach move: Newly upgraded. Direct instruction works (d=0.64 to 0.87); "if-then" plans are brief and effective. Teach it explicitly.

Durable-skill link: Communication (secondary).

Headline for coaches

Time management and organization are Priority 1: strongest evidence, biggest payoff. Note the gap, organization has no items, so it lives in the conversation for now.

Regulation and Activation: Feeling and Getting Started

Emotional Regulation Moderate

What it is: Managing feelings so they don't hijack the task.

C2A measure: Applied-EF, via shared personality items (Emotional Control).

Coach move: Often improves indirectly, stabilize daily operations first; needs-based coaching helps it as a side effect.

Durable-skill link: Resilience (via cognitive flexibility).

Task Initiation Mod (upgraded)

What it is: Getting started without putting it off.

C2A measure: Applied-EF, via shared items (Initiate).

Coach move: Newly upgraded. Behavioral activation works (d=0.49); a 30-minute "if-then" exercise raised goal-directed work 60%.

Durable-skill link: Initiative (primary).

Self-Monitoring Mod (upgraded)

What it is: Noticing how you're doing and adjusting.

C2A measure: Applied-EF, via shared items (Self-Monitor).

Coach move: Newly upgraded. "Goal-Plan-Do-Check" generalized to new tasks in a clean trial; build it into the coaching cycle.

Durable-skill link: Relationship Building.

Sustained Attention Low

What it is: Staying focused over time.

C2A measure: Not measured (no items).

Coach move: Weakest evidence. CBT helps the feeling of inattention; capacity training is unproven. Reduce distraction, add physical activity.

Durable-skill link: Not directly assigned.

The Fuel, Part 1: ARC Psychological Needs

Feeding these needs is an active intervention, not a nicety.

Autonomy High

What it is: Feeling you drive your own choices.

C2A measure: BMPN-18.

Coach move: Offer real choice and rationale. Choosing to prioritize family is autonomous, not a distraction.

Relatedness High

What it is: Feeling connected and genuinely seen.

C2A measure: BMPN-18.

Coach move: For NLU's population this is the gateway: until a student feels connected, other moves don't land. Lead here.

Competence High

What it is: Feeling capable of the challenge.

C2A measure: BMPN-18.

Coach move: Build through small real wins. Watch for need frustration, a separate dial needing its own repair.

Why it matters

Low-income students arrive with higher baseline need frustration, less fuel for learning new systems. Needs are universal across cultures, but the entry point shifts.

Part 3 · The Applied Work  |  Section 15 of 23

The Fuel, Part 2: Motivation Type and the EF Tangle

Motivation Type (GMS) High

What it is: The kind of motivation, from "I want to" to "why bother."

C2A measure: GMS-Bookends (10 items).

Coach move: Quality beats quantity. Watch for the language shift from "want to" toward "should", the early warning that effort has turned fragile.

Amotivation (warning light) Moderate

What it is: No drive, checked out.

C2A measure: GMS-Bookends.

Coach move: Breaks attendance and effort before grades. Treat as "fix the fuel," not "add more structure."

Motivation x EF Moderate

What it is: Motivation and EF are tangled (they share variance), not separate problems. They also feed each other: repeated failure erodes motivation, and weak motivation makes EF effort exhausting.

Coach move: Work on both at once. The largest effect in the EF coaching literature (d=1.10 for self-regulation) blended motivation support with skill-building.

The one exception

A student in acute need frustration needs a brief relatedness-first stabilization before skill work. A defensive brain can't absorb new strategies. (Basis for the triage step.)

The Road, Part 1: Framing and Financial Stress

Strengths-First Framing Moderate

What it is: How a profile is presented changes outcomes.

C2A measure: Report-design rule, not a score.

Coach move: "Strengths first, growth included." Frame growth as "growing edges" tied to a strength, never deficits. A balanced profile beats Pollyanna and beats deficit-first.

Why: The effect is larger for racially marginalized students, NLU's cohort.

Financial Precarity High

What it is: Money stress is a temporary tax on the EF skills C2A measures.

C2A measure: Not measured, a context flag worth adding.

Coach move: Flag, don't label. A stressed student's low EF is partly stress, not stable capacity. Resource navigation may restore more than memory exercises.

Why: Largely reversible (same person scores worse stressed, better when it lifts).

The numbers (Shields 2016, 51 studies)

Money stress impairs working memory (g=-0.20), cognitive flexibility (g=-0.30), and cognitive inhibition (g=-0.21), but slightly enhances response inhibition (g=+0.30). The recognizable pattern: working memory and flexibility down, response inhibition normal or up, points to a stress state rather than stable weakness. A first assessment from a stressed student is a snapshot of their situation, not a verdict on their potential.

Part 3 · The Applied Work  |  Section 17 of 23

The Road, Part 2: Trauma and Culture

Trauma-Informed Coaching Moderate

What it is: Adjusting when history and stress show up as EF struggles.

C2A measure: Not assessed, a coach triage judgment.

Coach move: "Stabilize first, and know your lane." Build safety before skills (Protect-Connect-Respect-Redirect). Coaching teaches coping and self-regulation; it does not diagnose or treat. Refer on the bright lines (suicidal ideation, acute crisis, persistent impairment, "if you have to ask, refer").

Cross-Cultural Factors High

What it is: How culture shapes the coaching relationship.

C2A measure: Not measured, a coaching posture.

Coach move: "Relatedness first, and don't generalize." Honor existing networks; differentiate guidance for Black vs. Latinx learners, not one template.

Why: Group-specific adaptation is about 4x more effective than generic; what the student perceives as cultural competence predicts outcomes (r=0.38), the coach's self-rating does not (r=0.06).

What This Means for Us

High  Lead the Coach View with applied EF

It is the layer that uniquely predicts durable skills and the one coaches can act on. Time management and organization first; planning, task initiation, and self-monitoring are now evidence-backed enough to surface with confidence.

High  Compensate for the Engine, don't train it; tell coaches the cross-cutting lever

Make "working memory is compensate-only" explicit in coach training. And give every coach the one lever that lifts several EF skills at once: regular physical activity (g=0.56).

High  Treat motivation type and needs as the durability layer

A strong skill under pressured motivation is fragile. Give coaches the ARC and motivation reading as the "will it stick" lens, relatedness first for this population. Add the 60-second triage check before RISE's Recognize step.

Critical  Shore up the measurement before publishing the number

Add a method-independent outcome (coach rating, behavioral log, or later-timepoint score) so the applied-EF advantage can't be dismissed as shared-method inflation; run a factor analysis to test whether the 16 constructs are distinct; and strengthen how we capture the applied-EF skills (more than double-duty items). Highest-leverage work for the SBIR.

Decision  Close the two measurement gaps

Decide what to do about Organization and Sustained Attention (add items, proxy, or conversation). Start with the cheap win: dual-score time management from the 3 existing ADEXI items.

Open questions for the next cohort

  • The big one: we have shown applied EF predicts durable skills and that these skills are trainable, but not that coaching them actually raises durable skills and real-world outcomes for our population. Proving that full chain is the central thing to test.
  • Does applied EF still predict durable skills against a coach-rated or later-timepoint outcome?
  • Do the 16 constructs hold up as distinct factors in our own data?
  • Should organization and sustained attention get dedicated items, or stay in the conversation?

Strongest evidence

The correspondence problem and ratings-beat-tests for real-world outcomes (multiple high-validity reviews; Toplak 2013; Barkley & Murphy 2010; Chaytor 2006; Cortes Pascual 2019). Working memory is compensate-only (3 meta-analyses). The universal "stabilize first" sequence (6 independent streams). ARC needs and cultural-adaptation effects (large meta-analyses). Financial stress as a reversible tax (51-study meta-analysis). Coaching improves self-regulation (Field 2013 RCT; Anderson & Marino 17-study review).

Weakest / honest gaps

  • The causal chain is unproven end to end: nobody has yet shown that coaching these EF skills raises durable skills and real-world outcomes for our population.
  • Population mismatch: most of this evidence comes from clinical, ADHD, or brain-injury groups, while NLU's cohort is non-clinical, working-adult, and majority Black and Latinx. Generalization is reasonable but largely untested.
  • Our headline number rests partly on shared-method variance until a method-independent outcome is added, and the 16 constructs are not yet shown to be distinct.
  • Sustained attention as a trainable capacity stays unproven; "durable skill" as a named outcome is mostly inferred from close cousins (GPA, job functioning, daily living).
  • Keeping skill gains without ongoing coaching is an open question.

This master report supersedes the two earlier drafts (applied-EF briefing and the construct compendium) and extends the six underlying NLU research reports, which remain the detailed source material.
