  • Key Message 1: Your three-model pipeline splits naturally: the NER model (obi/deid_roberta_i2b2) stays on Aptible's CPU, while the LLM (qwen2.5:7b) and OCR model (GLM-OCR) move to a serverless GPU.
  • Key Message 2: Serverless GPU hosting costs $3-5/month at pilot scale (100-500 docs), compared to $730+/month for always-on GPU instances.
  • Key Message 3: RunPod and Modal now offer HIPAA BAAs (Business Associate Agreements), making serverless GPU inference HIPAA-compliant as of February 2026.

How fast will it be?

A 150-page document takes roughly 3.5-5 minutes with GPU acceleration, compared to 17-25 minutes on CPU only. The bottleneck is the two LLM passes (qwen2.5:7b), each taking 60-100 seconds on an L4 GPU.

What does it cost?

At pilot scale (100-500 documents per month), you need only 33 minutes to 2.8 hours of GPU time monthly. Serverless providers bill per-second and scale to zero between requests, keeping costs under $5/month. Always-on endpoints (AWS SageMaker, GCP Vertex AI) cost $292-730/month for the same workload.

Is it HIPAA-compliant?

Yes. The architecture is stateless: text goes in, entities come out. No PHI (Protected Health Information) is persisted on the GPU service. Combined with a signed BAA from RunPod or Modal, this meets HIPAA requirements. However, VLM-based OCR has a newly documented PHI leakage risk through vision tokens (Young, 2025), so OCR should stay on controlled infrastructure.

Section 2 of 10

GPU vs. CPU: Which Models Need a GPU?

Not every model benefits from GPU acceleration. The decision depends on model architecture, parameter count, and your throughput requirements.

obi/deid_roberta_i2b2 (355M params) CPU OK

This is a RoBERTa-LARGE encoder-only model (24 layers, hidden_size=1024). Encoder-only models are "embarrassingly parallel" on CPU because they process all tokens at once, unlike autoregressive decoders. CPU inference: 100-300ms per chunk unoptimized, or 50-80ms with ONNX Runtime + INT8 quantization. At 100-500 docs/month, CPU throughput is more than sufficient.

INT4 quantization is safe for encoder-only models with no accuracy loss (Wu et al., 2023). Multiple clinical NER studies confirm BERT-class models outperform 70B LLMs for de-identification tasks (Zambare et al., 2026).

qwen2.5:7b (7B params) GPU REQUIRED

Decoder-only models generate tokens one at a time, making them memory-bandwidth bound. On CPU (Ollama), this model produces only 8-12 tokens/second. On an L4 GPU: ~53 tokens/second. For a 150-page document requiring ~5,000 tokens per LLM pass, that is 100 seconds (GPU) vs. 500 seconds (CPU) per pass.

T4 GPUs are impractical at ~3.8 tok/s. L4 is the "practical sweet spot" at $0.50-0.75/hr. A100 (~84 tok/s) is overkill for single-request inference (Wltsankalpa benchmark, 2025).

GLM-OCR (0.9B params) GPU REQUIRED

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) process images through both a vision encoder and a language decoder. GLM-OCR achieves 1.86 pages/second on GPU vs. near-impossible throughput on CPU. A 150-page document takes ~75 seconds on GPU.

Critical HIPAA finding: VLM OCR leaks PHI even with vision token masking (Young, 2025). Self-hosted or TEE-isolated OCR is recommended for HIPAA compliance.

Recommended Architecture

The pipeline splits into two environments connected by HTTPS. Aptible handles data persistence, user-facing app, and CPU-viable NER. The GPU service handles stateless inference only.

Aptible (HIPAA PaaS, CPU only)

  • Express API orchestrates the pipeline and stores documents
  • Presidio Flask + obi/deid_roberta_i2b2 runs NER detection (355M params, CPU-viable)
  • Post-filter rules suppress false positives (O1-O4, L2, D1-D7, etc.)
  • React frontend provides the review UI and export

Serverless GPU (RunPod/Modal, L4 24GB VRAM)

  • GLM-OCR (0.9B VLM) for PDF text extraction (~2.2GB VRAM)
  • qwen2.5:7b for Pass 1 (recall extraction) + Pass 2 (precision verification) (~14GB VRAM, or 7GB with INT8)
  • Total VRAM: ~16-21GB, fits on a single L4 (24GB)
  • Stateless: text in, results out, no storage, no PHI persisted

Communication

Aptible sends text to the GPU service via HTTPS (TLS 1.2+). The GPU service returns structured entities/text. No PHI is logged or stored on the GPU side. The existing codebase already supports configurable endpoints via GLM_OCR_ENDPOINT and llm_verification.url in detection-config.json.

Section 7 of 10

What Researchers Agree and Disagree On

Strong Consensus

  • BERT-class NER on CPU is fine. Multiple studies confirm encoder-only models are CPU-viable and actually outperform 70B LLMs for clinical de-identification (Zambare et al., 2026).
  • L4 is the sweet spot. T4 is too slow for 7B models (3.8 tok/s). A100 is overkill for single-request inference. L4 hits ~53 tok/s at $0.50/hr.
  • Serverless wins at pilot scale. The math is overwhelming: $3-5/month vs. $292-730/month.
  • INT4 quantization is safe for encoders, risky for decoders. Wu et al. (2023, 30 citations) showed encoder-only models tolerate INT4 with no accuracy loss, but decoder-only models lose quality.

Active Debate

  • RunPod/Modal vs. hyperscaler compliance depth. Newer BAAs (Feb 2026) vs. battle-tested AWS/Azure HIPAA programs.
  • Ollama vs. vLLM for production serving. Red Hat benchmarked a 19x throughput gap under concurrent load, but single-request performance is similar. At pilot scale, this may not matter.
  • Where should OCR live? The PHI leakage paper (Young, 2025) suggests keeping VLM OCR on controlled infrastructure, but this conflicts with the cost advantage of co-locating on the GPU service.
Section 8 of 10

HIPAA Risk Assessment

CRITICAL VLM OCR PHI Leakage

Young (2025) demonstrated that Vision-Language Models leak Protected Health Information through vision token embeddings, even when explicit text masking is applied. This means sending document images to an external GPU service for OCR carries inherent PHI exposure risk.

Mitigation: Either keep OCR on Aptible (CPU, much slower) or ensure the GPU service has full BAA coverage and uses encrypted, ephemeral processing with no logging of image data.

HIGH New BAA Track Record

RunPod's and Modal's HIPAA BAAs date to February 2026. Neither has completed a full audit cycle under their BAAs. AWS and Azure have years of HIPAA compliance history.

Mitigation: Start with RunPod/Modal for cost savings. Include contractual audit rights. Have a migration path to SageMaker/Azure ML if compliance concerns arise.

MODERATE Stateless Architecture Helps

The GPU service processes text and returns results without storing anything. No PHI at rest on the GPU service. Combined with TLS in transit and a signed BAA, this architecture minimizes the HIPAA attack surface.

What This Means for Us

CRITICAL Deploy split architecture: Aptible + serverless GPU

Keep obi/deid_roberta_i2b2, Presidio, post-filter rules, and the Express/React app on Aptible. Move qwen2.5:7b and GLM-OCR to RunPod or Modal serverless with L4 GPUs.

Evidence: L4 benchmarks (53 tok/s), cost analysis ($3-5/mo vs $730/mo), existing DR-010 decision record.

CRITICAL Sign a BAA before sending any PHI to GPU service

Contact RunPod or Modal Enterprise for a signed BAA. The BAA must cover inference endpoints specifically. Modal's BAA excludes Volumes v1, Images, and Snapshots, but stateless inference is covered.

Evidence: HIPAA requires BAA for any third-party PHI handling. No exceptions for "ephemeral" processing.

HIGH Keep OCR on controlled infrastructure

Given the VLM PHI leakage finding (Young, 2025), consider keeping GLM-OCR on Aptible (accept slower OCR) or ensuring the GPU service provides confidential computing (TEE isolation).

Evidence: Young (2025) demonstrated PHI leaks through vision tokens. Counterargument: BAA-covered stateless processing may be sufficient.

HIGH Use vLLM instead of Ollama for production

Red Hat benchmarked a 19x throughput gap under concurrent load. For production with multiple simultaneous users, vLLM's continuous batching provides significantly better throughput. At pilot scale with single-request patterns, Ollama may be adequate.

Evidence: Red Hat benchmark (2025). Uncertain: single-request performance is similar.

MODERATE Optimize obi/deid with ONNX+INT8 on CPU

The 355M RoBERTa-LARGE model runs 100-300ms per chunk unoptimized. ONNX Runtime with INT8 quantization can reduce this to 50-80ms with no accuracy loss. This is a low-effort optimization for the CPU-hosted NER.

Evidence: Wu et al. (2023, 30 citations); HuggingFace Optimum benchmarks.
