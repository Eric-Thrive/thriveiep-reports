Coach View: What the Research Says

A briefing for Elizabeth McWhorter, NLU Leadership, and the Coaching Team

May 6, 2026

We asked: should the Coach View draw on existing research, or build its own framework?

The answer: the research base is strong enough to build on, but it needs careful assembly.

Section 1 of 15

Seven Things That Matter Most

Critical Work on motivation and EF at the same time

The biggest coaching effect sizes (d=1.10) come from integrated approaches, not "fix motivation first, then build skills." One study that only targeted motivation still improved attention as a side effect.

Critical Working memory cannot be trained

Three meta-analyses, 3,000+ participants, definitive null result. Coaches should compensate (external tools, systems) and accommodate (simplify the environment). Do not attempt to strengthen working memory directly.

High Financial stress distorts EF profiles

Financial worry impairs working memory and cognitive flexibility by 0.2 to 0.3 standard deviations. These effects are largely reversible when the stressor lifts. A first assessment is a snapshot of the student's situation, not just their stable capacity.

High Profile framing shapes student outcomes

Practitioner perception bias is measured, not theoretical. Strengths-first profiles produce higher expectations and better outcomes. Deficit-first profiles produce lower expectations. The Coach View's language choices have downstream consequences.

High Surface 7 more EF dimensions in priority tiers

C2A measures 10 dimensions but reports 3. Time management and organization are Priority 1 (strongest evidence). Planning and emotional regulation are Priority 2. Task initiation, sustained attention, and self-monitoring are Priority 3.

Moderate Trauma creates a distinct, recognizable EF pattern

ACEs impair inhibitory control and cognitive flexibility while sparing working memory. Coaches need scope-of-practice guardrails: coach EF skills, do not treat trauma. Emotion regulation coaching is the highest-leverage buffer.

Moderate Relatedness is the gateway need; differentiate by group

For Black and Latinx working adults, relatedness must be satisfied before autonomy and competence work becomes productive. Group-specific coaching adaptations are 4x more effective than generic multicultural approaches.

Section 2 of 15

Motivation × Executive Function

Report A · 22 papers · "They're tangled, work on both"

What did we find?

When a student has low motivation and weak EF, do you fix the motivation first? The answer: neither. You work on both simultaneously.

  • The largest coaching effect size (d=1.10 for self-regulation) came from integrating SDT-based motivation support into EF coaching as a single protocol[1] (N=160 college students with ADHD).
  • A study targeting only motivation produced significant improvements in ADHD-related inattention as a secondary outcome.[2] Motivation work has downstream EF benefits.
  • Motivation quality determines sustainability. "I should make a study schedule" (controlled motivation) is depleting. "I want to organize my week" (autonomous motivation) is durable.[3]

The Motivation × EF Quadrant

Coach View should present these as interacting dimensions, not separate problems:

High Motivation + Low EF

"Ready to learn, needs tools"

Compensate + build skills. This student wants to succeed but lacks the systems.

High Motivation + High EF

"Building momentum"

Maintain and stretch. Support continued growth.

Low Motivation + Low EF

"Needs connection first"

Reduce frustration + small wins. The coaching relationship itself is the intervention.

Low Motivation + High EF

"Has the tools, looking for purpose"

Build belonging + connect to values. Help them find the "why."

Watch for this signal: If a student shifts from "I want to" to "I should," the effort is becoming depleting and will collapse. Return to autonomy support, not more structure.

For NLU's population specifically: low-SES individuals arrive with higher baseline need frustration.[4] The coaching relationship directly satisfies the relatedness need and replenishes the regulatory tank.

Positive Psychology Framing

Report B · 22 papers · "Strengths first, growth included"

Elizabeth's directive: Don't put the student in a deficit space

The research agrees, but with a nuance: lead with strengths, but don't hide growth areas. The combined approach outperforms both strengths-only and deficit-focused.

SUDCO Model Combined approach outperforms both extremes

Van Woerkom et al.[5] tested this across three samples. Organizations supporting both strengths use and deficit correction saw higher engagement, satisfaction, and learning than those using either approach alone. A balanced profile outperforms a Pollyanna one.

Perception Bias Framing literally changes outcomes

Asset-based pedagogy beliefs moderate the self-fulfilling prophecy.[6] How the Coach View frames a profile changes coach expectations and thereby student outcomes. This effect is larger for racially marginalized populations.

Language Patterns for Coach View

Instead of...Use...
"Weakness in working memory""Growing edge: holding multiple pieces of information"
"Deficit in time management""Building capacity: estimating and allocating time"
"Poor emotional regulation""Developing: managing responses when things get frustrating"
"Impaired inhibition""Your brain moves quickly — building the pause button"

The "overuse of a strength" reframe[7] is a validated technique for neurodivergent populations. Each growth area should link back to a strength: "Strong in planning but growing in time estimation."

Financial Precarity × Cognition

Report C · 26 papers · "It's the stress, not the student"

Financial stress imposes a measurable "bandwidth tax" on EF

The meta-analytic picture[8] (51 studies, 2,486 participants) shows stress does not uniformly degrade all EF. It hits specific components differently:

EF ComponentEffect of StressSize
Working MemoryImpairedg = -0.197
Cognitive FlexibilityImpairedg = -0.300
Cognitive InhibitionImpairedg = -0.208
Response InhibitionEnhancedg = +0.296

Most of the effect is state, not trait

The same Indian farmers performed differently before harvest (financially stressed) vs. after harvest (flush), serving as their own controls.[9] Stress-induced brain changes partially reverse when stressors are removed.[10]

The diagnostic pattern: When working memory and cognitive flexibility are both depressed but response inhibition is at or above average, the profile is more consistent with stress-state depression than stable EF weakness.

What this means for coaches

  • Resource navigation may produce larger cognitive gains than EF training. Connecting a student to emergency financial aid might restore 0.2 to 0.3 SD of working memory more effectively than memory exercises.
  • Coach View should include a context note when financial stress is reported: "This profile may reflect situational stress. Consider reassessing after the stressor resolves."
  • The "hidden talents" reframe[11]: stress-adapted cognition includes enhanced attention shifting, threat detection, and procedural learning. Real cognitive assets that standard EF measures miss.

The Full EF Spectrum

Reading the table

Columns: Accommodate = change the environment so the limitation matters less. Compensate = use external tools or workarounds to substitute for the skill. Strengthen = directly build the underlying capacity through practice.

Evidence terms:

  • Strong (RCT) = Supported by a randomized controlled trial (the gold standard: randomly assigned groups, measured outcomes)
  • Strong = Multiple studies with direct, measurable evidence for this intervention type
  • Yes = Evidence exists from coaching studies that included this dimension, though not always tested in isolation
  • Modest = Small but real effect found in meta-analysis (d=0.378 means a modest improvement)
  • Context-only = Works only when practiced in real-world situations, not through generic brain-training games
  • Indirect = Improves as a side effect of working on other dimensions (not from direct targeting)
  • Consensus = Recommended by experienced clinicians (Dawson & Guare and others) but not yet tested in research studies
  • No = Meta-analytic evidence against this working (three studies, 3,000+ people, null result)

Key Insights

  • Working memory is compensate-only. Three meta-analyses, N > 3,000, zero far-transfer. Give students external memory tools and simplify the environment.
  • Time management and organization are Priority 1 for coach surfacing. RCT-level support with gains sustained at 3 months. Top pain points for working adults.
  • Emotional regulation improves indirectly when daily operations stabilize. Coach TM and Org first; ER often follows.
  • Physical activity is the one cross-cutting intervention with meta-analytic support (g=0.56) across multiple dimensions.
Honest gap: The Dawson & Guare ACS framework itself has not been validated in randomized trials. It rests on clinical consensus and widespread practitioner use.
Section 6 of 15

Trauma-Informed Coaching

Report E · 24 papers · "Stabilize first, and know your lane"

Trauma creates a distinct EF pattern

ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences) shrink the prefrontal cortex and expand the amygdala. This selectively impairs inhibitory control and cognitive flexibility while sparing working memory. Compare to financial stress (Report C), which impairs WM and CF but enhances response inhibition. Different stressor, different EF pattern.

PCRR Framework Actionable coaching moves for non-clinicians

Protect (establish safety) → Connect (build relationship) → Respect (honor the student's experience) → Redirect (guide toward skill-building). Maps naturally to the RISE cycle's proposed triage step.

Stabilize first, then build

This principle appeared in every report. The trauma literature adds the biological grounding: a dysregulated stress response system literally cannot support new skill acquisition until baseline safety is established.

  • Emotion regulation is the teachable buffer. Building regulation capacity reduces the downstream impact of trauma on other EF dimensions.
  • ER improves indirectly when time management and daily operations stabilize (convergence with Report D).

Scope of practice: the critical gap

When does a coach say "this is beyond what I can help with"? The literature has practitioner-generated guidelines but no empirical validation.

Coach View language: An "Adaptive Pattern Detected" banner signals trauma-informed sequencing may be needed without using clinical terms like "trauma" or "PTSD." Clear guidance: coach the EF skills, don't treat the trauma.

Cross-Cultural Factors in Coaching

Report F · 27 papers · "Relatedness first, and don't generalize"

SDT works across cultures, but the entry point matters

Autonomy, relatedness, and competence matter across cultures[12] (1,349 citations across 5 countries). But for Black and Latinx working adults, relatedness is the gateway need. Until a student feels genuinely connected to their coach, autonomy-supportive and competence-building moves don't land.

Surprising Demographic match is overrated

Students prefer coaches who share their background (d=0.63 preference effect), but actual outcome effect is tiny (d=0.09). What predicts outcomes: perceived cultural competence (r=0.38 as rated by the client). Coach self-rated competence doesn't predict anything (r=0.06).

This means coach training beats demographic matching. It's a training investment, not a hiring filter.

4x Group-specific > generic multicultural

Culturally adapted interventions outperform generic ones by d=0.45 to 0.50[13] (1,178 citations; 76 studies). Group-specific adaptations are four times more effective than generic multicultural approaches. NLU should differentiate guidance for Black vs. Latinx learners.

The RISE Coaching Cycle

Synthesis across all 6 reports · "The framework that connects every finding"

The RISE framework — Recognize → Identify → Strategize → Evaluate — is the coaching cycle that ties together the individual report findings into a single workflow. Each phase is grounded in SDT's dual-pathway model: need satisfaction feeds growth; need frustration activates defensiveness.[14]

R

Recognize

Review the student's 10-dimension EF profile and motivational state simultaneously (Report A). Check for stress signatures (Report C), trauma-related patterns (Report E), and cultural context (Report F). Listen for motivation quality: "I want to" vs. "I should."[15]

I

Identify

Name the pattern — both the EF challenge and its context. Use the ACS typology (Report D) to classify: is this a dimension to accommodate, compensate, or strengthen? Frame in strengths-first language (Report B). Let the student choose their entry point to preserve autonomy.[16]

S

Strategize

Match strategies to both the EF need and the motivational state.[3] A student under controlled motivation should not be asked to "strengthen" — start with accommodate/compensate, build autonomous motivation, then strengthen. Under financial stress, resource navigation may outperform EF training (Report C).

E

Evaluate

Track EF skill use, motivation quality, and need satisfaction together.[14] Use 3-session rolling averages, not snapshots. Normalize non-linear progress. For students under high stress, maintaining current functioning is progress (Report E).

The Proposed Triage Step

All six reports converge on a pre-RISE check: before entering Recognize, assess whether the student is in a state where coaching can land.

Section 9 of 15

Five Cross-Cutting Themes

1. The Coaching Relationship Is the Foundation

Across all six reports, the quality of the coach-student relationship is not background context. It is an active intervention. Relatedness satisfaction replenishes regulatory resources (A), asset-based framing shapes outcomes through expectancy effects (B), emotional connection buffers the cognitive cost of stress (C), and cultural competence determines whether coaching lands (F).

2. State vs. Trait: Most EF Profiles Are a Mix

Financial stress accounts for 0.2 to 0.3 SD of apparent EF weakness (C). Motivational state shapes EF performance (A). Some dimensions fluctuate with life circumstances (D). Trauma creates its own pattern (E). First assessments are snapshots, not verdicts.

3. The RISE Cycle Needs a "Triage" Step

Before Recognize → Identify → Strategize → Evaluate, the coach should check: Is this student in acute need frustration? Is financial stress distorting the profile? Does trauma history call for different sequencing? Is the profile being read through a deficit lens? The PCRR framework (E) maps naturally onto this triage.

4. "Stabilize First, Then Build" Is Universal

This emerged independently in every report: address need frustration before skill-building (A), lead with strengths before growth areas (B), navigate resources before training EF (C), accommodate before strengthen (D), establish safety before coaching (E), build relatedness before autonomy (F). Same principle, six different framings.

5. Differentiate, Don't Generalize

The 10 EF dimensions respond differently to different intervention types (D). Culturally adapted coaching is 4x more effective than generic (F). Trauma-adapted EF profiles look different from stress-influenced ones (E vs. C). The Coach View should help coaches see the specific pattern and choose matched moves.

Coach Training: What the Evidence Prioritizes

Report F's strongest finding: coach training on cultural competence matters more than demographic matching. This is a training investment, not a hiring filter. Here is what the evidence says coaches need to learn, in priority order:

Priority 1 Strengths-first profile reading

Practitioner perception bias is measured. Train coaches to read the strengths section first, then approach growth areas as developmental edges. The SUDCO model (Report B) and Lopez[6] perception bias research ground this.

Priority 1 Motivation quality monitoring

Train coaches to detect the "I should" to "I want to" language shift. When EF effort becomes controlled motivation, the effort is depleting and unsustainable (Report A). The move is not "more structure" but "return to autonomy support."

Priority 2 Stress-context interpretation

Train coaches to ask about current stressors before interpreting EF scores. Financial stress depresses WM and CF by 0.2-0.3 SD (Report C). Resource navigation may produce larger gains than EF training.

Priority 2 Group-specific cultural competence

Not generic "cultural sensitivity" training. Specific guidance on relatedness-building, mental health referral language, and stigma patterns for Black vs. Latinx learners (Report F). Client-perceived competence (r=0.38) is what predicts outcomes.

Priority 3 RISE cycle fluency and trauma-informed triage

Train coaches on the full RISE workflow: Recognize the EF + motivation pattern, Identify the ACS approach, Strategize with matched moves, Evaluate both skill use and motivation quality. Include the pre-RISE triage check and scope-of-practice guardrails (Report E).

The universal principle across all 6 reports: Stabilize first, then build. Every coaching relationship starts with connection, safety, and context before moving to skill development.

For Discussion: What Resonates?

This is a starting point, not a finished product · We want your practitioner lens on what the research got right, got wrong, or missed

Motivation and EF Together

Slide 3 describes how motivation and executive function interact. The biggest research finding is that you get better results working on both at the same time — not "fix motivation first, then build skills." One study even found that just working on motivation improved attention as a side effect. In your coaching, do you see students where motivation and skill struggles feel like one problem, not two? What does that look like in a session? (See Slide 3 for the full picture.)

Strengths-First Language

Slide 4 shows a table of language reframes for how we describe a student's profile. For example, instead of saying "weakness in working memory" the Coach View would say "growing edge: holding multiple pieces of information." The idea is that how the coach reads the profile changes what the coach expects, and expectations shape outcomes. Do those reframes feel natural in a coaching conversation? What language do you actually use when a student is struggling with something? (See Slide 4 for the full table.)

Stress: It's the Situation, Not the Student

Slide 5 shows that financial stress measurably impairs specific thinking skills — and that most of the impairment goes away when the stressor lifts. The same person scores differently depending on what's happening in their life. Your students are also working full-time, going to school, and often caring for others — so financial stress is just one layer. When you see a student struggling, how often is it "who they are" vs. "what they're dealing with right now"? How do you tell the difference? (See Slide 5 for the stress × EF data.)

Emotional Regulation

Slide 6 lists emotional regulation as a "Priority 2" dimension to surface in the Coach View. But most of the research on emotional regulation comes from K-12, where it tends to mean "being calm" or "staying neutral." We think for working adults juggling jobs, school, and family, emotional regulation might be more about staying functional under pressure — not being emotionless. How would you describe what good emotional regulation looks like for your students? (See Slide 6, EF Spectrum table.)

New Dimensions in the Coach View

Right now the Coach View shows three dimensions: working memory, inhibition, and cognitive flexibility (the "Engine" from the May 5 mockup). Slide 6 shows that the research supports surfacing more — especially time management and organization, which have the strongest evidence for coaching interventions. If those showed up as new dimensions, would that help you prepare for a session? What else would you want to see? (See Slide 6 for the full 10-dimension evidence table.)
