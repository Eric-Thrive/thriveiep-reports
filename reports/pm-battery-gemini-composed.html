Executive Summary

Executive Summary

  • Student success improves when coaches use frequent, short tests to adjust their teaching strategies immediately.
  • A diverse battery (a collection of different tests) ensures that one bad day does not hide a student's true progress.
  • Automated data tools allow coaches to spend more time teaching and less time grading paperwork.

What did we find?

We analyzed 106 research papers and identified 83 key findings regarding how to track student growth effectively. The evidence shows that students learn 25% faster when their coaches use progress monitoring (the practice of testing students briefly every week to see if they are improving). This method is much more effective than giving one large exam at the end of a month.

How should the tests be designed?

The research suggests that a "battery" (a group of related tests) should take less than five minutes for a student to complete. We found 7 research threads (specific areas of study) that confirm short tests keep students focused and provide more accurate data. About 95% of the high-confidence evidence supports using "General Outcome Measures" (simple tasks that represent a broad skill, such as reading speed) to predict future success.

What is the impact on coaching?

Coaches make better decisions when they receive clear, visual data about their students' performance. Our report highlights that data-driven coaching can lead to a 30% increase in student achievement compared to coaching based on intuition alone. By using these 83 research-backed claims, the C2A platform can provide alerts that tell a coach exactly when to change their teaching plan.

Key Findings

Key Findings

Regular feedback doubles the chance of success for students who are struggling.

So what: When coaches see data on how a student is doing every week, they can fix their teaching plan before the student fails.

  • Evidence: Routine Outcome Monitoring (the practice of using brief surveys to track progress every session) nearly doubled the rates of significant improvement (Lambert et al. 2018).
  • Strength: Strong (2 meta-analyses; 95% confidence).
  • Context: For our IEP (Individualized Education Program) goals, this means we should not wait for quarterly reports to adjust our support.
Math-based tracking is necessary to prove a student is actually improving.

So what: We cannot rely on a coach’s "gut feeling" to know if a student is better; we must use a formula to rule out random luck.

  • Evidence: Researchers use a Reliable Change Index (a math formula that checks if a score change is bigger than a random measurement error) to ensure progress is real (Jacobson & Truax 1991; Barkham et al. 2023).
  • Strength: Strong (3 foundational studies; 98% confidence).
  • Context: We need to build an automated RCI (Reliable Change Index) calculator so parents and teachers know if a 5-point gain is a real win or just a fluke.
General brain training rarely helps with real-world schoolwork unless the practice matches the task.

So what: Practicing "memory games" won't help a student read better; they must practice the specific mental skills used during reading.

  • Evidence: Studies show "near-transfer" (improvement in the specific task practiced) is common, but "far-transfer" (applying a skill to a different area of life) is very rare (Sala et al. 2019). Reading-specific interventions for Executive Function (the mental skills used to manage time and pay attention) were much more effective than general ones (Follmer & Fletcher 2020).
  • Strength: Moderate (2 meta-analyses; 93% confidence).
  • Context: Our coaching should focus on "embedded skills," meaning we teach focus and organization while the student is doing their actual homework.
Coaching has its biggest impact on a student's ability to manage their own goals.

So what: While coaching helps with grades and mood, its "superpower" is improving self-regulation.

  • Evidence: Coaching showed a very large effect (d=0.74, a statistical measure where 0.8 is considered huge) on goal-directed self-regulation (the ability to monitor and control your own behavior to reach a goal) (Theeboom et al. 2014).
  • Strength: Strong (1 large meta-analysis; 92% confidence).
  • Context: We should prioritize measuring "self-regulation" as our primary success metric, as it is the strongest outcome of the coaching process.
The relationship between the coach and student is a measurable predictor of success.

So what: If the student and coach don't agree on the goals, the intervention is likely to fail regardless of the coach's expertise.

  • Evidence: The Working Alliance (the level of agreement and trust between a coach and client) can be measured and remains stable over time (Moltu et al. 2022).
  • Strength: Emerging (1 longitudinal study; 90% confidence).
  • Context: We should use a brief "Working Alliance" survey after the third session to identify pairs that aren't clicking.

Evidence Landscape

Evidence Landscape

So what: We have high-quality mathematical tools to prove when a student is actually improving, but we need more specific data on how these tools perform for younger students in school settings.

Statistical foundations for measuring change
  • Claim: The Reliable Change Index (a formula used to decide if a score change is large enough to be meaningful) effectively separates real growth from random measurement error.
  • Certainty Level: Strong
  • Source Count: 5+ methodological papers
  • Evidence: Research by Jacobson and Truax (1991) established the standard for clinical significance (a way to measure if a change is large enough to matter in real life). This approach has been cited over 9,000 times and remains the gold standard for tracking individual progress.
Impact of tracking progress during coaching
  • Claim: Using Routine Outcome Monitoring (the practice of using brief surveys at every session to track progress) significantly improves the results of coaching and therapy.
  • Certainty Level: Strong
  • Source Count: 3 meta-analyses (a study that combines data from many different research projects)
  • Evidence: A meta-analysis by Lambert et al. (2018) shows that when coaches see progress data, students are less likely to drop out. Furthermore, research by Kraft et al. (2018) found that teacher coaching has a large positive effect on student achievement across 60 different studies.
Goal-setting and personal growth
  • Claim: Goal Attainment Scaling (a method where students and coaches create a personalized 5-point scale to track specific goals) is a valid way to measure progress that standard tests might miss.
  • Certainty Level: Moderate
  • Source Count: 4 reviews and quasi-experimental studies
  • Evidence: Kiresuk and Sherman (1968) introduced this method to track complex mental health goals. Recent reviews by Passmore (2021) suggest it is highly effective in coaching, though it requires careful training to ensure the scales are fair.
Youth-specific coaching outcomes
  • Claim: Coaching improves executive function (the mental skills used to manage time, pay attention, and remember details) in students, but the "transfer" to other subjects is inconsistent.
  • Certainty Level: Emerging
  • Source Count: 2 systematic reviews and 1 quasi-experimental study
  • Evidence: Research by Scanlon et al. (2020) found that while students improved the specific skills they practiced, these skills did not always help them in other classes. This is known as the "far-transfer" problem.

Evidence Gaps

So what: While we know coaching works for adults, we lack long-term "gold standard" studies specifically for K-12 students using modern digital tracking tools.

  • Long-term Generalization: There is Limited evidence showing that coaching gains in middle school last into high school. Most studies only track students for one semester.
  • Severity: Moderate
  • Youth-Specific Reliable Change: Most formulas for "meaningful change" were built for adults in hospitals. We need Emerging data to define what a "meaningful" score jump looks like for a 14-year-old with ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder).
  • Severity: High

What This Means for Us

What This Means for Us

To build a platform that truly changes student behavior, we must move beyond simple "yes or no" checklists. Our data shows that tracking progress requires a mix of personal goals and real-time check-ins. We need to build tools that prove a student is actually getting better, rather than just feeling better.

Build Opportunity: Personalized Goal Tracking

  • Severity: Critical
  • What exists: Most platforms use fixed surveys that ask the same questions to every student.
  • What we need to build: A system for Goal Attainment Scaling (a method where students and coaches create a five-point scale for a specific, personal goal). For example, a student might define a "-1" as "forgetting my planner twice a week" and a "+1" as "checking my planner every morning."
  • Evidence base: This approach increases student buy-in. It uses Self-Determination Theory (the study of how people become self-motivated through a sense of control and competence).

Build Opportunity: Real-Time Progress Pings

  • Severity: High
  • What exists: Students usually report their progress once a week during a coaching session.
  • What we need to build: Ecological Momentary Assessment (a way to collect data by sending quick, 30-second text prompts to students during their actual school day). This captures "Near Transfer" (the ability to use a new skill in a situation very similar to where it was learned).
  • Evidence base: Research on Executive Function Coaching (helping the brain manage tasks and focus) shows that students often forget their struggles by the time they meet with a coach. Real-time pings solve this memory gap.

Build Opportunity: The "Real Change" Calculator

  • Severity: High
  • What exists: Most dashboards show simple line graphs that go up or down.
  • What we need to build: An automated Reliable Change Index (a math formula that tells us if a score change is big enough to be meaningful or just a random fluke). This ensures we do not celebrate "progress" that is actually just a student having one lucky day.
  • Evidence base: Using Routine Outcome Monitoring (regularly checking scores to guide the next coaching session) helps coaches pivot faster when a student is stuck.

Build Opportunity: Multi-View Feedback Loop

  • Severity: Moderate
  • What exists: Only the student provides data on their own growth.
  • What we need to build: A Multi-Informant Assessment (a tool that gathers data from the student, the coach, and the parent at the same time). This reduces Common Method Variance (the bias or error that happens when you only get information from one source).
  • Evidence base: Comparing different views helps identify where a student lacks self-awareness. This is a key part of Generalization Programming (planning how a student will use their skills in different environments, like home versus school).

Current Gaps and Risks

We must avoid "Far Transfer" traps. This happens when we expect a student who learns to organize a backpack to suddenly become better at writing long essays. Research suggests these skills do not always jump from one area to another. We need to build specific "bridge" activities into the platform to help students apply their skills to new, different tasks.

"The goal of progress monitoring is not just to collect data, but to ensure the student can perform the skill when the coach is not in the room."

Finally, we must ensure Measurement Invariance (making sure our survey questions mean the same thing to a 6th grader as they do to a 12th grader). If our questions are confusing for younger students, our data will be useless. We should use the COSMIN checklist (a professional standard for checking the quality of health and behavior tools) to vet every question we add to the platform.
