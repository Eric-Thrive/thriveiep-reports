Progress Monitoring Battery Research

148 sources across 7 research threads, investigating how to build a change-sensitive progress monitoring battery aligned to C2A's T1 v6trans trait profiling battery. Population: working adults in NLU's LE3 accelerated degree program.

The Core Problem

The T1 battery (17 instruments, 123 items) was designed for stability and reliability -- exactly what you want for profiling. But progress monitoring requires instruments selected for responsiveness to intervention, not factor loadings. Items that show change do not correspond to items grouped by factor analysis (Kopta 1994, Collins 1991). This is a structural mismatch, not a sensitivity problem.

What the Field Does (and Doesn't Do)

Most coaching platforms measure goal completion and engagement, not psychometric change. BetterUp is the exception -- they explicitly separate change-amenable behavioral capabilities from stable traits. Therapy's Routine Outcome Monitoring (ROM) framework is the most evidence-backed pattern: brief session measures with "not on track" algorithmic alerting reduces deterioration by 67% and doubles recovery rates (Lambert 2018).

EF is the Mechanism, Not the Outcome

C2A coaching uses EF as the intervention mechanism -- the DDx engine diagnoses EF constraints (WM, Inhibition, CF) and prescribes EF-targeted tools (compensation, scaffolds). But the outcome we measure is whether the durable skill improves behaviorally. Kassai (2019): abstract EF training doesn't generalize (g=0.11). But Cartwright (2020): contextualized EF training -- exactly what C2A's DDx prescribes -- DOES transfer. PM should measure initiative, communication, collaboration behaviors, not EF itself.

Key Findings

The 11 most important findings for C2A progress monitoring battery design, ranked by impact.

1 Trait instruments cannot serve as progress monitors
Structural Collins 1991, Kopta 1994
▼
Factor-analytically derived items cluster by shared variance, not by sensitivity to change. The SCL-90-R showed items cluster into three change-rate categories (acute, chronic, characterological) that do NOT correspond to factor structure.
Design implication: Item selection for the PM battery must be based on responsiveness to intervention, not construct overlap with T1 instruments. This means building new items informed by the T1 subdomain definitions but optimized for detecting change.
2 EF is the mechanism, not the outcome -- measure durable skill behaviors
Meta-analysis Kassai 2019, Cartwright 2020
▼
Abstract EF training: near-transfer g=0.44, far-transfer g=0.11 (non-significant) (Kassai 2019). But Cartwright et al. (2020) showed contextualized EF training embedded in real tasks DOES transfer. C2A's DDx prescriptions ARE contextualized -- they coach EF within the durable skill context (e.g., "use a written plan to support initiative"), which is exactly the condition where transfer works.
Critical reframe: EF measures (ADEXI, C2A-CF) belong in T1 only -- they profile the mechanism the DDx uses to select interventions. PM should NOT re-measure EF. Instead, PM measures the durable skill behavior that coaching is designed to improve: "How often did you start a task using a written plan?" (initiative), not "How often did you forget instructions?" (working memory).
Analogy: Physical therapy measures walking speed (functional outcome), not joint angle (intervention target). C2A should measure initiative behavior (outcome), not working memory capacity (mechanism).
3 Coaching effect sizes are moderate and domain-specific
Consensus Theeboom 2013, Kraft 2018
▼
Self-regulation: d=0.74 | Performance/skills: d=0.60 | Instruction quality: d=0.49 | Academic achievement: d=0.18
Behavioral outcomes respond more than stable traits. Self-regulation and behavioral skills are the primary PM targets.
Design implication: Instruments must detect d=0.18-0.74. Plan for COSMIN's rule: Minimal Important Change must exceed Smallest Detectable Change (Terwee 2006, 10,551 cites).
4 ROM from psychotherapy is the most transferable framework
Framework Lambert 2018, Lutz 2022
▼
ROM with feedback to practitioners reduces deterioration by 67% and nearly doubles reliable change rates for at-risk clients. Architecture: short self-report every session + empirically-based NOT (not-on-track) decision rules.
Key instruments: ORS (4 items, <1 min), CORE-10 (10 items), OI-21 (21 items with responsiveness data). The SFSS tracked 1,906 youth with multilevel modeling for expected change trajectories.
5 RCI methodology needs careful implementation
Methods Jacobson & Truax 1991 (9,861 cites)
▼
Hageman-Arrindell method produces fewer false positives/negatives when practice effects exist (critical for repeated measures). IRT-based RCI with empirical priors improves individual change detection. Multi-timepoint RCI via multilevel models is directly applicable to C2A's multi-sprint design (Morgan-Lopez 2022).
6 Generalization requires active programming and distinct measurement
Theory Stokes & Baer 1977, Baldwin & Ford 1988
▼
Stokes & Baer (3,235 cites): Nine categories of generalization programming. Baldwin & Ford (3,197 cites): Generalization (new contexts) and maintenance (durability) are distinct dimensions requiring separate measurement.
For C2A's transfer gradient: Implementation and consistency = maintenance. Narrow benefit = near-transfer. Broad transfer = far-transfer. Each level needs different items with different context specificity.
7 Frequency items outperform agreement items for PM
Empirical PMC 2014 (N=1,017 IRT study)
▼
"How often do you..." items have higher reliability and better information in the normal range than "I tend to..." items. Agreement items perform better only at extremes. For working adults not at clinical extremes, frequency format is the optimal PM design choice.
8 Multi-method is non-negotiable against method variance
Critical Podsakoff 2003 (73,601 cites)
▼
Self-report-only designs inflate apparent change through common method variance. The PM battery needs at least one non-self-report source. Best options: GAS (coach + student) and coach behavioral observation. Coach-student rating discrepancies are informative, not error (De Los Reyes 2005).
9 RTI/MTSS decision rules transfer to coaching
Education ERIC education research
▼
Every-other-week monitoring preserves accuracy vs. weekly (validates 3-week cadence). Nonlinear growth breaks linear decision rules through 16 weeks (use piecewise models). Probe reliability gates decision quality (establish minimum thresholds). Brief rating scales can be purpose-built from validated item pools.
10 Measurement invariance is feasible across populations
Methods Cheung & Rensvold 2002 (15,129 cites)
▼
Delta-CFI <= 0.01 is the criterion. Longitudinal invariance of WAI-S confirmed across coaching sessions (N=690). DIF analysis via IRT and logistic regression is the template for comparing working adults vs. traditional students. R code tutorial available (ERIC).
11 EMA detects change as well as retrospective measures
Empirical JMIR 2025, 4 trials (N=412)
▼
EMA at 3x/day achieves 72-80% compliance in 3-4 week interventions. Sensitivity to change comparable to or better than retrospective measures. Best for mood/state constructs; less tested for complex competencies. Self-efficacy shows within-person micro-temporal fluctuations detectable by EMA (cutting-edge dual-process theory).

Recommended PM Architecture

A three-tier measurement model that measures at different frequencies depending on how much time the student has. Draws from three evidence bases explained below.

Background: Where These Ideas Come From
Three fields inform this architecture
▼
Routine Outcome Monitoring (ROM) comes from psychotherapy research. The idea: give clients a very brief questionnaire at every session, track their scores over time, and alert the therapist when someone is falling behind expected progress. A meta-analysis by Lambert et al. (2018) showed this simple practice reduces client deterioration by 67% and doubles recovery rates. We want to adapt this for coaching.
RTI/MTSS progress monitoring comes from K-12 special education. Response to Intervention (RTI) and Multi-Tiered Systems of Support (MTSS) are frameworks where schools give students brief, frequent assessments to decide if an intervention is working. The key finding: you don't need to measure every week -- every-other-week or every 3 weeks works just as well for making good decisions (ERIC research). Growth isn't always linear, so the system needs to handle curved trajectories.
Multi-informant assessment means getting more than one perspective. When only the student self-reports, you get a single viewpoint that can be biased by how they're feeling that day or by wanting to look good. Adding a coach's perspective (even a quick rating) gives a second data point. Research by De Los Reyes & Kazdin (2005) showed that when the student and coach disagree, the disagreement itself is meaningful -- it tells you something about the student's self-awareness or the coaching relationship.
Key Terms Glossary
Abbreviations used in the architecture below
▼
ORS (Outcome Rating Scale) -- A 4-item visual slider questionnaire that takes under 1 minute. The client marks where they are on four dimensions (personal well-being, close relationships, social life, overall). Originally from therapy; we'd adapt the dimensions to match coaching goals. Validated in 3+ randomized trials with 21,000+ patients.
GAS (Goal Attainment Scaling) -- A method where the coach and student define a personalized goal, then rate progress on a 5-point scale from -2 (much worse than expected) to +2 (much better than expected), with 0 = expected outcome. The power of GAS is that every student can have different goals, but you can still compare and aggregate scores across students because the scale is standardized.
NOT (Not-On-Track) alerting -- An algorithm that compares a student's actual progress to their expected trajectory. If they fall below the expected curve, the system flags them for the coach's attention. This is the mechanism behind ROM's effectiveness -- it catches students who are struggling before they drop out or disengage.
RCI (Reliable Change Index) -- A statistical formula (Jacobson & Truax, 1991) that answers: "Did this student change more than you'd expect from measurement error alone?" If the RCI exceeds 1.96, you can be 95% confident the change is real, not just noise. The Hageman-Arrindell variant is better when students take similar measures repeatedly, because it accounts for practice effects (people scoring slightly better just because they've seen the questions before).
DIF (Differential Item Functioning) -- A statistical test that checks whether a question works the same way for different groups of people. For example: does "I take initiative at work" mean the same thing and have the same difficulty for a 35-year-old working adult vs. a 20-year-old traditional student? If not, that item has DIF and needs to be revised or scored differently for each group.
MIC / SDC (COSMIN framework) -- MIC is the Minimal Important Change: the smallest score change that actually matters to the student (not just statistically detectable). SDC is the Smallest Detectable Change: the smallest change the instrument can reliably detect given its measurement error. The rule: MIC must be larger than SDC, otherwise your instrument can't distinguish real meaningful change from noise. COSMIN is the international consensus framework that defines these standards.
Behavioral frequency items -- Questions that ask "In the past 2 weeks, how often did you [specific action]?" with response options like Never / Once / A few times / Weekly / Daily. These are more change-sensitive than agreement items ("I am the kind of person who...") because they anchor to a specific time window and ask about concrete countable actions rather than general self-concept.
Tier 1: Every Coaching Session

Student Quick Check-In

Every coaching session | Under 2 minutes
  • Adapted ORS -- 4-6 slider items rating how things are going in areas relevant to the student's coaching goals. Takes under a minute. Tracked over time to create a progress curve.
  • "Overall, how much have things changed?" -- A single question asking the student to rate their overall sense of progress since coaching started (1-5 scale). Simple but surprisingly effective as a progress signal.
  • NOT alerting -- The system compares the student's scores against their expected trajectory. If they're falling behind, the coach gets a flag before the next session so they can adjust their approach.

Coach Quick Rating

Every session | Under 1 minute
  • GAS rating per goal -- After each session, the coach rates: "Compared to what I expected, how is this student progressing on their primary goal?" on the -2 to +2 scale. Takes 15 seconds per goal.
  • Skill observation -- Brief coach note on whether they observed the student demonstrating the target durable skill behavior during the session (yes/no/partially + optional 1-line note).
  • When student and coach disagree -- If the student rates progress as high but the coach rates it as low (or vice versa), that discrepancy is surfaced as a conversation starter, not treated as an error.
Tier 2: Every 3 Weeks (Sprint Boundary)

Targeted Behavioral Frequency Check

Every 3 weeks | About 10 minutes
  • Only the skills being coached -- If the DDx identified Initiative and Communication as this student's focus areas, they only answer questions about initiative and communication behaviors. Not all 12 skills.
  • Behavioral frequency questions -- "In the past 2 weeks, how often did you [specific action the coach recommended]?" These are concrete and countable, anchored to the DDx intervention.
  • Adaptive by DDx state -- A student in State 7 (EF Blocker) gets items about tool adoption ("How often did you use a written plan?"). A student in State 1 (Direct Skill Building) gets items about the skill behavior itself ("How often did you start a new task without being asked?").
  • Growth modeling -- Scores are tracked on a curve, not assumed to be a straight line. Research shows assuming linear growth leads to wrong decisions by week 16. The system uses piecewise models that allow for fast initial gains that plateau.

Goal Progress Review

Every 3 weeks | About 3 minutes
  • Expanded GAS -- The student and coach each independently rate progress on each active goal using the -2 to +2 scale, with personalized descriptions at each level written during goal-setting.
  • Transfer gradient check -- For each goal, the student answers: (1) Am I doing the recommended action? (2) Am I doing it consistently, not just sometimes? (3) Is it actually helping with the target skill? (4) Am I noticing improvement in other areas too?
  • Comparable across different goals -- Because GAS uses the same -2 to +2 scale regardless of what the goal is, you can aggregate and compare scores across students who have completely different coaching targets. A student working on initiative and a student working on communication both produce comparable progress numbers.
Tier 3: Major Timepoints (T2 at 6-8 Weeks, T3 at End of Semester)

Full Progress Monitoring Battery

T2 (mid-semester, 6-8 weeks) and T3 (end of semester, ~15 weeks) | About 15 minutes
  • All 12 durable skills -- Behavioral frequency items covering every skill, not just the coached ones. This is where we detect broad transfer: are uncoached skills improving too?
  • ~51-53 items total -- Short enough to complete in one sitting. Every item uses "In the past 2 weeks, how often did you..." format.
  • Re-administer BMPN-18 and GMS-Bookends -- These two instruments from the T1 battery already measure state-level constructs (psychological need satisfaction and motivation). Re-administering them tells us whether the coaching environment is supporting or undermining the student's basic needs.
  • Optional: coach behavioral observation -- A structured rating form where the coach rates the student's behavioral skills across all 12 domains, providing a second perspective beyond self-report.
  • Statistical analysis -- For each subscale, calculate the Reliable Change Index to determine whether this individual student's change is real (exceeds measurement error). Use the Hageman-Arrindell variant to account for practice effects from repeated measurement.
  • Population fairness check -- Run DIF analysis to verify that items work equivalently for working adults and traditional-age students. If an item functions differently across groups, flag it for revision.

Quality Gates

Checked at each measurement point
  • ☑ Reliability check -- Are the scores consistent enough to make decisions from? If internal consistency is too low, flag the subscale.
  • ☑ Effort/validity check -- Did the student engage genuinely? Detect straight-lining (same answer for everything), impossibly fast completion, or random-looking patterns.
  • ☑ MIC exceeds SDC -- Can this instrument actually detect changes that matter? If the smallest meaningful change is smaller than the instrument's noise floor, the instrument isn't useful for progress monitoring.
  • ☑ Cross-wave invariance -- Does the instrument measure the same thing at T2 as it did at T1? If the meaning of items drifts over time (e.g., students interpret "take initiative" differently after coaching), apparent change could be an artifact.
How the Three Tiers Work Together
Each tier answers a different question at a different timescale
▼
Session-level answers: "Is this student still engaged and making subjective progress?" It's an early warning system. If a student's ORS scores drop two sessions in a row, the coach knows something changed before the next formal assessment.
Sprint-level answers: "Is the student actually doing the recommended behaviors, and are those behaviors producing change in the targeted skill?" This is where the DDx intervention meets measurement. If the coach recommended using written plans (for a WM-constrained student), are they using them? And is their initiative actually improving as a result?
Timepoint-level answers: "Across the full semester, did coaching produce statistically reliable change in durable skill behaviors? Did improvement generalize beyond the coached domains?" This is the summative evaluation that feeds back into the DDx for the next coaching cycle and provides outcome data for the program.
The data flows up: Session data feeds the NOT algorithm. Sprint data feeds growth trajectory models. Timepoint data feeds RCI calculations and the program-level effectiveness report. At no point do we re-measure EF -- the T1 EF profile persists as the diagnostic context that explains why the student's coaching plan looks the way it does.

Build Opportunities

Where no adequate instrument exists. These are custom development items informed by rigorous methods from adjacent fields.

DDx-Aligned Durable Skill Behavioral Frequency Battery

Severity: Critical. The PM battery's core: 12 durable skills x 3-4 behavioral frequency items each (~36-48 items). Each item measures the observable behavioral outcome of coaching, NOT the EF mechanism. Items should be contextualized to the DDx intervention (e.g., for Initiative State 7/WM Blocker: "In the past 2 weeks, how often did you start a task using a written plan or checklist?"). Include both coached-context items and novel-context items to detect the transfer gradient. Can be partially adaptive -- DDx state determines which items a student receives at sprint-level.

Paper Browser

Clinical significance: A statistical approach to defining meaningful change
Jacobson & Truax, 1991 | 9,861 cites SS + OA
▼
Established the Reliable Change Index (RCI) formula: RCI = (X2-X1) / Sdiff, where Sdiff = sqrt(2(SE)^2). Individual change exceeding 1.96 * Sdiff is "reliable" at p<.05. Foundational method for determining whether coaching produced real change for an individual student.
Common method biases in behavioral research
Podsakoff et al., 2003 | 73,601 cites OA
▼
Self-report measures carry substantial shared method variance. Remedies: temporal separation of predictor and criterion measures, obtaining measures from different sources (multi-informant), and using different response formats. Directly supports multi-method PM architecture.
Evaluating goodness-of-fit indexes for testing measurement invariance
Cheung & Rensvold, 2002 | 15,129 cites OA
▼
Established delta-CFI <= 0.01 as the criterion for measurement invariance. Essential for confirming PM battery measures the same constructs equivalently across working adults and traditional-age students.
Quality criteria for health status questionnaires: responsiveness
Terwee et al., 2006 | 10,551 cites OA
▼
Minimal Important Change (MIC) must exceed the Smallest Detectable Change (SDC) for a measure to be useful for progress monitoring. This is the psychometric bar every C2A PM instrument must clear.
Near- and far-transfer effects among children's executive function
Kassai et al., 2019 | 231 cites SS
▼
Meta-analysis: EF training near-transfer g=0.44 (significant), far-transfer g=0.11 (p=.11). PM battery must track specific subdomain coached, not general constructs.
Does coaching work? A meta-analysis on individual-level effects
Theeboom et al., 2013 | 707 cites OA
▼
Self-regulation shows the largest coaching effect (d=0.74), followed by performance/skills (d=0.60). These should be the primary progress monitoring targets.
Collecting and delivering progress feedback: A meta-analysis of ROM
Lambert et al., 2018 | 324 cites SS
▼
ROM with feedback reduces deterioration rates and nearly doubles reliable change for at-risk clients. Architecture: brief session measures + empirically-based NOT alerting.
An implicit technology of generalization
Stokes & Baer, 1977 | 3,235 cites OA
▼
Nine categories of generalization programming. Generalization does not happen automatically -- it must be designed into the intervention and separately measured.
Goal Attainment Scaling: A general method for evaluating community programs
Kiresuk & Sherman, 1968 | 1,944 cites OA
▼
GAS uses a -2 to +2 scale with individualized goal anchors. Enables aggregation and comparison across individuals with different goals. The key bridge between idiosyncratic coaching goals and quantitative progress data.
Ecological momentary assessment
Shiffman, Stone & Hufford, 2007 | 6,044 cites OA
▼
EMA minimizes recall bias and captures within-person variability in real time. Reveals behavioral trajectories invisible to pre/post measurement.

Evidence Strength Assessment

Strong: Psychometric methods (RCI, COSMIN, MTMM, DIF) -- foundational papers with 1,000-73,000+ citations.
Moderate: Coaching outcomes (meta-analyses exist), ROM (therapy RCTs), GAS (rehabilitation).
Thin: Progress monitoring battery design for coaching specifically. Construct alignment between trait and behavioral measures. Cross-population invariance for coaching instruments. These are build opportunities, not evidence gaps to be filled by more searching.
