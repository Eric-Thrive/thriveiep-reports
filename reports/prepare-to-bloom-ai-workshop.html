Too much context

The model loses the thread, blends details, or gives a vague answer that sounds polished but shallow.

Not enough context

The model misses your philosophy, your practice standards, and what actually matters in the case.

Generic AI often fails because there is both too much and not enough context.
Directly aligned with the Shayna check-in framing

Speaker Notes

"How many of you have tried pasting a long document into ChatGPT and gotten something back that felt vaguely right but not actually useful?" Too much context: the model blends details and gives a polished but shallow answer. Not enough: it doesn’t know BBI, IECA standards, or what ‘least restrictive environment’ means in practice. "This is the paradox. Generic AI fails because there’s simultaneously too much and not enough context."

Slide 9 of 17

The pipeline matters

Step 1

Redact / minimize

Reduce unnecessary exposure of sensitive information before broader processing.

Step 2

Extract

Process documents one at a time and pull out structured claims.

Step 3

Reconcile

Resolve dates, source conflicts, and timeline questions with rules.

Step 4

Synthesize

Use generative AI only after the evidence is organized.

Step 5

Check

Run quality controls before calling the report finished.

We do not ask one model to freestyle the whole case.

Speaker Notes

This is the key slide. Walk through a real case: "4 documents in. Step 1: redaXtor strips PII -- no AI, pattern matching. Step 2: each doc read separately -- 300 claims extracted in 6 minutes. Step 3: timeline built with clinical rules (source hierarchy: trust parents on placement sequence, trust facilities on their own dates, compute age from DOB). This step took 2 milliseconds -- pure code. Step 4: ONLY NOW does the powerful AI see the clean, organized data and write the report in 2 minutes. Step 5: 29 of 30 quality checks passed automatically." Punchline: "We don't ask one model to freestyle the whole case."

Slide 11 of 17

Fluent is not the same as true.

Hallucinations are a predictable failure mode. The answer is not panic. The answer is better workflow design.

Ground

Use real source material, not vague prompting.

Narrow

Give the model smaller, clearer tasks.

Check

Verify important claims before trusting the final output.

Speaker Notes

”Hallucination means sounding confident while being wrong. In clinical docs, that's a date shifted by a year, a diagnosis attributed to the wrong evaluator, or details 'borrowed' between documents. In our reference case, the pipeline checked 300 claims for cross-document contamination -- zero flags. 86 claims in the final report, every one traced to a source document. That's what grounding and isolation give you. The answer isn't to avoid AI -- it's to design the workflow so errors get caught.”

Give the right folder at the right time

A

Guidelines

Best practices and philosophical guardrails do not need to sit in one giant prompt.

B

Report structure

The format can be held outside the model and applied when it is needed.

C

Case facts

The model sees the evidence relevant to the step it is performing, not every file all at once.

This is why the file system matters: it lets us apply guidelines, customize format, and reduce confusion.

Speaker Notes

"We built the filing cabinet the AI doesn't have. IECA principles, TCA standards, BBI pillars, level of care criteria -- these live in files loaded when the AI needs them. Report format and guiding questions are held outside the model. Case facts are fed one step at a time. This is why you can customize: swap the guidelines file, change the report format. Even if you're using Claude directly -- same principle. Save your standards as text. Build a system prompt. Give the AI the right context, not all the context."

Speaker Notes

"The #1 question after the TCA keynote was about HIPAA and FERPA. When you paste a psych eval into ChatGPT, that data goes to a server. Our approach: the AI never sees real names. M. Rivera -- Bloom Report

Prepared by: Bloom Protocol (Prepare to Bloom)  |  Date: April 2026  |  All names and identifying details have been changed.

Background

Maya Rivera is a 16-year-old multiracial female with a complex psychiatric history spanning over three years of escalating treatment intensity. Following multiple residential treatment center (RTC) placements, acute psychiatric hospitalizations, and a nine-month period of near-total disengagement from school and therapy while living at home, Maya is currently placed at Cedarbrook Academy, a short-term stabilization program, where she continues to exhibit self-harm behaviors requiring 1:1 supervision (Cedarbrook Clinical Notes, 2026). Her family, with support from an educational consultant, is seeking a long-term RTC placement capable of addressing her intertwined neurodevelopmental, mood, and safety needs.

Clinical Profile

Major depressive disorder (MDD), recurrent episode, severe (F33.2); Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) (F32.81); Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) (F41.1); Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) (F84.0); Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), under clinical observation (pending formal evaluation).

Diagnostic convergence exists across providers regarding MDD, GAD, and ASD; the Ridgeview Treatment discharge summary (2023) and the Individualized Education Program (IEP) team (Cedarbrook Clinical Notes, 2026) both document these diagnoses. PMDD was identified at Ridgeview (age 14) and has been corroborated by four years of cyclical behavioral escalation tracked by Maya's mother (Cedarbrook Clinical Notes, 2026). Current medications include fluoxetine (Prozac) 40 mg, Vraylar, and continuous birth control to manage PMDD (Cedarbrook Clinical Notes, 2026).

Personality and Strengths

Maya is described as intelligent, capable of exceeding state academic standards in both English Language Arts and math, and she enjoys debates and activities requiring reasoning (Annual IEP, 2025). She has demonstrated entrepreneurial initiative by independently selling items online and has expressed a career interest in becoming a coach in the mental health field (Annual IEP, 2025). Maya engages meaningfully with creative outlets; at Ridgeview, she processed emotions through poetry and writing (Ridgeview Discharge Summary, 2023). She is athletic and has used physical activity such as running as a successful coping strategy (Ridgeview Discharge Summary, 2023; Annual IEP, 2025).

Needs and Challenges

Maya's most acute safety concern is self-harm through ligature risk; during her stay at Ridgeview (age 14), she required 12 physical restraint interventions across a 28-day period, including multiple strangulation attempts (Ridgeview Discharge Summary, 2023). At Cedarbrook (age 16), eight unsafe behavioral incidents occurred in approximately three weeks, including wrapping a hair tie around her neck and placing a battery in her mouth (Cedarbrook Clinical Notes, 2026). These behaviors escalate in a cyclical pattern correlated with hormonal fluctuations. Attachment disruption is a significant driver: a majority of incidents occur during staff shift changes (Cedarbrook Clinical Notes, 2026).

Academic Functioning

Maya has historically exceeded state standards in ELA and math. However, she has earned 0 of the 220 credits required for her anticipated 2028 graduation, and she has not attended school since September 2024, a gap of over nine months (Annual IEP, 2025). Her IEP identifies Emotional Disability as the primary eligibility category. At Cedarbrook, Maya has been productively engaged in two hours of daily core academics (Cedarbrook Clinical Notes, 2026).

Family System Impact

Maya's parents are actively engaged in placement decisions and have participated in parent coaching, though Maya's refusal to engage has limited the effectiveness of these supports (Annual IEP, 2025). The family system is strained; behavioral outbursts create significant conflict at home, and the impact on Maya's sibling has been described as "very difficult" (Annual IEP, 2025).

Treatment History and Failed Interventions

Maya's treatment trajectory began approximately five months before her admission to Ridgeview Treatment, when she was placed at Westbrook RTC (Ridgeview Discharge Summary, 2023). At Westbrook (age 14), she engaged in strangulation attempts and was subsequently sent to the emergency department after ingesting dangerous objects. She spent 40 days cycling between inpatient, residential, and partial hospitalization levels before discharging to Creekside Academy RTC (Ridgeview Discharge Summary, 2023). Following her return home (approximately mid-2024), Maya disengaged from all outpatient services (Annual IEP, 2025). The IEP team, treatment team, and the family all concur that long-term residential treatment is indicated.

Recommendations

Environment Features: 24-hour awake staff with crisis de-escalation training and ligature-risk management; capacity for 1:1 supervision; small, structured milieu (8-12 residents) with consistent staffing; on-site accredited academic program with credit recovery; integrated psychiatric and gynecological/endocrine consultation.

BBI Screening: Family involvement (weekly family therapy, parent coaching); Trauma-informed (avoid punitive responses to self-harm, use DBT/CBT with ASD-informed adaptations); Cultural affirmation (multiracial identity reflected in staffing); Transition criteria (zero self-harm incidents over sustained period, consistent engagement in therapy and academics).

Strength-Based Hooks: Creative writing and poetry as therapeutic engagement; physical activity as coping; entrepreneurial interests channeled into goal-setting.

Placement Goals: Student: Reduce self-harm to zero over sustained periods; re-engage in full-time academics; build capacity to tolerate transitions without escalating. Family: Develop boundary-setting strategies; address sibling impact; build sustainable step-down plan.

Executive Summary

Maya Rivera is a 16-year-old with MDD (severe, recurrent), PMDD, GAD, ASD, and suspected OCD. Despite multiple RTC placements and current stabilization at Cedarbrook Academy, she continues to exhibit self-harm behaviors at a rate of approximately eight incidents per three weeks, requiring 1:1 supervision, with cyclical intensification correlated to hormonal fluctuations. She has refused all community-based interventions over the past year, earning zero academic credits. Data indicates the need for a long-term RTC with 24-hour crisis-capable staffing, ligature-risk protocols, integrated psychiatric and endocrine management, on-site credit-recovery academics, and structured family therapy.

Use AI well

Best practices

  • be specific about the task
  • give relevant context, not all context
  • ask for a draft, not a final truth
  • check important claims
  • keep humans in the loop
