RAN-Only Dyslexia: What the Research Says

Based on 35 peer-reviewed papers | April 2026 | 5 HIGH, 12 MODERATE, 8 LOW quality studies

Key Messages

Your student's profile has a name. Children who struggle with reading speed but not accuracy are recognized as having a "naming-speed-only" dyslexia subtype. About 1 in 4 children with reading disabilities fit this profile. It is the mildest of the three dyslexia subtypes, but it is still real dyslexia with real consequences.
Phonics-only instruction will plateau for this student. Orton-Gillingham (OG) style tutoring fixes decoding accuracy, which is its job. But it does not fix the speed problem. Research consistently shows that naming-speed deficits predict poor response to standard phonics intervention.
One intervention was designed specifically for this profile. RAVE-O (Retrieval, Automaticity, Vocabulary Elaboration, Orthography) is the only published program that targets the naming-speed bottleneck directly. A randomized controlled trial with 279 students showed strong improvements in both fluency and comprehension after about 70 hours of instruction.
Text-to-speech should start now, not after intervention fails. Federal guidance (OSEP 2024) and leading organizations say assistive technology should accompany intervention, not follow it. There is no legal requirement to "try and fail" before getting text-to-speech.

What kind of dyslexia is this?

Researchers call it naming-speed-only or RAN-only dyslexia. RAN stands for Rapid Automatized Naming, a timed test where you name letters, numbers, colors, or objects as fast as you can. Children with this profile are slow at this task even though they can sound out words correctly. Their brain wiring for speed is different from their wiring for accuracy. Brain imaging confirms these are separate systems (Saygin et al., 2013).

How is it different from other dyslexia?

The double-deficit hypothesis (Wolf & Bowers, 1999) identifies three subtypes: children who struggle with phonics only (~22%), children who struggle with speed only (~24%), and children who struggle with both (~54%). The "both" group has the hardest time. The speed-only group reads the most accurately but slowly. A meta-analysis of 8,335 students found the speed deficit is large (d = 1.19, meaning most dyslexic readers are substantially slower than their peers).

What does the evidence actually look like?

This review examined 35 academic papers: 5 were high-quality (large randomized trials or meta-analyses), 12 were well-designed studies, and 8 were smaller or limited. The core finding that naming speed and phonological awareness are separate brain functions is supported by strong, converging evidence across multiple countries and languages. The intervention evidence is narrower: RAVE-O has good trial data, but it comes mainly from one research group.

Section 2 of 9

The RAN-Only Profile: What It Is

RAN and phonological awareness are separate brain systems

HIGH CONFIDENCE

Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN) is a timed task where you name familiar things (letters, digits, colors) as fast as you can. It was first described by Denckla & Rudel in 1976 and has been cited over 1,500 times. Brain imaging shows that the white matter tract called the left arcuate fasciculus correlates with phonological awareness but not with naming speed (Saygin et al., 2013). These are genuinely different brain processes.

"Phonological deficits and processes underlying naming-speed deficits represent 2 separable sources of reading dysfunction." — Wolf & Bowers, 1999 (1,904 citations)

Three subtypes, not one

HIGH CONFIDENCE

The double-deficit hypothesis says there are three groups of struggling readers:

SubtypePrevalenceWhat's HardSeverity
Phonological-only~22%Sounding out wordsModerate
Naming-speed-only (RAN-only)~24%Reading speed/fluencyMildest
Double-deficit (both)~54%Both accuracy and speedMost severe

This framework has been validated across English, Dutch, French, German, and Greek (Landerl et al., 2018).

RAN-only children are the "highest achieving and most selectively impaired"

MODERATE

In a study of 166 children, the naming-speed-only group had the best overall reading scores among the three subtypes. Their problem is specific: they can decode words correctly, but slowly. This means they read accurately but not fluently, which makes it harder to understand longer texts because working memory gets overloaded by the slow pace.

"The VNS group children were the highest achieving and most selectively impaired readers." — Lovett et al., 2000

The deficit is in sequencing, not vocabulary access

MODERATE

Eye-tracking research (Jones et al., 2016; Araujo et al., 2020) shows that dyslexic readers activate word meanings normally. The bottleneck is in serial processing: coordinating the sequence of recognizing one word, encoding its sounds, producing it, and moving to the next. Think of it like a factory where each machine works fine, but the conveyor belt between them runs slowly.

Why Orton-Gillingham Hits a Ceiling

RAN deficits predict treatment resistance to standard phonics

MODERATE

Two key studies (Fletcher et al., 2011; Miciak et al., 2014) found that naming speed is a cognitive correlate of inadequate response to standard reading intervention. In plain language: children with slow naming speed are the ones most likely to "not respond" to phonics-based tutoring like OG.

This makes sense once you understand what OG targets. OG teaches the rules for converting letters to sounds (decoding). Your student has already learned those rules after 3 years of intensive tutoring. The remaining problem is speed/automaticity, which is a different brain system that OG was not designed to train.

You cannot drill RAN directly

MODERATE

A common question is whether you can improve naming speed by practicing naming tasks. The consensus from researchers and practitioners (including the International Dyslexia Association and literacy researcher Timothy Shanahan) is clear: RAN is a diagnostic indicator, not a teachable skill. Repeatedly practicing rapid naming does not transfer to reading improvement. The intervention must target the downstream reading processes that naming speed affects.

The 4th-grade shift: "reading to learn"

MODERATE

In 4th grade, classroom expectations shift from learning to read to reading to learn. Students are expected to read longer texts, extract meaning, and build knowledge from what they read. A child who reads accurately but slowly hits a wall here: they can decode every word, but the slow pace makes it hard to hold the meaning of a paragraph together. This is exactly where RAN-only students struggle most.

RAVE-O: The Intervention Designed for This Profile

What RAVE-O is

HIGH CONFIDENCE

RAVE-O stands for Retrieval, Automaticity, Vocabulary Elaboration, Orthography. It was developed by Maryanne Wolf, one of the researchers who identified the naming-speed subtype. Unlike OG, which focuses on one thing (phonics), RAVE-O works on five components of word knowledge simultaneously: how words sound (phonology), how they look (orthography), what they mean (semantics), how they fit in sentences (syntax), and how they're built from parts (morphology).

The idea is that automaticity (speed) comes from having rich, interconnected knowledge about words, not from drilling one pathway.

The RCT evidence

HIGH CONFIDENCE

The strongest evidence comes from Morris et al. (2012), a randomized controlled trial with 279 children using a 2x2x2 design:

OutcomeEffect SizeWhat It Means
Oral reading fluencyd = 0.76Medium-to-large improvement
Reading comprehensiond = 0.90Large improvement
Duration needed~70 hoursAbout 1 school year at 2-3 sessions/week
Follow-up1-year maintainedGains held after instruction stopped

Gains were equivalent across IQ levels, socioeconomic status, and racial groups. Earlier intervention (grade 1 vs. grade 3) produced stronger effects (Lovett et al., 2017).

Important limitations

CANDOR CHECK

Here is where honesty about the evidence matters:

  • One research group. The RAVE-O evidence comes primarily from Wolf, Lovett, and Morris. Independent replication by other teams is limited. This does not mean the results are wrong, but it does mean we should hold them with appropriate caution.
  • No head-to-head comparison. There are no published trials comparing RAVE-O versus OG specifically for RAN-only children. We know RAVE-O works for mixed groups of struggling readers. We have strong theoretical reasons to believe it is especially suited for RAN-only students. But we do not have a trial that proves it directly.
  • 70 hours is a lot. That is roughly a full school year of twice-weekly sessions. Finding qualified providers may be challenging depending on your location.

Text-to-Speech: Start Now, Not Later

TTS produces modest but real comprehension gains

HIGH CONFIDENCE

A meta-analysis by Wood et al. (2018) found that text-to-speech and read-aloud tools produce a weighted average effect of g = 0.35 on reading comprehension. That is a small-to-medium effect. It means TTS helps, but it is not a substitute for intervention. Think of it as removing a barrier so the student can access grade-level content while their fluency catches up.

One limitation: this meta-analysis did not separate results by dyslexia subtype. We do not know if RAN-only students benefit more or less than other subtypes from TTS. That study has not been done.

Federal law says: no "try and fail" requirement

CRITICAL

Multiple authoritative sources agree that assistive technology should be introduced alongside intervention, not after intervention fails:

  • IDEA regulation (34 CFR 300.324(a)(2)(v)): IEP teams must consider assistive technology at every meeting.
  • IDEA regulation (34 CFR 300.105): Schools must provide AT if it is needed for a free appropriate public education (FAPE).
  • OSEP 2024 Dear Colleague Letter: Explicitly debunks 8 myths about AT, including the myth that an evaluation must precede AT, or that a child must fail intervention first.
  • International Dyslexia Association, Colorado CDE, Yale Dyslexia Center all recommend AT alongside intervention.

If anyone at the school says your student needs to "fail first" before getting text-to-speech, that position is contradicted by federal guidance.

Why 4th grade is the right time

MODERATE

The shift from "learning to read" to "reading to learn" happens around 4th grade. Students with fluency deficits face a widening gap: content gets harder, reading volume increases, and slow readers fall behind in science, social studies, and math word problems. TTS bridges this gap by letting the student access grade-level content through listening while continuing to build reading fluency through intervention.

Strong verbal comprehension (which your student has) is a significant protective factor here. It means the student can learn effectively through listening. TTS leverages an existing strength.

Timeline and Prognosis

RAN-only has the best prognosis among the three subtypes

MODERATE

Among the three groups (phonological-only, naming-speed-only, and double-deficit), RAN-only students consistently show the best outcomes. They start from a higher baseline, and their intact phonological skills and verbal comprehension serve as compensatory strengths.

But fluency deficits persist into adulthood

MODERATE

Longitudinal research (Lyytinen et al., 2006) shows that naming-speed deficits are persistent. Reading fluency remains the primary area of residual difficulty even for adults who were identified early and received intervention. The realistic goal is not "grade-level fluency" but rather functional reading efficiency supported by compensatory strategies and tools.

This is important context for choosing an educational environment. A school that relies heavily on timed reading or penalizes slow reading will be a poor fit. A school that allows flexible pacing, provides audio alternatives, and values comprehension over speed will let this student thrive.

What the evidence does NOT tell us

CANDOR CHECK

The research has significant gaps when it comes to practical questions parents face:

  • No evidence-based criteria for when to transition from phonics-focused to fluency-focused intervention. The "OG plateau" is clinically recognized but not formally defined.
  • No RCTs comparing intervention protocols specifically for the RAN-only subtype versus other subtypes.
  • Limited longitudinal data tracking RAN-only students separately from other subtypes into adulthood.
  • No published research on the specific clinical question: after OG succeeds at decoding but fluency remains impaired, what is the optimal next step?
  • No evidence-based timeline for when to shift the primary goal from remediation to compensation.

These are not obscure questions. They are exactly the questions parents and clinicians need answered. The field has not caught up.

What This Means for Your Student

CRITICAL Request assistive technology at the next IEP meeting

Text-to-speech for content-area reading (science, social studies, any subject where the student is reading to learn). Federal law requires the IEP team to consider AT at every meeting (34 CFR 300.324(a)(2)(v)). There is no "try and fail" prerequisite. The 2024 OSEP Dear Colleague Letter makes this explicit. If the team resists, cite the DCL by name.

Evidence: Meta-analysis shows g = 0.35 comprehension gain (Wood et al., 2018). Your student's strong verbal comprehension means they are well-positioned to benefit.

CRITICAL Investigate RAVE-O or fluency-focused intervention

After 3 years of OG, your student has the decoding skills OG was designed to build. The remaining deficit (fluency) requires a different approach. RAVE-O is the only published intervention specifically designed for naming-speed deficits. It requires about 70 hours (one school year at 2-3 sessions per week).

Evidence: RCT with 279 students, d = 0.76 fluency, d = 0.90 comprehension (Morris et al., 2012). Caveat: Evidence comes primarily from one research group.

HIGH Evaluate schools based on flexibility, not speed expectations

Because fluency deficits are likely to persist in some form, the educational environment matters as much as the intervention. Look for:

  • Flexible pacing on reading assignments and tests
  • Audio alternatives for content-area texts
  • Assessment of comprehension separate from reading speed
  • Willingness to implement AT as a routine accommodation

Evidence: Longitudinal data shows fluency remains the primary area of residual difficulty into adulthood (Lyytinen et al., 2006).

HIGH Leverage the student's verbal comprehension strength

Strong verbal comprehension is this student's biggest asset. It is a documented protective factor for RAN-only students. Use it: audiobooks for pleasure reading, verbal participation in class discussions, oral exams where permitted. Every channel that lets the student demonstrate understanding without being bottlenecked by reading speed is a win.

MODERATE Do not drill RAN tasks directly

If any provider suggests rapid-naming drills (flash cards with speed pressure, timed letter-naming practice), that approach is not supported by evidence. RAN is a diagnostic indicator, not a trainable skill. The intervention should target reading fluency through multi-component word knowledge, not through speed drills on the test itself.

Open Questions Worth Asking Providers

  • Are you familiar with the double-deficit hypothesis and the RAN-only subtype?
  • What is your plan for addressing fluency now that decoding is solid?
  • Have you used RAVE-O or a similar multi-component fluency intervention?
  • How do you decide when to shift goals from remediation to compensation?

What Researchers Agree On (and Where They Don't)

Strong Consensus

These points are supported by meta-analyses and multiple independent research groups:

  • RAN and phonological awareness are separable constructs with distinct neural substrates. This is confirmed across 5+ languages.
  • Double-deficit children (both problems) show the most severe impairments. RAN-only is the mildest subtype.
  • RAN is a universal predictor of reading fluency regardless of how transparent the writing system is.
  • Directly practicing RAN tasks does not improve reading. Multiple authorities converge on this.
  • Assistive technology should accompany, not follow, intervention. Supported by federal guidance and professional organizations.
  • The 2025 IDA definition update now explicitly includes naming speed, validating all three subtypes as "real" dyslexia.

Active Debate

  • Is RAN truly independent of phonological processing? Vukovic & Siegel (2006) note that RAN and phonological awareness are moderately correlated, creating statistical challenges for demonstrating full independence. Most researchers accept they are separable but not fully independent.
  • Does RAVE-O generalize beyond the Wolf/Lovett/Morris group? Independent replication is limited. This is the biggest caveat on the intervention evidence.
  • What is the optimal intervention dosage for RAN-only children in upper elementary grades? The 70-hour figure comes from studies with younger children.

The 2025 IDA Definition Matters

The International Dyslexia Association updated its definition of dyslexia in 2025 to explicitly include naming speed as a core feature. This is significant because it validates the RAN-only subtype as "real" dyslexia in the eyes of the leading professional organization. Any school or provider who says a child with intact phonics but impaired naming speed "doesn't really have dyslexia" is now contradicted by the IDA's own definition.
